# Pet Health & Behavior Documentation

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/pet-services|Pet Services]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** Cross-segment (applies to all pet service providers tracking pet health and behavior)
**Share of Parent Industry:** Cross-cutting operational function
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Most providers track nothing beyond what they can remember; those who document use unstructured text notes.
**Target Buyer:** Pet sitters, boarders, daycare operators, and walkers who want to track pet health and behavior trends over time
**Automation Potential:** High — Structured observation logging, trend detection, and health concern flagging are systematizable.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Pet service providers observe pets more frequently than veterinarians — a daily walker sees a dog 5 times per week, while the vet sees them once a year. This means providers often notice health and behavior changes (limping, decreased appetite, increased lethargy, behavioral regression) before the owner or vet does. But these observations aren't documented, shared with the owner in a structured way, or connected to the pet's veterinary care. The provider mentions it verbally ("Bella seemed a little off today") and the observation is lost. A structured documentation system could transform pet service providers into an early warning system for pet health.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Gingr and PetExec have report card features for daycare facilities, but they're designed for daily parent updates, not longitudinal health tracking. No tool captures structured behavioral and health observations over time, detects trends (gradual weight change, decreasing energy, increasing anxiety), or generates reports that a veterinarian could use. The data that could make pet service providers valuable health monitors is being lost to memory and unstructured text.
