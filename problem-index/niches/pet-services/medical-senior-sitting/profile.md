# Specialized Pet Sitting (Medical/Senior)

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/pet-services|Pet Services]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $680M
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~6%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Sitters specializing in medical or senior pets use text messages and paper notes for care instructions; no platform models medical pet care workflows.
**Target Buyer:** Pet sitters specializing in diabetic, post-surgical, epileptic, or geriatric pet care
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Medication schedules, vital monitoring, and veterinary communication are systematizable; hands-on medical care requires training.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Medical and senior pet sitting requires skills beyond standard pet care: administering insulin injections, monitoring seizure patterns, managing post-surgical wound care, recognizing signs of pain or decline in elderly animals, and communicating effectively with the pet's veterinarian. These sitters charge premium rates ($50-100+/night vs. $25-40 for standard sitting) because the care responsibility is closer to nursing than pet sitting. The client base — pet parents with chronically ill or aging pets — is intensely anxious about leaving their pet and requires detailed reporting that standard sitters don't provide.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Rover and standard pet sitting platforms don't differentiate medical pet sitters or provide tools for medical care documentation. Sitters use text messages to communicate medication timing, paper notes for care instructions, and phone calls to relay concerns. There's no structured way to hand off a medical pet's care protocol (medications with timing, dosages, special food, warning signs) or to document care delivered in a format the pet's veterinarian could review.
