# Community Development Finance Navigator

**Niche:** [[niches/commercial-real-estate/minority-owned-cre-firms/profile|Minority-Owned CRE Firms]]
**Industry:** [[industries/commercial-real-estate|Commercial Real Estate]]
**Type:** Buy & Customize (Vertical Adaptation)
**One-liner:** General CRE finance tools model conventional debt and equity structures, but don't incorporate the layered community development financing — NMTC allocations, Opportunity Zone deferrals, CDFI lending, HUD Section 108, and state-level incentive programs — that minority developers in underserved markets depend on to make projects pencil.
**Tags:** #automation #compliance #data-integration #workflow-orchestration

## The Problem
A minority developer building in an Opportunity Zone may layer 5-6 funding sources: conventional bank debt, CDFI subordinate debt, NMTC allocation (7-year tax credit), Opportunity Zone equity (capital gains deferral), state historic tax credits, and city TIF (Tax Increment Financing). Each source has different application processes, compliance requirements, and reporting obligations. Modeling the complete capital stack — with each source's terms, timing, and covenants — requires expertise that large developers have in-house but small minority developers lack. Missing one incentive program that a project qualifies for can mean the difference between a viable and unviable pro forma.

## What Already Exists
Argus Enterprise and conventional CRE financial modeling tools handle standard debt/equity structures. NMTC and Opportunity Zone calculators exist as standalone spreadsheets from tax advisors. State incentive program databases (Good Jobs First, CDFA) list available programs. None are integrated into a single capital stack modeling tool.

## The Customization Gap
A community development finance tool needs to: (1) screen a project address against all eligible federal, state, and local incentive programs (NMTC, OZ, HTC, TIF, enterprise zone, CDFI fund); (2) model the layered capital stack with each source's terms and timing; (3) generate pro forma projections showing project feasibility with and without each incentive layer; (4) track application deadlines and compliance requirements for each source post-closing. The regulatory complexity across 50 states and hundreds of local incentive programs is the barrier.

## Target Customer
Minority CRE developers pursuing projects in distressed or underserved census tracts — approximately 3,000-5,000 active developers nationally, plus the 200+ CDFIs and community development consultants advising them.

## Impact If Solved
Developers identify 1-2 additional incentive programs per project that they would have missed, adding $200,000-$1M in capital per project. Application success rates improve because pro formas accurately model the layered structure that program administrators expect to see.
