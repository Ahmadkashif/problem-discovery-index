# Small Manufacturer Overflow Storage

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/warehouse-3pl|Warehouse & 3PL]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $10-15B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~6%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — small manufacturers using 3PL overflow storage often have no WMS, track inventory in spreadsheets, and coordinate with the 3PL via email and phone rather than system integration
**Target Buyer:** Manufacturer logistics manager or plant operations director at companies with $5-50M revenue
**Automation Potential:** Medium — inventory visibility and order coordination are automatable, but the irregular and unpredictable nature of overflow needs limits standardization

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Small manufacturers ($5-50M revenue) often outgrow their on-site storage but can't justify building or leasing a dedicated warehouse. They use 3PL overflow storage: renting space in a shared warehouse to store excess raw materials, work-in-progress, or finished goods. The relationship is typically informal — the manufacturer sends a few pallets when they're out of space and retrieves them when needed. Unlike e-commerce fulfillment (which is high-velocity, standardized), manufacturer overflow is irregular, unpredictable, and often involves materials that require special handling (chemicals, heavy components, temperature-sensitive goods). The manufacturer often has no WMS — they track what's in the 3PL's warehouse via email confirmations and spreadsheets.

## Current Tools & Gaps
3PL Central and Deposco serve 3PLs managing overflow, but require the manufacturer to have an integrated system for inventory updates. Small manufacturers use QuickBooks or Excel for inventory. No platform bridges the gap between a manufacturer's informal inventory tracking and a 3PL's WMS for the overflow relationship.
