# Critical Medical Information Accessibility at the Field

**Niche:** [[niches/youth-sports-orgs/special-needs-adaptive-leagues/profile|Special Needs Adaptive Leagues]]
**Industry:** [[industries/youth-sports-orgs|Youth Sports Organizations]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Seizure action plans, allergy protocols, and behavioral intervention guides for adaptive sports participants are stored in a paper binder at the coordinator's table — inaccessible to the buddy on the far side of the field when a medical incident happens.
**Tags:** #automation #compliance #worker-facing #data-integration

## The Problem
Adaptive sports participants often have complex medical needs: seizure disorders (15-20% of participants in many programs), severe allergies, diabetes requiring blood sugar monitoring, behavioral protocols for de-escalation, and medication schedules. This information is documented in action plans provided by families — but it's stored in a physical binder at the registration table. When a child has a seizure at the far end of the field, the buddy must shout for the coordinator, the coordinator runs to the binder, finds the child's page, and reads the seizure action plan. By this time, 2-3 minutes have passed and the initial response window is closing. The binder system fails exactly when it matters most: during an emergency on a large field.

## Why It's Still Broken
Medical action plans are legally sensitive documents that programs are cautious about digitizing. Paper feels "HIPAA-safe" (even though HIPAA doesn't directly apply to most youth sports programs). Digitizing requires a secure system that limits access to authorized volunteers while making critical information instantly available during emergencies. Mainstream sports platforms don't include medical action plan storage, and medical record platforms aren't designed for volunteer access at athletic events. The result is a paper-based system that provides a false sense of compliance while failing to deliver information when needed.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A secure mobile medical information system that: (1) stores each participant's action plans (seizure, allergy, behavioral) in a secure digital format with photo, (2) provides role-based access — buddies see only their assigned participant's critical information, coordinators see all participants, (3) enables one-tap emergency access on a volunteer's phone showing the child's photo, action plan steps, emergency contacts, and medications, (4) includes a "medical event" button that timestamps the incident, alerts the coordinator and parent simultaneously, and logs what happened for documentation, and (5) auto-expires access at end of season and re-authorizes each season with updated information.

## Who Feels the Pain
Buddy volunteers who are assigned a participant with a seizure disorder and carry the anxiety of not having the action plan accessible, and coordinators who know that their paper binder system will fail during a real emergency.

## Impact If Fixed
Reduces medical emergency response time from 3-5 minutes (binder retrieval) to under 30 seconds (phone access), potentially preventing serious harm in the 5-10 medical incidents per season that a typical adaptive program experiences — while providing the documentation trail that protects the program from liability.
