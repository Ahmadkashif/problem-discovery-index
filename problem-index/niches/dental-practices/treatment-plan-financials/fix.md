# Standardized Financial Presentation Workflow

**Niche:** [[niches/dental-practices/treatment-plan-financials/profile|Treatment Plan Financial Presentation]]
**Industry:** [[industries/dental-practices|Dental Practices]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Treatment plans presented without clear financial information have ~50% acceptance — when patients say "I need to think about it" they rarely come back — and the quality of financial communication depends entirely on which staff member happens to be presenting, not on a system.
**Tags:** #automation #workflow-orchestration #worker-facing #revenue-impact #quick-win #large-language-models #transfer-learning

## The Problem
In most dental practices, the financial presentation is not a system — it's a person. The best treatment coordinators have scripts, visual aids, and practiced responses to objections ("I can't afford it" → "Let me show you some payment options that make this manageable"). They know to present the total treatment plan as phases ("We can start with the two most urgent crowns this month and schedule the remaining work over the next 6 months"), to anchor on monthly payment amounts rather than total cost, and to address insurance coverage proactively before the patient asks. The worst presenters hand the patient a printout with a number and say "Do you want to schedule?" The gap between the best and worst treatment coordinators in the same practice can be a 30-percentage-point difference in acceptance rates. When the best coordinator is sick, on vacation, or leaves for another job, acceptance rates drop immediately. This is a training and standardization problem — the institutional knowledge of how to present financial information effectively lives in one person's head, not in a workflow.

## Why It's Still Broken
Dental practices are clinical organizations — they invest heavily in clinical training (continuing education, hands-on courses, certifications) but almost nothing in financial presentation training. Treatment coordinator training, where it exists, is delivered by practice management consultants ($2,000-5,000 for a 2-day workshop) and produces a temporary lift that fades within months as the coordinator reverts to old habits or leaves. No dental software system guides the financial presentation interaction — the PMS generates a treatment plan document, and everything after that is improvised by the human presenter. The dental industry treats financial communication as a soft skill that some people have and others don't, rather than as a structured workflow that can be templated, measured, and improved.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A guided financial presentation workflow built into the treatment plan delivery process. When a treatment plan is ready for financial presentation, the system generates: (1) a presentation script customized to this patient's treatment plan, insurance coverage, and financial options — using an LLM to adapt the template to the specific clinical scenario ("I know two crowns sounds like a lot, but let me show you how your insurance helps and what your monthly options look like"), (2) a visual comparison of payment options on a tablet the patient can hold and review (not a paper printout handed across a counter), (3) objection response prompts — when the treatment coordinator marks an objection ("patient said they can't afford it"), the system surfaces the appropriate response ("Show the phased treatment option — start with the most urgent procedure this month"), (4) follow-up automation for patients who don't accept on the spot — a sequenced outreach campaign (email with the financial package PDF → text in 3 days → phone call in 7 days) with tracking, and (5) acceptance analytics per coordinator, procedure type, and financial presentation method — enabling the practice to identify what works and replicate it. The system turns financial presentation from a personal art into a trainable, measurable, improvable process.

## Who Feels the Pain
Treatment coordinators who lack training or confidence in financial conversations and default to "here's the printout," dentists who diagnose necessary treatment only to see half of it declined due to poor financial communication, and patients who leave without understanding their options and never return.

## Impact If Fixed
Standardizing financial presentation across staff members narrows the acceptance rate gap between the best and worst coordinators from 30 points to under 10 points. For a practice with 3 treatment coordinators and average 50% acceptance, lifting the bottom two coordinators by 15 points adds $100K-200K in annual accepted treatment. The follow-up automation alone recovers 10-15% of "I'll think about it" patients who would otherwise be permanently lost.
