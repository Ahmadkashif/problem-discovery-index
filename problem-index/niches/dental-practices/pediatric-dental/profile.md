## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Pediatric dental practices treat patients from infancy through adolescence (typically 0-18), creating clinical and operational workflows that differ fundamentally from general dentistry. Clinically: treatment decisions depend on developmental stage (primary vs. mixed vs. permanent dentition), eruption timing, growth patterns, and the timing sensitivity of interceptive interventions (space maintainers must be placed within a narrow window after premature primary tooth loss; early orthodontic evaluation should happen at age 7). Behavior management is a core clinical skill — techniques like tell-show-do, positive reinforcement, nitrous oxide sedation, and general anesthesia are part of every pediatric dentist's daily workflow and require specific documentation and monitoring. Operationally: the patient (child) and the decision-maker (parent) are different people, creating communication complexity. Insurance is often CHIP, Medicaid pediatric dental (EPSDT), or commercial pediatric riders — each with different covered services for children versus adults. Consent requires a legal guardian, which becomes complicated with divorced parents and custody arrangements.

## Current Tools & Gaps
General dental PMS (Dentrix, Open Dental, Eaglesoft) handles pediatric patients technically — a child is just another patient record. But these systems don't track developmental milestones (eruption timing, mixed dentition stage, growth patterns), don't have behavior management documentation templates (which techniques were used at prior visits and what worked), don't include pediatric-specific sedation monitoring logs (vital signs at 5-minute intervals during nitrous or conscious sedation), and don't adapt billing workflows for CHIP/EPSDT coverage rules. Communication tools (Weave, RevenueWell) send appointment reminders but don't differentiate between communicating with a 14-year-old patient and their divorced parents. The pediatric dental EHR market is essentially unserved by purpose-built software.
