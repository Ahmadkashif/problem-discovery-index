# Per-Load Profitability Tracking

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/owner-operator-trucking|Owner-Operator Trucking]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $4-6B (embedded analytics)
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~2% (services), affects 100% of operators
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Fewer than 10% of owner-operators know their true cost-per-mile; most track revenue but not per-load profit
**Target Buyer:** Any OO who knows their gross revenue but not their net profit per load
**Automation Potential:** High — Per-load P&L requires connecting revenue (rate confirmation) with costs (fuel, tolls, ELD/maintenance, insurance allocation) — all data that exists digitally but is never combined

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
The single most impactful financial metric for an owner-operator is net profit per load (or per hour). Yet fewer than 10% of operators can tell you this number for their last load. They know the rate ($3,200 for Chicago-to-Dallas), but not the true cost: $480 fuel, $65 tolls, $180 deadhead fuel to pickup, $45 in scale tickets and permits, $38 ELD subscription allocation, $95 insurance allocation, $120 maintenance reserve, $85 tire reserve. The actual net was $2,092, and at 22 hours door-to-door, that's $95/hour — which sounds good until they realize the load they rejected (Dallas-to-Houston, $1,100, 5 hours, $40 fuel) would have netted $85/hour. Without per-load P&L, operators can't evaluate load quality, can't identify their most and least profitable lanes, and can't make data-driven decisions about rates, routes, or equipment.

## Current Tools & Gaps
TruckingOffice and Rigbooks offer basic accounting but require manual cost entry per load. ATBS provides annual P&L but not per-load analysis. ELD providers show miles but not costs. Fuel cards show purchases but not per-load allocation. No tool automatically calculates per-load profit by connecting rate confirmation revenue to load-specific costs from ELD, fuel card, and toll data.
