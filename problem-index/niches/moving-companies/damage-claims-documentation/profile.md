# Damage Claims Documentation & Resolution

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/moving-companies|Moving Companies]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $800M-1.2B (claims volume across the industry)
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~5% of revenue, but 15-25% of margin erosion
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Most movers document pre-move condition with phone photos dumped into a folder, process claims via email and paper forms, and track resolution status in spreadsheets or not at all.
**Target Buyer:** Claims manager or owner at a moving company handling 5-50 damage claims per month
**Automation Potential:** High — Pre-move documentation, claim intake, liability calculation, and resolution tracking follow rigid procedural steps ideal for automation

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Damage claims are a universal pain point across all moving segments, but the claims workflow itself is a distinct operational domain with its own logic, regulations, and economics. Every third long-distance move and every fifth local move generates at least one damage claim. The process involves pre-move condition documentation (photos, inventory notes), post-move damage identification, liability determination (carrier liability at $0.60/lb vs. full-value protection), repair-or-replace assessment, settlement calculation, and payment processing. Each step is currently manual, creating a 30-60 day resolution cycle that generates negative reviews, chargeback disputes, and regulatory complaints. For many movers, claims handling is the single largest source of customer dissatisfaction and the primary driver of negative online reviews.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Moving CRMs (SmartMoving, MoveitPro) have basic claim-logging fields but no structured claims workflow — no photo-to-claim matching, no liability calculator, no repair vendor coordination, no resolution tracking with customer communication automation. Generic claims management software (ClaimXperience, Snapsheet) is built for insurance adjusters, not moving companies, and doesn't understand moving-specific liability structures (released value vs. full value, interstate vs. intrastate regulations, carrier vs. agent liability splits).
