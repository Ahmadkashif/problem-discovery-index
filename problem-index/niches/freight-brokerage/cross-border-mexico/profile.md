# Cross-Border Mexico Freight Brokerages

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/freight-brokerage|Freight Brokerage]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $5-7B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~8%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — customs documentation is often faxed, Mexican carrier networks are managed through personal relationships rather than load boards, and cross-border drayage coordination is phone-based
**Target Buyer:** Cross-border operations manager or bilingual broker at US-Mexico freight brokerages
**Automation Potential:** Medium — customs pre-clearance documentation and carrier coordination have clear automation paths, but the cross-border regulatory environment adds complexity

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
US-Mexico cross-border freight requires coordination across three legs: the US domestic haul to the border, the cross-border drayage through the port of entry (POE), and the Mexican domestic haul to final destination. Each leg may use a different carrier, and the border crossing requires customs documentation (CBP on the US side, SAT/Aduana on the Mexico side), C-TPAT compliance for trusted shippers, and coordination with Mexican customs brokers (agentes aduanales). The bilingual requirement, dual-country regulatory knowledge, and three-leg coordination create a workflow that generic FTL brokerage tools cannot address.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Some brokerages use McLeod or Mercury Gate for the US domestic leg but manage the border crossing and Mexico leg through separate systems or manual processes. No TMS natively handles the three-leg cross-border workflow with integrated customs documentation, POE appointment scheduling, and Mexican carrier dispatch. The Mexican carrier ecosystem is less digitized than the US — fewer carriers on load boards, more phone-based negotiation.
