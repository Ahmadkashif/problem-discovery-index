# Native Land & Tribal Property Appraisers

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/real-estate-appraisers|Real Estate Appraisers]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $100-200M
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~1-2%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — no MLS coverage on most tribal land, county assessor data is inapplicable (trust land is not taxed), and standard appraisal methodology doesn't fit properties that can't be sold on the open market
**Target Buyer:** Appraiser certified for tribal trust land assignments by the Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA), or tribal housing authority needing appraisals for HUD Section 184 loans
**Automation Potential:** Low — the unique legal framework (trust land, restricted fee, allotted land) and absence of market data resist standard automation

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Tribal trust land appraisal is fundamentally different from all other residential appraisal: the land is held in trust by the federal government for the tribe or individual allottee and cannot be sold on the open market (only leased or transferred with BIA approval). There are no comparable sales in the traditional sense — you can't derive market value from sales of similar properties because the properties can't be sold. Appraisers must use replacement cost, income capitalization from ground lease rents, or comparable sales from nearby fee-simple land with adjustments for the trust status limitation. BIA requires specific qualifications and report formats. Only 200-400 appraisers nationally are qualified for tribal trust land work, and the demand exceeds supply — HUD Section 184 loans (the primary homeownership program for tribal members) are delayed 3-6 months waiting for appraisals.

## Current Tools & Gaps
TOTAL provides standard residential forms not designed for trust land valuation methodology. BIA provides guidance documents but no software. No appraisal tool addresses the unique requirements: trust land vs. fee-simple adjustments, ground lease income capitalization for tribal leases, replacement cost methodology as the primary approach (instead of sales comparison), or BIA-specific report formats.
