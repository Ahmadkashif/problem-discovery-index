# Booth-Rental Salon Operators

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/hair-salons-independent|Independent Hair Salons]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $12B
**Share of Parent Industry:** 21%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — Salon owners use basic scheduling; booth renters operate independently with personal tools.
**Target Buyer:** Salon owner managing 4-12 booth renters
**Automation Potential:** High — Rent collection, lease management, common-area cost allocation, and occupancy tracking are highly automatable.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
In the booth-rental model, the salon owner rents stations to independent stylists who operate their own businesses — setting their own prices, booking their own clients, and buying their own products. The salon owner is essentially a landlord providing space, utilities, and common amenities. This creates a management challenge unlike any other salon model: the owner has minimal visibility into revenue (renters don't share their numbers), limited control over service quality, and complex financial relationships involving base rent, utility splits, common-area charges, and sometimes supply-room access fees. Most salon management software is built for the commission model where the salon controls everything — it doesn't work for the landlord-tenant dynamic of booth rental.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Most booth-rental salon owners use Venmo/Zelle for rent collection, a spreadsheet for lease tracking, and rely on renters to manage their own booking and clients. Salon-specific platforms (Vagaro, Booker) are designed for centrally managed salons where the owner sees all appointments and revenue. No platform is built for the booth-rental owner's needs: lease management, automated rent collection with late-fee logic, common-area cost allocation, occupancy tracking, and renter communication.
