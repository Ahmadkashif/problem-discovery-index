# Client Mobility & Health Accommodation Tracking

**Niche:** [[niches/hair-salons-independent/aging-clientele/profile|Salons Serving Aging Clientele]]
**Industry:** [[industries/hair-salons-independent|Independent Hair Salons]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Salons accommodate aging clients' physical limitations informally and inconsistently — a regular stylist knows Mrs. Johnson needs the accessible chair, but a substitute doesn't, creating uncomfortable and potentially unsafe situations.
**Tags:** #automation #worker-facing #data-integration #quick-win

## The Problem
An aging client may need: an accessible shampoo station (can't lean back fully), extra service time (slower transitions between stations), a specific chair height, assistance standing, gentler water temperature, avoidance of certain chemicals (scalp sensitivity from medication), or even a companion present during service. The client's regular stylist knows all of this. But when the regular stylist is sick, on vacation, or has left the salon, none of this information is documented. The substitute stylist learns through trial and error — potentially seating the client at a standard station they can't use, applying a chemical that causes a reaction, or scheduling insufficient time.

## Why It's Still Broken
Salon client profiles capture: name, phone, email, service history, and maybe product preferences. There are no fields for physical accommodations, health-related service modifications, or safety notes. Adding these requires: (1) a sensitive intake conversation ("do you have any mobility limitations?"), (2) structured data entry that stylists will actually use, and (3) surfacing this information to every staff member before each appointment. Salons avoid this because it feels medical, and they worry about liability from documenting health information.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A simple accommodation flag system in the client profile: icons or tags for common accommodations (accessible station needed, extra time, companion present, scalp sensitivity, chemical restrictions, mobility assistance). Flags appear on the daily schedule view so every stylist — including substitutes — sees them before the client arrives. Updated by the stylist after each visit with a one-tap interface. Not medical records — functional accommodation notes for service delivery. Paired with a brief consent process explaining what's tracked and why.

## Who Feels the Pain
Substitute stylists who unknowingly create uncomfortable or unsafe situations for aging clients, salon owners who risk liability from unaccommodated physical limitations, and aging clients who feel invisible when their regular stylist isn't available.

## Impact If Fixed
Prevents accommodation failures that drive away long-term clients, reduces fall/injury risk for clients with mobility limitations, and ensures consistent service quality regardless of which stylist is assigned.
