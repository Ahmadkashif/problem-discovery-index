# Stylist Commission & Booth-Rent Payroll

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/hair-salons-independent|Independent Hair Salons]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $3.2B (payroll processing)
**Share of Parent Industry:** 6% (of total operating costs)
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Most independent salons calculate commissions manually from POS reports using spreadsheets.
**Target Buyer:** Salon owner/bookkeeper
**Automation Potential:** High — Commission calculations follow deterministic rules but are complex enough that manual calculation takes hours and produces errors.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Salon payroll is uniquely complex because compensation models mix multiple structures within a single business: commission-based pay (40-60% of service revenue), tiered commissions (higher-performing stylists earn higher percentages), guaranteed minimums (hourly rate or minimum weekly pay), booth-rent deductions, product sales commissions (different rate than service commissions), tip distribution, backbar product charges (some salons charge stylists for product usage), and benefits deductions. A single salon might have 3-4 different compensation structures across its staff. Standard payroll services (Gusto, ADP) handle hourly and salary pay but don't natively calculate salon-specific commission structures.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Gusto, ADP Run, and QuickBooks Payroll handle tax withholding, direct deposit, and compliance. Some salon POS platforms (Vagaro, Booker) calculate basic commissions. None handle: tiered commission structures that change based on monthly performance targets, guaranteed minimum reconciliation (pay whichever is higher — commission or guaranteed minimum), mixed employee/contractor payroll (W-2 commission stylists and 1099 booth renters in the same system), or backbar product charge deductions.
