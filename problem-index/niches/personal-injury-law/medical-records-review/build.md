# AI Medical Record Chronology & Extraction Engine

**Niche:** [[niches/personal-injury-law/medical-records-review/profile|Medical Records Review]]
**Industry:** [[industries/personal-injury-law|Personal Injury Law]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** PI paralegals stop spending 4-40 hours manually reading, organizing, and summarizing medical records — an AI engine ingests raw PDFs, extracts medical entities, builds chronological timelines, flags gaps and pre-existing conditions, and generates attorney-ready summaries.
**Tags:** #bert #transformers #large-language-models #transfer-learning #attention-mechanisms #feature-engineering #automation #revenue-impact #tacit-knowledge-ml

## The Problem
Medical record review is the largest labor cost in personal injury law. A paralegal receives 200-2,000+ pages of records from multiple providers — hospital ERs, orthopedists, physical therapists, pain management clinics, primary care physicians, imaging centers. The records arrive as unsorted PDFs, sometimes faxed images, with no consistent format. The paralegal must: (1) sort records by provider, (2) order them chronologically, (3) read every page to identify diagnoses, procedures, medications, referrals, and provider opinions, (4) flag pre-existing conditions mentioned in the medical history, (5) identify treatment gaps (periods where the client wasn't treating — the defense will argue they were healthy during those gaps), (6) note inconsistencies (the client told the ER they had no prior back problems, but an orthopedic note from 2019 mentions chronic lumbar pain), and (7) write a narrative summary for the attorney. An experienced medical records paralegal can "feel" when a record set tells a strong story versus a weak one — they've reviewed thousands of records and internalized the patterns. A junior paralegal takes 3x longer and misses critical details.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
The record format problem is extreme: every hospital, clinic, and provider uses a different EHR system (Epic, Cerner, Athena, paper charts) with different output formats. OCR quality varies widely — faxed records, handwritten physician notes, and poor-quality scans degrade extraction accuracy. Medical terminology is dense and abbreviation-heavy ("Pt c/o LBP radiating to b/l LE x 3/12, hx DJD L4-5" requires clinical knowledge to parse). Pre-existing condition detection requires understanding medical causation — a 2019 mention of "occasional knee discomfort" may or may not be relevant to a 2024 knee injury claim, and that judgment requires clinical and legal context. The labeling challenge is significant: training data requires paralegals to annotate records with the entities and relationships they extract, but different paralegals extract different things from the same records.

## What to Build
A full-pipeline medical record processing engine for PI. Input: raw PDF medical records (scanned and electronic). Pipeline: (1) Document segmentation — identify provider, record type, and date range for each section of a combined PDF. (2) OCR with medical-context enhancement — extract text with specialized handling of medical abbreviations, handwriting, and poor scans. (3) Entity extraction — identify dates, providers, diagnoses (ICD-10 mapping), procedures (CPT mapping), medications, referrals, and functional assessments using a fine-tuned BERT/transformer model. (4) Chronological timeline — organize all extracted events by date across all providers, showing the treatment narrative. (5) Gap detection — identify periods with no documented treatment and calculate gap duration. (6) Pre-existing condition flagging — surface mentions of prior conditions related to the claimed injury. (7) Deduplication — identify overlapping records from different providers describing the same encounter. (8) Summary generation — LLM produces an attorney-ready narrative summary highlighting key findings. Output: interactive timeline, flagged items for attorney review, and exportable summary document.

## Target Customer
Medical records paralegals and case managers at PI firms with 100+ active cases who spend 30-60% of their working hours on record review and summarization.

## Impact If Built
Reduces medical record review from 4-40 hours per case to 1-4 hours of review and correction. For a 500-case firm with an average 8-hour review per case, that saves 2,000-3,000 paralegal hours annually ($60K-$150K in labor). More critically, catches pre-existing conditions and treatment gaps that junior paralegals miss — issues that destroy case value when the defense discovers them first.
