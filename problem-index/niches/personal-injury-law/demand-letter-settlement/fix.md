# Settlement Valuation Inconsistency

**Niche:** [[niches/personal-injury-law/demand-letter-settlement/profile|Demand Letter & Settlement Valuation]]
**Industry:** [[industries/personal-injury-law|Personal Injury Law]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** PI firms stop leaving money on the table when different attorneys at the same firm value the same case differently by $30K-$80K — a data-driven valuation model provides a consistent baseline that captures the firm's collective experience.
**Tags:** #gradient-boosting #decision-trees #feature-engineering #evaluation-metrics #cross-validation #tacit-knowledge-ml #revenue-impact

## The Problem
Case valuation in PI law is fundamentally subjective. Two attorneys at the same firm can look at the same case — cervical strain with 6 months of chiropractic treatment, soft tissue only, no surgery, good liability, State Farm insurer — and produce demand amounts $30K-$80K apart. One attorney "knows" from experience that this adjuster at this carrier in this jurisdiction settles these cases at 2.5x specials. The other attorney, with less experience in this specific carrier-injury-jurisdiction combination, might demand 4x specials and get a counteroffer that starts the negotiation lower. This inconsistency costs firms hundreds of thousands in aggregate: overvalued demands get rejected and delay resolution (costing the firm carrying costs on cases that take longer to settle), while undervalued demands leave 20-40% of potential recovery on the table. No one at the firm knows their demand-to-settlement ratio by attorney, so the inconsistency is invisible.

## Why It's Still Broken
Settlement valuation is the deepest tacit knowledge problem in PI law. Experienced attorneys have internalized the interaction of a dozen variables: injury type and severity, treatment duration and type, surgical intervention, plaintiff age and demographics, jurisdiction and venue, carrier and adjuster, liability strength, policy limits, and comparable verdicts. They don't apply a formula — they pattern-match against hundreds or thousands of prior outcomes they've experienced or heard about from colleagues. This knowledge cannot be written down as rules because the interactions are nonlinear (a cervical fusion in Dallas is worth 3x what it's worth in Lubbock, but a TBI is worth 2x, not 3x — and no one can explain why). Case management systems store outcome data but don't analyze it for valuation patterns. There is no "Kelley Blue Book" for PI case values.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A settlement valuation model trained on the firm's own historical outcomes. Input features: injury type (ICD-10 codes), body region, treatment type and duration, surgical intervention (yes/no, type), plaintiff age, jurisdiction (county level), carrier, special damages total, liability strength (percentage), and policy limits. Target: settlement amount or verdict amount. Model: gradient-boosted trees (handles the nonlinear interactions between jurisdiction, carrier, and injury type that make this problem hard). Output: predicted settlement range (25th-75th percentile) with the top 3 feature drivers ("this prediction is driven primarily by carrier=StateFarm, jurisdiction=HarrisCounty, and surgery=yes"). The model is retrained quarterly as new outcomes are added. Attorneys use the prediction as a baseline — they can demand above or below the model's range, but the model provides an objective anchor that reduces the wide variability of purely subjective valuation. The firm can also analyze which attorneys consistently settle above or below the model's prediction, identifying top performers and coaching opportunities.

## Who Feels the Pain
Managing partners who suspect their firm's settlement values are inconsistent but have no way to measure it. Junior associates who have no objective benchmark for case valuation and rely on asking senior attorneys (who may not be available or may give different answers). Clients whose case outcomes are partially determined by which attorney at the firm happens to be assigned their case.

## Impact If Fixed
Reduces intra-firm settlement valuation variance by 40-60%. Prevents undervaluation that leaves an estimated 10-20% of potential recovery on the table across the firm's portfolio. Provides an objective training tool for junior associates that accelerates their valuation learning curve from 5-7 years to 1-2 years. For a 500-case firm with $15K average settlement, a 10% improvement in average settlement value through more consistent, data-informed demands represents $750K in additional annual contingency revenue.
