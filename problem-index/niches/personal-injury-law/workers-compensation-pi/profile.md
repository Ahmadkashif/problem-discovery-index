# Workers' Compensation PI (Plaintiff Side)

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/personal-injury-law|Personal Injury Law]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $3-4B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~7%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — most WC plaintiff attorneys use generic case management tools with no integration into state-specific workers' comp schedules, impairment rating calculators, or administrative hearing systems
**Target Buyer:** Workers' comp plaintiff attorney handling claims against employers and their insurers
**Automation Potential:** High — indemnity benefit calculations, impairment rating lookups, MMI determination tracking, and state-specific schedule applications are all rule-based and automatable

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Workers' comp PI straddles two legal systems. The base workers' comp claim follows state administrative law — no jury trials, statutory benefit schedules, administrative law judges, and employer/insurer as the opposing party. But when a third party caused the workplace injury (defective equipment manufacturer, negligent driver who hit the work vehicle, unsafe premises where the worker was dispatched), a separate tort claim exists alongside the WC claim. The attorney must navigate both systems simultaneously, with lien rights between them. The WC carrier has a lien on any third-party tort recovery. State-specific WC benefit formulas (Average Weekly Wage calculation, compensation rate, Permanent Partial Disability schedules, Maximum Medical Improvement determination) are complex, vary across 50 states, and change frequently. Attorneys manually calculate benefits using state-published tables and frequently make errors that cost their clients thousands.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Generic PI case management (Filevine, Clio) handles document storage and deadlines but has no workers' comp awareness — no impairment rating calculators, no benefit schedule lookups, no AWW computation tools. CompScribe and similar WC-specific tools exist for the insurance/employer side but not for plaintiff attorneys. Disability rating guides (AMA Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment) are published as reference books, not integrated into any case management software. State-specific WC benefit calculators exist as standalone spreadsheets or websites maintained by state agencies, but none integrate into the attorney's workflow.
