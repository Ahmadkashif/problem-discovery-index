# After-Hours & Overnight Dispatch Operations

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/towing-companies|Towing Companies]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $2-3B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~20%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — After-hours dispatch is often handled by the owner's personal phone, a rotating on-call driver, or a third-party answering service with no dispatch capability
**Target Buyer:** Towing company owner who needs 24/7 dispatch coverage but can't afford a dedicated overnight dispatcher
**Automation Potential:** High — After-hours call intake, truck assignment, and ETA communication are prime automation targets

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Towing is a 24/7 business — breakdowns, accidents, and police calls don't stop at 6pm. But most small towing companies (3-10 trucks) can't afford a dedicated overnight dispatcher ($35,000-$50,000/year). The owner either takes overnight calls personally (leading to burnout and poor decision-making at 3am), rotates on-call drivers who dispatch themselves (no coordination, no SLA tracking), or uses an answering service that takes a message and pages a driver (adding 10-15 minutes of response time). None of these solutions provide the dispatch intelligence — truck type matching, closest-truck identification, SLA monitoring — that the daytime dispatcher provides.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Answering services (AnswerFirst, MAP Communications) take calls but can't dispatch — they relay information and the driver self-selects. Towbook has no self-dispatch mode where incoming calls auto-assign to the closest available driver. Motor club portals push calls digitally but the towing company still needs someone to acknowledge the call and dispatch a truck within minutes. No tool bridges the gap between "call received" and "truck dispatched" during unstaffed hours.
