# Rural & Highway Heavy-Duty Recovery

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/towing-companies|Towing Companies]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1.5-2B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~15%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Most run on VHF radio, phone calls from state patrol, and paper invoices; GPS tracking is rare and cloud tools are unreliable in coverage gaps
**Target Buyer:** Owner-operator running 1-3 heavy-duty wreckers servicing 100+ mile highway corridors with limited cellular connectivity
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Pricing complexity (equipment hours, mileage, winching, hazmat surcharges) and invoicing are automatable but tools must work offline

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Heavy-duty recovery is the most specialized and highest-revenue-per-call segment of towing: righting an overturned tractor-trailer can bill $5,000-$50,000+ depending on cargo, hazmat involvement, and complexity. Rural heavy-duty operators cover 100-200 mile highway corridors, responding to state patrol calls for jackknifed trucks, rollovers, and bridge strikes. Each job is unique — there's no standard process for recovering a 40-ton grain trailer in a ditch at 2am in January. The pricing is equally complex: equipment hours (boom time, air cushion time, rotator crane time), mileage to scene, environmental cleanup, traffic control, and cargo transfer all carry separate rates. These operators are deeply skilled but completely un-digitized.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Most heavy-duty recovery operators use paper invoices with handwritten line items. Towbook and TOPS handle light-duty dispatch but don't model heavy-duty job complexity (multi-equipment deployments, extended scene operations, environmental cleanup tracking). No tool helps with heavy-duty-specific pricing (which varies by state tariff, insurance company, and job complexity), scene documentation (required for insurance claims), or the unique scheduling challenge of 24/7 on-call coverage across a 200-mile corridor.
