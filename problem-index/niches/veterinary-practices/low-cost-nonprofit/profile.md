# Low-Cost & Nonprofit Clinics

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/veterinary-practices|Veterinary Practices]]
**Category:** Underserved

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1-1.5B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~2%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — many nonprofit clinics run free or ultra-low-cost PMS (often paper-based or spreadsheet-driven), with minimal integration between surgical workflows, inventory tracking, and grant reporting
**Target Buyer:** Executive director of a low-cost spay/neuter clinic, humane society veterinary program, or community veterinary clinic serving underserved populations
**Automation Potential:** High — high-volume surgical workflows and grant reporting requirements are both highly structured and repetitive, making them strong automation candidates

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Low-cost and nonprofit veterinary clinics operate on a fundamentally different model than private practices. They are grant-funded or donation-supported, charge $50-150 for surgeries that private practices charge $300-800 for, and measure success by animals served and cost per procedure rather than revenue per exam hour. Their core workload is high-volume spay/neuter — a well-run clinic performs 30-50 surgeries per day with assembly-line efficiency: pre-op intake, anesthesia induction, surgery, recovery, and discharge, each happening in a different zone with different staff. They also provide vaccination clinics (100-200 animals in a single Saturday event), basic sick animal care, and sometimes emergency services for pet owners who cannot afford private practice fees. Staff is a mix of employed veterinarians, volunteer vets, vet tech students, and community volunteers. Funding comes from municipal contracts, foundation grants, ASPCA grants, and individual donations — each with different reporting requirements.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Some nonprofit clinics use Shelter Buddy, PetPoint, or other shelter management software designed for animal shelters (tracking animals by intake source and outcome) rather than veterinary practices (tracking patients by client and medical history). Others use Cornerstone or Avimark at steeply discounted nonprofit rates but find these systems designed for individual-patient appointment workflows, not batch surgical operations. The most common gap: no tool manages the high-volume surgical day workflow — tracking 40 animals through pre-op, surgery, recovery, and discharge simultaneously, assigning animals to surgeon rotations, and generating end-of-day surgical logs. Grant reporting is equally broken — tracking animals served by zip code, species, procedure type, and funding source for quarterly grant reports requires manual data compilation from multiple sources.
