# Real-Time AI Radiograph Screening at Point of Care

**Niche:** [[niches/veterinary-practices/diagnostic-imaging/profile|Diagnostic Imaging]]
**Industry:** [[industries/veterinary-practices|Veterinary Practices]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** An AI screening tool that flags abnormalities on veterinary radiographs — fractures, masses, foreign bodies, cardiac enlargement, pleural effusion — in real time at the point of care, giving the GP vet immediate decision support before the teleradiology report arrives 24-72 hours later.
**Tags:** #cnns #object-detection #semantic-segmentation #transfer-learning #tacit-knowledge-ml #evaluation-metrics #worker-facing #revenue-impact

## The Problem
When a GP vet takes a radiograph of a dog's abdomen to check for a foreign body, they need an answer now — during the appointment, with the owner in the room, while the dog is still sedated. But the vet's own interpretation confidence is limited: they can identify obvious findings (a clearly visible sock in the stomach, a displaced fracture) but miss subtle ones (early cardiac enlargement, a small soft-tissue mass obscured by bowel gas, a hairline fracture). Sending the image to teleradiology gets a specialist-quality read, but it arrives 24-72 hours later — by which time the owner has gone home, the clinical moment has passed, and the vet has either acted on their uncertain interpretation or delayed treatment waiting for results. Board-certified veterinary radiologists develop pattern recognition through reading 50,000-100,000+ images — they see things that GP vets cannot articulate or describe, a form of visual tacit knowledge built through massive exposure.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
SignalPET has built a version of this product and is actively marketing it. But several barriers limit adoption: (1) integration — SignalPET requires uploading images to a separate platform rather than displaying results inline on the imaging workstation, adding friction to a time-pressured workflow, (2) trust — vets are skeptical of AI accuracy and worry about liability if they act on an AI finding that turns out to be wrong (or miss something the AI didn't flag), (3) species and view coverage — canine and feline thoracic/abdominal radiographs are well-covered, but orthopedic views, dental radiographs, exotic species, and unusual positions are not, (4) the business model — practices already pay for teleradiology and are reluctant to pay for AI screening AND a specialist read, so the AI must either replace teleradiology for routine cases or be priced as a nominal add-on ($3-5/study). Training a CNN for veterinary radiograph screening requires large, labeled datasets of veterinary images with radiologist-confirmed findings — data that exists within teleradiology companies but is not publicly available.

## What to Build
An AI screening engine that integrates directly with the practice's DICOM-compatible imaging workstation, analyzing each radiograph as it is captured and displaying findings within 30 seconds on the same screen. Core capabilities: (1) abnormality detection across 15-20 common findings (fractures, masses, foreign bodies, cardiac enlargement, pleural effusion, pneumothorax, megaesophagus, GDV, bladder stones, hepatomegaly, splenomegaly) using a CNN trained on 500,000+ veterinary radiographs with radiologist-confirmed labels, (2) semantic segmentation that highlights the region of concern with a confidence score and brief description ("Possible soft tissue mass in caudal abdomen — recommend ultrasound"), (3) structured output compatible with the medical record (findings can be auto-inserted into the SOAP note), (4) a "request specialist review" button that sends the image to teleradiology pre-populated with the AI findings, reducing specialist interpretation time. Transfer learning from human medical imaging CNNs (chest X-ray abnormality detection models) provides a strong starting point, adapted for species-specific anatomy.

## Target Customer
GP veterinarians who take 5-15 radiographic studies per day and currently either rely on their own uncertain interpretation or wait 24-72 hours for teleradiology results, willing to pay $3-8 per study or $199-399/month unlimited for real-time screening that improves diagnostic confidence.

## Impact If Built
Gives 20,000+ GP vets real-time radiograph screening that catches findings they would otherwise miss — studies suggest GP vets miss 20-30% of radiographic abnormalities that specialists identify. Reduces unnecessary teleradiology submissions for clearly normal studies (saving $30-80 per study), while increasing appropriate submissions for subtle findings the vet wasn't confident enough to send. Improves patient outcomes by enabling same-visit clinical decisions rather than 24-72 hour delays.
