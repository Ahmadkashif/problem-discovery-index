# Mixed Animal Rural

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/veterinary-practices|Veterinary Practices]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $3-4B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~7%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — many rural mixed practices run outdated PMS versions or paper records; those with software use it primarily for invoicing rather than medical documentation
**Target Buyer:** Rural veterinarian who sees both companion animals and livestock, typically a solo practitioner or 2-vet practice serving a wide geographic area
**Automation Potential:** Medium — the dual-species workflow creates unique inventory, regulatory, and scheduling challenges that no existing tool addresses, but any solution must work in low-connectivity rural environments

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Rural mixed animal veterinarians are the general practitioners of the veterinary world — they see a Labrador for vaccinations at 9 AM, drive 40 miles to pregnancy-check a herd of cattle at noon, remove a foxtail from a cat's ear at 3 PM, and perform an emergency C-section on a heifer at midnight. They must maintain clinical competency across companion animal medicine, equine practice, and food animal production medicine simultaneously. Their drug inventory spans both companion animal pharmaceuticals (small quantities, high margins, few regulatory constraints) and food animal drugs (large quantities, thin margins, strict FDA withdrawal period regulations that determine when a treated animal's milk or meat can enter the food supply). They are often the only veterinarian within 50-100 miles, making them an essential rural infrastructure provider. These practices are severely underserved by technology because they don't fit neatly into either companion animal PMS platforms or livestock management software.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Rural mixed practices typically own Cornerstone or Avimark (companion-animal-oriented) and attempt to use it for all species. Some also maintain a separate livestock management tool (like CattleMax or CowSense) for herd health records, creating duplicate data entry. Drug inventory management is particularly broken: the PMS tracks companion animal dispensing but has no concept of FDA food animal drug withdrawal periods, bulk livestock drug purchasing, or the regulatory requirement to maintain treatment records for food-producing animals. Scheduling is equally problematic — farm calls for livestock work require route planning across large geographic areas, while companion animal appointments require traditional time-slot scheduling, and no tool handles both.
