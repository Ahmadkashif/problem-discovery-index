## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Exotic animal veterinarians treat reptiles (bearded dragons, ball pythons, tortoises), birds (parrots, cockatiels, raptors), small mammals (rabbits, ferrets, guinea pigs, hedgehogs, sugar gliders), and sometimes amphibians and invertebrates. Each species group has fundamentally different anatomy, physiology, drug metabolism, disease patterns, and husbandry requirements. A rabbit's GI system bears no resemblance to a dog's; a parrot's respiratory anatomy has no mammalian equivalent; reptile drug dosing must account for metabolic scaling and environmental temperature. Most exotic vets are not board-certified specialists — they are companion animal GPs who developed exotic expertise through years of continuing education, mentorship, and self-study. There are only ~200 board-certified avian and exotic specialists in the US, serving an estimated 15-20 million exotic pets. These practitioners operate in a knowledge desert: no standard drug formulary, no consensus treatment protocols, and diagnostic reference ranges that are often extrapolated from a handful of research papers rather than validated population data.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Exotic vets use the same PMS as companion animal practices (Cornerstone, Avimark, Vetspire) but the species-specific features are absent. IDEXX in-house diagnostic analyzers provide reference ranges for dogs and cats — when a vet runs bloodwork on a bearded dragon, the "normal ranges" displayed are canine, which is clinically meaningless. Drug dosing is looked up in the Exotic Animal Formulary textbook (Carpenter's) or personal notes, not in the PMS. Clinical notes templates are designed for canine/feline anatomy ("Heart: normal sinus rhythm, no murmur" is meaningless for a snake). The gap is a clinical decision support layer with species-specific drug formularies, diagnostic reference ranges, husbandry checklists, and anatomy-appropriate medical record templates.
