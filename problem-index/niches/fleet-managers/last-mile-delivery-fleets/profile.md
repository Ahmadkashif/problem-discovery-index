# Last-Mile Delivery Fleets

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/fleet-managers|Fleet Managers]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $18B
**Share of Parent Industry:** 25%
**Digital Adoption:** Medium-High — Large carriers use Samsara and Motive, but mid-size delivery fleets (50-500 vehicles) often use fragmented combinations of GPS tracking, paper logs, and spreadsheet-based maintenance scheduling.
**Target Buyer:** Delivery fleet operations manager at a regional courier, food delivery, or e-commerce fulfillment company
**Automation Potential:** High — Route optimization, driver performance scoring, and vehicle rotation decisions follow quantifiable rules and are heavily data-dependent.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Last-mile delivery fleets operate in a uniquely punishing cycle: high daily mileage in stop-and-go urban traffic, 100+ stops per route, frequent door openings that stress HVAC and electrical systems, and driver behavior that varies wildly (hard braking, curb strikes, idle time). These vehicles accumulate wear patterns that differ fundamentally from highway fleets — brakes wear 3x faster, transmissions experience 10x more shift cycles, and cargo area damage is constant. Fleet managers need to rotate vehicles across routes to equalize wear, predict failures that are specific to stop-and-go duty cycles, and manage driver behavior that directly impacts vehicle life.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Samsara and Motive provide GPS tracking, basic driver scoring, and ELD compliance. Fleetio and Fleet Complete handle maintenance scheduling. Route optimization tools (OptimoRoute, Routific) handle routing. But none connect these three domains: the route a vehicle runs determines its wear pattern, which determines its maintenance needs, which determines its availability for tomorrow's routes. The fleet manager manually bridges routing, maintenance, and telematics data.
