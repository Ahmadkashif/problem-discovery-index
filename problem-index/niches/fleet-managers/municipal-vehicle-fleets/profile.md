# Municipal Vehicle Fleets

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/fleet-managers|Fleet Managers]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $8B
**Share of Parent Industry:** 11%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Most municipalities use legacy fleet systems installed 10-20 years ago (or paper-based tracking), with procurement cycles too slow to adopt modern cloud platforms.
**Target Buyer:** City or county fleet superintendent managing 50-500 municipal vehicles
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Maintenance scheduling, fuel reconciliation, and asset lifecycle management are automatable, but government procurement and IT security requirements create adoption barriers.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Municipal fleets include police cruisers, fire trucks, ambulances, public works vehicles, parks department trucks, and administrative sedans — a wildly diverse fleet with each department having different operational requirements, different usage patterns, and different maintenance urgencies. A police cruiser idles 6-8 hours per day (destroying engines faster than highway miles), a snowplow operates intensively for 3 months then sits for 9, and a parks department mower has completely different maintenance cycles than any road vehicle. The fleet superintendent must manage this diversity under government budgeting constraints, union labor rules, and procurement regulations that make commercial fleet tools difficult to adopt.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Many municipalities still use AssetWorks FleetFocus or Faster Asset Solutions — legacy client-server systems installed in the 2000s with outdated UIs and limited mobile access. Some use Cartegraph for asset management. Modern cloud platforms (Fleetio, RTA Fleet Management) exist but require government procurement processes that take 12-18 months. The gap isn't technology — it's packaging modern fleet management in a way that passes government IT security review, fits government budgeting cycles, and handles the diverse vehicle types municipalities operate.
