# Reserve Study & Capital Planning

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/hoa-management|HOA Management]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $500M-800M (services)
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~5-7% (embedded services cost)
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — reserve studies are delivered as static PDF reports, reserve funding is tracked in spreadsheets or basic platform modules, and capital project planning is managed through board meeting discussions
**Target Buyer:** Reserve study analyst, board treasurer, or community manager responsible for reserve fund adequacy and capital project planning
**Automation Potential:** High — component condition assessment, remaining useful life estimation, replacement cost inflation, and funding adequacy scenarios are all calculable and automatable

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Reserve studies — the engineering and financial analysis that determines how much an HOA must save annually to fund future capital replacements — are a $500M+ services industry unto themselves. A reserve study identifies every major building/community component (roof, parking lot, pool, HVAC, painting, fencing), estimates remaining useful life and replacement cost, and models whether current reserve contributions will fund all replacements on schedule. Post-Surfside legislation in Florida (and spreading to other states) has made reserve studies mandatory and eliminated the ability of boards to vote to underfund reserves — transforming reserve planning from advisory to compulsory. The workflow is highly quantitative (component inventories, inflation projections, funding scenarios) and ripe for automation.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Reserve study firms use proprietary software (Reserve Study Pro, FacilityDude) or custom Excel models. Community managers receive static PDF reserve studies and manually track reserve balances against the study's projections. No platform connects the reserve study's component inventory and funding projections to the community's actual financial data, maintenance history, and capital project execution — creating a disconnect between the study (a snapshot) and ongoing operations (reality).
