# Self-Managed Small HOAs

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/hoa-management|HOA Management]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1.5-2.5B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~15-20%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — managed by volunteer board members using personal bank accounts, Excel for budgets, email for communications, and paper files for CC&Rs and meeting minutes
**Target Buyer:** Volunteer board president or treasurer at a self-managed HOA with 10-100 units
**Automation Potential:** Medium — assessment collection, financial reporting, and meeting management are automatable; volunteer board recruitment and governance education resist automation

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
An estimated 150,000-200,000 small HOAs in the US are self-managed — governed entirely by volunteer board members who have no property management training, no budget for professional management ($15-$50/unit/month is unaffordable for a 20-unit community), and no technology beyond personal email and banking. The volunteer board president works 5-15 unpaid hours per month: collecting assessments (often by check), paying vendors, managing the community bank account, enforcing CC&Rs (awkwardly, since they enforce on their neighbors), running board meetings, and handling resident complaints. Board burnout produces 50%+ annual turnover, and incoming board members inherit incomplete financial records, unsigned vendor contracts, and unresolved violation disputes with no transition documentation.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Vantaca and AppFolio HOA are designed for professional management companies, not volunteer boards — too complex, too expensive ($200-$500/month), and requiring training that volunteers don't have. PayHOA and HOALife provide simplified platforms for self-managed HOAs, but adoption remains low because volunteer board members don't know these tools exist and resist spending community funds on software. QuickBooks is used for basic accounting by financially-savvy treasurers.
