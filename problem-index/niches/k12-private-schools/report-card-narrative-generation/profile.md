# Report Card Narrative Generation

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/k12-private-schools|K-12 Private Schools]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** ~$1B in teacher labor costs dedicated to narrative report card writing across private K-12 schools
**Share of Parent Industry:** N/A (operational cost center — teacher time)
**Digital Adoption:** Low — narrative report cards are written in Word, Google Docs, or the SIS text field, with no AI assistance; templates are informal and shared informally among teachers
**Target Buyer:** Division head, academic dean, or head of school
**Automation Potential:** High — narrative report cards follow predictable structures (strengths, growth areas, social-emotional observations, recommendations) with student-specific content that can be drafted from academic and behavioral data

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Private schools — especially independent and Montessori schools — pride themselves on individualized narrative report cards rather than (or in addition to) letter grades. A teacher writing narratives for 60-80 students produces 60-80 unique paragraphs 2-4 times per year. Each narrative must be specific to the student (referencing particular assignments, behaviors, and growth moments), aligned with the school's communication style (warm but honest), and free of the repetitive language that parents notice when narratives feel templated. Teachers report spending 8-12 hours per reporting cycle on narrative writing — one of the most time-consuming non-instructional tasks.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Some schools maintain informal "comment banks" — collections of starter phrases organized by skill area and performance level. These are typically shared Google Docs maintained by department heads. No tool generates student-specific narrative drafts from academic data (grades, assignment completion, assessment results) and teacher observations. The SIS (Veracross, FACTS) provides a text field for narratives but no drafting assistance.
