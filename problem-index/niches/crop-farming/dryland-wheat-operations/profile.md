# Dryland Wheat & Small Grain Operations

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/crop-farming|Crop Farming]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $15-20B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~7%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — GPS guidance is common, but yield monitors on combines are less prevalent than in corn/soybean, and precision ag tools designed for irrigated Corn Belt conditions poorly serve dryland Great Plains operations
**Target Buyer:** Farm Owner / Operator managing dryland wheat, grain sorghum, and small grains in the Great Plains (western Kansas, Oklahoma panhandle, Montana, eastern Colorado, Dakotas)
**Automation Potential:** Medium — fallow management decisions, variety selection, and stored soil moisture management are the primary decision support opportunities

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Dryland wheat and small grain operations on the Great Plains operate under fundamentally different agronomic constraints than irrigated Corn Belt farms: rainfall is the limiting input (12-20 inches annually, highly variable), fields are often in crop-fallow rotation (producing a crop every other year), and stored soil moisture at planting determines yield potential more than any in-season management decision. The tacit knowledge in dryland farming centers on reading soil moisture — an experienced dryland farmer probes the soil profile with a shovel, feels moisture at different depths, and determines whether the stored moisture is sufficient to justify planting a crop or whether the field should remain in fallow for another year. This moisture assessment determines whether the farmer invests $100-200/acre in a crop or $15/acre in fallow management — a $85-185/acre decision made on the basis of tactile judgment.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Precision ag platforms are designed for irrigated row crops — their yield prediction models, satellite imagery interpretation, and input recommendation engines assume Corn Belt conditions (30+ inches of rainfall, consistent growing season, irrigated stress management). Dryland wheat operations need tools that model stored soil moisture, predict yield from moisture profile at planting, and optimize crop-fallow rotation decisions. Weather-based decision tools (DTN, WeatherBug) provide forecasts but do not integrate soil moisture monitoring with planting decisions.
