# Vaccine & Biologics Logistics

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/cold-chain-logistics|Cold Chain Logistics]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $5-8B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~5-7%
**Digital Adoption:** Medium — monitoring hardware is deployed but ultra-cold chain management and multi-stakeholder coordination remain manual
**Target Buyer:** Clinical supply chain director or biologics logistics manager at biopharma companies, public health agencies, or specialty distributors
**Automation Potential:** Medium-High — ultra-cold chain monitoring, vaccine inventory management, and regulatory documentation follow strict protocols amenable to automation

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Vaccine and biologics logistics operates at temperature extremes that standard pharma cold chain cannot serve — ultra-cold (-60 to -80 degrees C for mRNA vaccines), cryogenic (-150 to -196 degrees C for cell and gene therapies), and controlled room temperature with narrow bands (2-8 degrees C for most vaccines). The COVID-19 vaccine rollout exposed the infrastructure gaps: ultra-cold storage capacity is limited, last-mile distribution to clinics and pharmacies requires precise timing coordination, and vaccine inventory management (tracking vials by lot, expiration, and puncture count) is done manually at administration sites. Unlike standard pharma shipments, vaccines involve multi-stakeholder handoffs (manufacturer to distributor to state health department to clinic) with different documentation requirements at each stage.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Controlant and Berlinger provide ultra-cold temperature monitoring. CDC's VTrckS system manages federal vaccine ordering and distribution. State immunization information systems (IIS) track vaccine administration. No platform connects the logistics chain — from manufacturer shipment through distributor handling through clinic receiving — with continuous temperature documentation and inventory status tracking across all handoff points.
