# Contractual Adjustment Verification to Catch Underpayments

**Niche:** [[niches/medical-billing/payment-posting-reconciliation/profile|Payment Posting & Reconciliation]]
**Industry:** [[industries/medical-billing|Medical Billing]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Payers underpay contracted rates on 2-5% of claims and most billing operations never catch it because verifying that a $95 payment matches the $95 contracted rate for that CPT code with that payer requires cross-referencing a fee schedule document that isn't loaded into the PM system.
**Tags:** #gradient-boosting #feature-engineering #evaluation-metrics #revenue-impact #automation #data-integration

## The Problem
Every provider has a contracted fee schedule with each payer — a document (typically a PDF or spreadsheet) specifying the reimbursement rate for each CPT/HCPCS code. When a payer sends payment, the billing operation posts the amount but rarely verifies it against the contracted rate. A payer might pay $85 for a CPT code with a contracted rate of $95, and the $10 difference could be: (1) a legitimate patient deductible/coinsurance amount, (2) a correct contractual adjustment reflecting an updated fee schedule the provider signed, (3) a payer processing error, or (4) an intentional underpayment the payer hopes won't be caught. Distinguishing between these scenarios requires cross-referencing three data sources: the ERA payment amount, the payer contract fee schedule, and the patient's specific plan benefits. No PM system performs this cross-reference because fee schedule data is not loaded into PM systems — it exists in PDF contracts stored in filing cabinets or shared drives. The result: systematic underpayments go undetected, costing practices 2-5% of revenue.

## Why It's Still Broken
The contractual rate verification problem has a straightforward technical solution — load fee schedules into software and compare them against payments — but three practical barriers persist: (1) Fee schedules are delivered in non-standard formats (PDF tables, Excel spreadsheets, payer portal downloads) with different structures per payer, making ingestion difficult. (2) Fee schedules change when contracts are renegotiated or when payers issue annual updates, requiring ongoing maintenance. (3) The verification math isn't simple — allowable amount minus patient responsibility (deductible + coinsurance + copay applied by payer) should equal provider payment, but this calculation requires knowing the patient's remaining deductible and coinsurance percentage, which are in the ERA's patient responsibility fields. Performing this calculation per claim at volume is tedious, and billing operations deprioritize it in favor of more visible revenue cycle tasks.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A contractual rate verification tool that integrates with the PM system's payment posting workflow: (1) Fee schedule ingestion — accepts payer fee schedules in any format (PDF, Excel, CSV, payer portal export), parses them into a structured database, and maps CPT codes to contracted rates per payer. (2) Automated verification — for each posted payment, calculates: contracted rate - patient responsibility (from ERA fields) = expected provider payment, and compares against actual provider payment. Any variance above a configurable threshold ($2-5) is flagged. (3) Underpayment worklist — presents flagged claims with the variance amount, contracted rate, paid rate, and patient responsibility breakdown, enabling the billing team to dispute the specific underpayment with the payer. (4) Trend detection — a gradient-boosted model identifies systematic underpayment patterns: specific CPT codes where a payer consistently pays below contract, which may indicate a fee schedule loading error on the payer's side. (5) Fee schedule maintenance alerts — prompts the user to update fee schedules when contracts are renegotiated or when a pattern of variances suggests the loaded fee schedule is outdated.

## Who Feels the Pain
The billing manager who suspects underpayments but can't quantify them, and the payment posting specialist who posts payments without the context needed to identify when a payment is below the contracted rate.

## Impact If Fixed
Identifies and recovers 2-5% of revenue currently lost to undetected payer underpayments. For a billing operation posting $500,000/month in payments, catching a 3% underpayment rate recovers $15,000/month — $180,000/year. Once the payer is made aware of systematic underpayments, they typically correct the fee schedule error, preventing future underpayments. The one-time retroactive recovery (going back 6-12 months of posted payments) often yields $50,000-150,000 for a mid-sized billing operation that has never performed contractual rate verification.
