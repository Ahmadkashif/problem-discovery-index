# Source Document Management and Chain of Custody Tracking

**Niche:** [[niches/podcasting-networks/true-crime-networks/profile|True Crime Networks]]
**Industry:** [[industries/podcasting-networks|Podcasting Networks]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Producers store case documents — court records, police reports, interview recordings, FOIA responses — in personal Dropbox folders and email attachments, making them unfindable when a legal challenge requires proving the factual basis for a claim.
**Tags:** #data-integration #compliance #automation #worker-facing

## The Problem
A true crime producer working a major case accumulates hundreds of documents: court filings downloaded from PACER, police reports obtained via FOIA, newspaper clippings, interview recordings (with consent documentation), photographs, and correspondence with sources. These documents are stored in a personal Dropbox folder structure that makes sense to the original producer but is incomprehensible to anyone else. When a subject threatens a lawsuit 18 months after publication and the network's attorney asks "show me the court record that supports the claim you made in Episode 12 at minute 34:22," the producer spends days searching their files. If the producer has left the network, the documentation may be entirely inaccessible.

## Why It's Still Broken
Document management systems (Google Drive, SharePoint, Dropbox) provide storage and search but don't enforce the organizational discipline that investigative content requires. Newsroom document platforms (DocumentCloud) are designed for text documents, not the mixed-media collection (audio interviews, photos, court filings, spreadsheets) that true crime production generates. And producers resist imposed organizational structures because each case has a different document landscape — a cold case from the 1970s has different source types than a recent trial. The real problem is that there's no standard for what constitutes a "case file" in audio journalism.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A document management system for investigative audio where: (1) every document is uploaded with metadata (source, date obtained, relevance to which episode/claim, classification type), (2) documents are linked to specific facts/claims in episode transcripts so that any statement can be traced back to its source document, (3) interview recordings are stored with consent forms and are searchable by transcript, (4) FOIA requests are tracked with submission date, response date, and documents received, (5) the entire case file is network property (not personal files) with access controls, and (6) when a legal challenge arises, the attorney can pull a complete evidence package for any claim with one search. Templates for common document types (court filing, police report, interview consent) standardize the intake process.

## Who Feels the Pain
Producers who spend 5–10 hours per episode locating source documents for fact-checking and script verification, and network executives who face legal exposure when supporting documentation can't be located.

## Impact If Fixed
Reduces document retrieval time from 1–2 hours to 5 minutes per query. Eliminates the existential risk of losing the documentary basis for published claims when a producer leaves the network. Saves 50–100 hours per legal challenge by enabling instant evidence assembly. Enables new producers to pick up existing cases without a 2-week document archaeology project.
