# Commercial Tenant Improvement Contractors

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/general-contractors|General Contractors]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $50-60B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~27%
**Digital Adoption:** Medium — many use Procore for larger TI projects, but multi-project resource visibility and landlord approval workflows are manual
**Target Buyer:** Commercial TI contractor owner or project manager running 10-20 simultaneous small projects
**Automation Potential:** High — multi-project resource allocation, landlord approval tracking, and ADA compliance checklists are structured, repeatable processes

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
TI contractors operate in a fundamentally different rhythm than ground-up builders. Projects are fast-track (6-10 weeks typical), happening inside occupied buildings with noise/dust/access restrictions, and require approvals from both the tenant (who is paying) and the landlord/property manager (who controls the building). A TI contractor typically runs 10-20 simultaneous small-to-mid projects, each with a 2-4 person crew rotation. The core operational challenge isn't managing one complex project — it's optimizing resource allocation across many small projects that each have compressed timelines, overlapping trade sequences, and dual-approval chains. Procore handles single-project management well but was built for large commercial jobs, not for the multi-project portfolio view that TI contractors need.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Procore is used by larger TI firms but its per-project pricing makes it expensive for a portfolio of 15 small TI jobs. Buildertrend targets residential, not commercial. Most TI contractors use a combination of Excel for resource scheduling across projects, email for landlord approvals, and a basic PM tool for individual project tracking. No tool provides a portfolio-level view showing: which crews are available next week across all projects, which projects are blocked on landlord approval, and which fast-track schedules are at risk of overlap conflicts in shared building spaces (freight elevators, loading docks, after-hours access windows).
