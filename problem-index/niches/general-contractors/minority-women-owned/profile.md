# Minority/Women-Owned Contractors (MBE/WBE/DBE)

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/general-contractors|General Contractors]]
**Category:** Underserved

## Profile
**Market Size:** $15-20B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~8%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — most are small firms (5-25 employees) using basic accounting tools and personal networks; certification management is paper-driven
**Target Buyer:** MBE/WBE/DBE certified contractor owner, typically a first- or second-generation business with $1-10M annual revenue
**Automation Potential:** High — certification tracking, set-aside contract discovery, and bonding capacity management are all structured, rule-based problems poorly served by existing tools

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
MBE/WBE/DBE contractors operate in a unique regulatory ecosystem where their competitive advantage is their certification status. They access set-aside contracts, sole-source opportunities, and subcontracting commitments that non-certified firms cannot compete for. But the certification itself is a burden — requiring annual renewal with extensive documentation (financial statements, ownership proof, personal net worth disclosures, control documentation), and different certifying agencies (SBA for 8(a), state DOTs for DBE, local agencies for MBE/WBE) have different requirements, timelines, and renewal processes. These firms also face structural challenges: lower bonding capacity (limiting project size), limited access to capital (restricting cash flow on government projects with 60-90 day payment cycles), and dependence on prime contractor relationships for subcontracting opportunities. Existing construction tech serves neither their certification management needs nor their unique business development model.

## Current Tools & Gaps
SAM.gov lists federal contracting opportunities but search is keyword-based and generic — it doesn't match a contractor's specific certifications, bonding capacity, geographic range, and trade specialties. State and local procurement portals each have their own interfaces with no aggregation. No tool tracks certification renewal deadlines across multiple certifying agencies or helps compile renewal documentation. Surety bonding is managed through broker relationships with no visibility into how to grow bonding capacity strategically. Prime contractors find DBE subs through personal networks and outdated certification directories, not through capability-matched search.
