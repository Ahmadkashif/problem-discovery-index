# Schedule Delay Cascade Predictor

**Niche:** [[niches/general-contractors/project-scheduling-management/profile|Project Scheduling & Management]]
**Industry:** [[industries/general-contractors|General Contractors]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** When the framing crew falls 3 days behind, the system instantly shows that drywall shifts 5 days, painting shifts 8 days, and the project completion moves from April 15 to April 28 — including weather windows, sub mobilization lead times, and inspector availability.
**Tags:** #gradient-boosting #time-series-forecasting #tacit-knowledge-ml #feature-engineering #optimization-fundamentals #worker-facing

## The Problem
Construction schedules are interdependent webs — 30-50 activities connected by predecessor/successor relationships, resource constraints, and external dependencies (weather, inspections, material deliveries). When one activity slips, the cascade through downstream activities is not linear. A 2-day slip in framing doesn't just push drywall by 2 days — it pushes drywall to the next available mobilization window for that crew (which might be 5 days later), which pushes painting past a planned inspection date (adding another 3-day wait), which pushes flooring into a week when the flooring sub is committed to another project. Experienced superintendents model these cascades intuitively — they know which subs have flexible schedules, which inspections have long lead times, and which weather windows can't be missed. Junior PMs see a 2-day delay and underestimate the true impact, leading to unrealistic recovery schedules and eventually missed completion dates with contractual penalties.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
Standard CPM scheduling tools (MS Project, P6) can calculate cascade impacts using predecessor logic, but they model an idealized world — every sub is available the next day, inspections happen on demand, and weather doesn't exist. Real cascade prediction requires: (1) sub-specific mobilization models — knowing that the drywall sub needs 3-day notice and only starts on Mondays; (2) weather integration — automatically blocking exterior activities during forecasted rain and calculating the probability-weighted impact on completion; (3) inspection scheduling reality — incorporating actual inspector availability, not the 24-hour turnaround the schedule assumes; (4) historical sub performance data — using past project data to model how each sub actually performs (the electrical sub is typically 2 days late on rough-in, the plumber is usually early). This is a data fusion problem combining schedule logic, external APIs (weather, inspector systems), and historical performance that no current tool integrates.

## What to Build
A predictive scheduling engine that layers on top of the project's baseline CPM schedule and continuously recalculates the completion date using real-world data. Inputs: (1) the baseline schedule with predecessor/successor logic; (2) daily progress updates from the field (which activities started/completed/slipped); (3) weather forecasts (10-day rolling) mapped to activity weather sensitivity; (4) sub mobilization rules (lead time, start-day preferences, availability windows); (5) inspection lead times by jurisdiction; (6) historical sub performance from the GC's past projects. Output: a continuously updated project completion forecast with probability ranges ("70% chance of completing by April 20, 90% chance by April 28"), a cascade visualization showing how today's delay propagates through the schedule, and early warnings when the forecasted completion approaches the contractual deadline. The superintendent validates and adjusts the model daily as part of the daily report process — no separate data entry.

## Target Customer
Project managers and superintendents at GCs running $1M-$50M projects who currently manage schedule risk through experience and intuition, and GC owners who discover completion date risk weeks too late to mitigate it.

## Impact If Built
Surfaces schedule risk 2-4 weeks earlier than current manual methods, giving the superintendent time to re-sequence trades, authorize overtime, or negotiate with subs before the delay is locked in. Reduces contractual delay penalties (which can range from $500-$5,000 per day depending on project type) by enabling proactive schedule recovery. Captures the experienced superintendent's cascade-prediction ability in a tool that junior PMs can use, accelerating their development from 5-7 years of experience to 1-2 years plus the system.
