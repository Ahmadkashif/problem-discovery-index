# Residential Remodeling Contractors

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/general-contractors|General Contractors]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $40-50B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~22%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — most 5-15 person remodeling companies use paper or basic tools; Buildertrend is aspirational, not standard
**Target Buyer:** Remodeling contractor owner (typically 5-20 employees), often an owner-operator who sells, manages, and sometimes works on jobs
**Automation Potential:** High — hidden condition documentation, change order generation, and scope management are pattern-rich processes currently handled through experience and memory

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Remodeling is structurally different from new construction because the existing building is an unknown variable. Opening a wall reveals plumbing that wasn't on any drawing, subfloor damage that wasn't visible, or electrical wiring that violates current code. Every remodel involves a tension between the contracted scope and the reality discovered during demolition. The homeowner is living in the house during construction — managing dust, noise, access to kitchens and bathrooms, and the emotional stress of a disrupted home. Permit requirements vary wildly by jurisdiction (some cities require permits for a bathroom remodel, others don't). And scope creep is endemic: the homeowner says "while you're at it, can you also..." 3-5 times per project, and the contractor often does the work without a signed change order because stopping to paper it feels adversarial in a relationship-driven business.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Buildertrend is the most common digital PM tool for remodeling, but it's designed for custom home builders running $500K+ projects — it's overbuilt for a 5-person remodeling crew doing $30-80K kitchen and bath renovations. Most remodelers use a combination of handwritten notes, text messages with the homeowner, and QuickBooks for invoicing. No tool helps with the core remodeling-specific challenges: documenting hidden conditions discovered during demo, generating change orders with photos and cost estimates on the spot, or managing the "while you're at it" scope creep that bleeds margin.
