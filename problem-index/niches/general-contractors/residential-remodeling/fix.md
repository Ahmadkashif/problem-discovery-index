# Scope Creep Documentation

**Niche:** [[niches/general-contractors/residential-remodeling/profile|Residential Remodeling Contractors]]
**Industry:** [[industries/general-contractors|General Contractors]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Remodelers stop doing free work because the homeowner asked "while you're at it, can you also..." and nobody wrote it down as a change order.
**Tags:** #large-language-models #automation #revenue-impact #worker-facing

## The Problem
Scope creep is the single largest margin killer in residential remodeling. A homeowner watching their kitchen demo says "while you're at it, can you move that outlet?" or "could you add a shelf in the pantry?" The contractor says yes — it's a $200-$800 task, and refusing feels adversarial when you're in someone's home for 6 weeks. But the request never gets documented as a change order. Over a typical kitchen remodel, 5-15 of these micro-additions accumulate to $2,000-$8,000 in unbilled work. The contractor absorbs the cost because there's no paper trail, and bringing it up weeks later creates conflict. Industry surveys consistently show that 20-30% of remodeling contractors report scope creep as their primary margin erosion factor.

## Why It's Still Broken
Scope creep is a social problem as much as a documentation problem. The contractor is standing in the homeowner's kitchen, the homeowner makes a reasonable-sounding request, and the friction of pulling out a laptop, writing a change order, pricing it, and asking for a signature feels disproportionate to a $300 task. Current tools require too many steps to capture a micro-change-order: Buildertrend's change order workflow was designed for $5,000+ scope changes with formal pricing and approval, not for a $300 "add an outlet" request. The result is that contractors either eat the cost or batch these conversations into an uncomfortable end-of-project reconciliation.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A voice-activated micro-change-order tool on the contractor's phone. When the homeowner says "can you also add recessed lighting in the pantry?" the contractor taps a button, speaks: "Client requested recessed lighting in pantry, two 4-inch cans, not in original scope," and the system generates a simple change order with a cost estimate (pulled from the contractor's standard pricing or regional averages), sends it to the homeowner's phone as a text with an approve button, and logs it to the project record. Total time: 60 seconds. The homeowner sees a professional, transparent process — not a confrontational "that'll cost you extra" conversation. The contractor captures revenue they would otherwise lose.

## Who Feels the Pain
The contractor-owner who absorbs thousands in unbilled work per project. Lead carpenters and foremen who do the extra work knowing it's not being billed but can't refuse because the homeowner is standing right there. And ultimately the homeowner, who would prefer transparent pricing over discovering at the end of the project that they owe an unexpected $5K reconciliation bill.

## Impact If Fixed
Recovers $2,000-$8,000 per remodeling project in currently unbilled scope creep. On a contractor doing 15-20 projects per year, that's $30K-$160K in annual recovered revenue — often the difference between a profitable year and a break-even one. Eliminates end-of-project billing disputes by documenting changes in real time with client approval.
