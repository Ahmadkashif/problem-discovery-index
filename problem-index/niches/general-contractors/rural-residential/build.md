# Rural Sub & Material Sourcing Network

**Niche:** [[niches/general-contractors/rural-residential/profile|Rural Residential Contractors]]
**Industry:** [[industries/general-contractors|General Contractors]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** Rural GCs find available subcontractors and source materials across a 100-mile radius through a regional network — instead of calling the same three guys and hoping one picks up.
**Tags:** #gradient-boosting #feature-engineering #optimization-fundamentals #data-integration #workflow-orchestration

## The Problem
A rural GC in central Montana needs an electrician for a custom home starting in 6 weeks. The two electricians they normally use are booked for 3 months. The GC's only option is to start calling electricians in increasingly distant towns — Billings (90 miles), Great Falls (120 miles) — hoping to find one willing to travel and available in the right window. This phone-call search takes 5-15 hours spread over days, and often fails — resulting in either a project delay or the GC attempting electrical work themselves (creating liability and code compliance risks). The same problem repeats for every trade on every project. Material sourcing follows the same pattern: the local lumber yard doesn't stock engineered floor joists, so the GC calls suppliers in three different cities comparing prices, delivery dates, and freight charges.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
Urban construction marketplaces (BuildZoom, Thumbtack's contractor side) aggregate demand in dense markets where subs compete for work. Rural markets have the opposite dynamic — demand exists but supply is thin and geographically dispersed. Building a network for rural contractors requires: (1) recruiting subs who serve wide geographic areas and are willing to travel 50-100+ miles for the right project; (2) understanding that "availability" in rural markets means 6-8 week scheduling windows, not next-week dispatch; (3) accounting for travel costs and per diem in pricing, which changes the economics fundamentally; (4) operating in areas with limited internet connectivity, requiring offline-capable tools. The small, dispersed market has not attracted venture-backed marketplace builders.

## What to Build
A regional subcontractor and material sourcing network for rural construction, organized by geographic regions (not metro areas). Subcontractors create profiles listing: trades, licenses held (by state/county), geographic range they'll travel, typical availability timeline, and pricing approach (whether they charge travel/per diem). GCs post upcoming trade needs with project location, timeline, and scope. The system matches and notifies qualified subs within range, showing the GC real-time availability across the region. For materials, the platform aggregates pricing and delivery timelines from regional suppliers — showing that a specific engineered truss is $X from Supplier A (90 miles, 2-week delivery) vs. $Y from Supplier B (140 miles, 1-week delivery, $Z freight). The system works offline and syncs when connectivity is available.

## Target Customer
Rural GCs and builders serving areas with populations under 50,000 who spend 5-15 hours per project on subcontractor sourcing and material procurement phone calls. Secondary market: rural subcontractors looking to fill schedule gaps with projects outside their immediate area.

## Impact If Built
Reduces subcontractor sourcing time from 5-15 hours to 1-2 hours per trade per project. Expands the effective sub pool from 2-3 local options to 10-20 regional options, reducing project delays from sub unavailability by 40-60%. Enables rural GCs to compare material pricing and delivery across regional suppliers, saving 10-20% on materials through competitive visibility they currently lack.
