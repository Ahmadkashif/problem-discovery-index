# HVAC Fault Diagnosis Decision Support

**Niche:** [[niches/hvac-contractors/residential-service-repair/profile|Residential Service & Repair]]
**Industry:** [[industries/hvac-contractors|HVAC Contractors]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** A diagnostic decision support system that takes symptom descriptions, system type, and measured readings (superheat, subcooling, pressures, amperage) and returns ranked probable diagnoses with step-by-step verification procedures — replacing the 5-10 years of pattern recognition that separates expert technicians from apprentices.
**Tags:** #tacit-knowledge-ml #gradient-boosting #data-integration #worker-facing #revenue-impact

## The Problem
HVAC fault diagnosis is the defining tacit knowledge task in the industry. An experienced technician arrives at a home with a "no cooling" complaint, listens to the system run for 30 seconds, checks superheat and subcooling readings, measures compressor amperage, and within 5 minutes has narrowed the diagnosis to 2-3 probable causes. A junior technician with the same readings doesn't know what the numbers mean in context — a subcooling reading of 5°F could indicate low refrigerant charge, a restriction in the liquid line, or a failing TXV, depending on superheat, ambient temperature, and system age. The misdiagnosis rate for technicians with under 3 years of experience is estimated at 30-40%, leading to unnecessary part replacements ($200-600 per wrong part), repeat visits (each costing $150+ in dispatch costs), and customer dissatisfaction that kills referral revenue. The expert diagnostic pattern is: system type + age + symptom cluster + 4-6 measured readings = ranked diagnosis. This is a structured multiclass classification problem, but nobody has built it because the training data — expert-verified diagnoses paired with pre-diagnosis readings — has never been systematically collected.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
Three barriers. First, data collection: the diagnostic reasoning of experienced technicians is never recorded. They arrive, diagnose, fix, and invoice — the invoice shows "replaced capacitor, $385" but not the diagnostic pathway that led there. To build a training dataset, you need to instrument the diagnostic process: capture readings, capture the technician's hypothesis, capture the verified root cause. This requires changing field workflow, which techs resist. Second, the label quality problem: even experienced technicians disagree on root cause in ambiguous cases. A system with both low refrigerant charge and a partially restricted filter-drier may be labeled as either diagnosis depending on which the tech addresses first. Third, the system diversity challenge: there are hundreds of residential HVAC system configurations (split systems, heat pumps, package units, dual fuel, ductless mini-splits) across dozens of manufacturers, each with different normal operating parameters. A model must handle this combinatorial space.

## What to Build
A mobile diagnostic assistant that technicians use during service calls. The technician enters: system type (brand, model, tonnage, refrigerant type, age), customer complaint (selected from structured symptom categories: no cooling, weak cooling, short cycling, strange noise, water leak, ice on coils, high energy bills), and measured readings (suction pressure, discharge pressure, superheat, subcooling, compressor amperage, supply/return temperature split, ambient temperature). A gradient-boosted multiclass classifier — trained on a growing dataset of expert-verified diagnoses — returns the top 3 most probable root causes with confidence scores and a step-by-step verification procedure for each: "87% likely: Low refrigerant charge. Verify: check for oil stains at service valve connections, perform electronic leak detection at evaporator coil joints and line set connections." The system learns from outcome data: when the technician confirms the actual root cause after repair, it strengthens the training signal. Initial training data comes from manufacturer service bulletins, HVAC training curricula (RSES, ACCA), and a paid cohort of 50-100 experienced technicians who annotate diagnostic scenarios.

## Target Customer
Residential HVAC service companies with 5-25 technicians, especially those hiring apprentices and struggling with the 12-24 month ramp time to independent diagnostic capability. Also HVAC training programs (trade schools, union apprenticeships) that need a diagnostic reasoning tool for instruction.

## Impact If Built
Reduces junior technician misdiagnosis rate from 30-40% to under 10%, eliminating $50K-150K annually in unnecessary part replacements and repeat truck rolls per shop. Cuts apprentice ramp time to independent service from 18-24 months to 6-9 months. Increases first-call fix rate from 72% to 90%+ across the technician roster.
