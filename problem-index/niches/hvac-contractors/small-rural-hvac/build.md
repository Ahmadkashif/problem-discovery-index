# Fuel System Comparison Calculator

**Niche:** [[niches/hvac-contractors/small-rural-hvac/profile|Small & Rural HVAC]]
**Industry:** [[industries/hvac-contractors|HVAC Contractors]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** A tool that takes a rural homeowner's current heating system (oil, propane, electric resistance, wood), local fuel prices, utility rates, home characteristics, and climate zone, and generates a side-by-side comparison of heating alternatives (heat pump, geothermal, propane, dual fuel) with 10-year total cost of ownership, available rebates, and payback periods — giving the rural HVAC contractor a data-driven advisory tool for the biggest conversation in their market.
**Tags:** #linear-regression #quick-win #revenue-impact #data-integration

## The Problem
The electrification wave is reaching rural America, and rural homeowners are asking their HVAC contractor the hardest question in the business: "Should I switch from oil/propane to a heat pump?" The answer depends on at least 10 variables: current fuel cost (propane at $2.80/gallon vs. $3.40 — a $600/year difference for a typical home), electric utility rate (varying 3x across rural service territories), climate zone (heat pumps perform differently in Vermont vs. Virginia), home insulation level (a poorly insulated home needs more BTUs regardless of fuel source), existing ductwork condition (ductless mini-splits avoid duct losses but cost more), available IRA/utility rebates ($2,000-8,000 for heat pumps depending on income level and state), geothermal feasibility (land area, soil conditions, drilling costs), current equipment age and efficiency, and the customer's comfort preferences and budget constraints. Rural HVAC contractors answer this question based on gut feel and limited mental math, often defaulting to recommending what they know best (the fuel type they've always installed) rather than what's economically optimal for the customer. There is no tool that helps them run the comparison accurately.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
Three barriers. First, the input data is complex and localized: propane prices vary by supplier and contract, electric rates vary by utility and rate structure (some rural co-ops have demand charges that make heat pumps less favorable), and rebate programs change quarterly. A calculator must be maintained with current fuel prices, utility rates, and rebate data for thousands of local markets. Second, the building science inputs — insulation level, duct condition, air sealing quality — are estimates that the HVAC contractor makes in the field, not precise measurements. The calculator must handle uncertainty in inputs and present ranges rather than false-precision single numbers. Third, the commercial model: rural HVAC shops are price-sensitive ($50-100/month for software is the ceiling), and a fuel comparison tool has to pay for itself through increased heat pump/geothermal sales or be bundled with other tools.

## What to Build
A mobile-first fuel comparison tool designed for the in-home sales conversation. The contractor enters: current system (fuel type, approximate age, estimated efficiency), home characteristics (square footage, estimated insulation quality — poor/average/good, duct condition, climate zone auto-detected from address), local fuel costs (propane price per gallon, oil price per gallon, electric rate per kWh — with defaults from local market data), and desired comparison systems (air-source heat pump, dual fuel, geothermal, new propane furnace). The tool generates a side-by-side comparison showing: installation cost (equipment + labor, localized), annual operating cost, 10-year total cost of ownership, available federal/state/utility rebates, net payback period, and monthly payment if financed. The output is a customer-facing PDF that the contractor emails or prints on-site, transforming the advisory conversation from "I think a heat pump would save you money" to "Based on your home and your propane costs, a heat pump saves you $847 per year and pays for itself in 6.2 years after rebates."

## Target Customer
Rural HVAC contractors (1-5 technicians) serving markets where natural gas is unavailable and customers are considering fuel system transitions. Also heat pump manufacturers and distributors who want to equip their dealer networks with a sales tool for the oil/propane replacement market.

## Impact If Built
Increases heat pump/geothermal close rates by 30-50% by replacing gut-feel recommendations with data-driven comparisons. Adds $50K-200K in annual equipment sales revenue per rural HVAC shop through improved advisory capability. Positions the local HVAC contractor as the trusted energy advisor in the community, defending against big-box retailer and national installer competition.
