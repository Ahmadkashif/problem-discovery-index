# Parts Sourcing for Legacy Oil/Propane Systems

**Niche:** [[niches/hvac-contractors/small-rural-hvac/profile|Small & Rural HVAC]]
**Industry:** [[industries/hvac-contractors|HVAC Contractors]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Rural HVAC technicians servicing 20-40 year old oil furnaces and propane boilers spend hours hunting for discontinued parts from obscure manufacturers — calling multiple distributors, searching eBay, and sometimes fabricating workarounds because the original part no longer exists.
**Tags:** #automation #data-integration #worker-facing

## The Problem
Rural homes often run heating equipment that urban/suburban markets have long since replaced. Oil furnaces from Beckett, Riello, and Carlin; propane boilers from Weil-McLain, Burnham, and Peerless; oil-fired water heaters from Bock and Bradford White — many of these systems are 20-40 years old and still in daily use because the rural homeowner can't afford a $8,000-15,000 system replacement. When these systems need repair, the parts are frequently discontinued. A Beckett AFG burner nozzle assembly, a Honeywell R8184G protectorelay control, or a specific Carlin EZ-1 oil burner motor may have been discontinued 10 years ago. The rural HVAC technician spends 30-90 minutes per call searching for parts: calling local supply houses (which often don't stock oil/propane parts), searching online distributors (SupplyHouse, FW Webb, PlumbersStock), checking eBay and Amazon for NOS (new old stock) parts, and identifying compatible replacements when the exact part is unavailable. This search time is unbillable — the homeowner won't pay $150/hour for the technician to sit on hold with a parts distributor.

## Why It's Still Broken
The oil and propane heating equipment market is shrinking as electrification accelerates, so distributors are reducing inventory of legacy parts rather than expanding it. Manufacturer cross-reference databases exist (Honeywell's replacement guides, Beckett's parts catalog) but they're published as PDFs or buried in manufacturer websites, not searchable from a single interface. The critical knowledge — "a Honeywell R8184G can be replaced with an R7184B with a wiring modification" — lives in the heads of experienced oil heat technicians, shared in trade forums (HeatingHelp.com, HVAC-Talk) and Facebook groups. No tool aggregates parts availability across distributors, cross-references compatible replacements, and provides the installation notes needed to make substitutions safely.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A parts sourcing and cross-reference tool for legacy heating equipment. The technician enters the part number, manufacturer, or equipment model number, and the tool: (1) searches real-time inventory across multiple distributors (SupplyHouse, FW Webb, R.E. Michel, Johnstone Supply, eBay NOS); (2) identifies compatible replacement parts with cross-reference data from manufacturer databases and community knowledge; (3) provides installation notes for replacement parts that aren't direct drop-in replacements (wiring changes, mounting adapter requirements, control modifications); (4) shows pricing comparison across available sources with estimated delivery time. The tool is mobile-optimized for use in the field — the technician identifies the failed part, searches while standing at the equipment, and orders the replacement before leaving the job site.

## Who Feels the Pain
Rural HVAC technicians who waste 30-90 minutes per legacy equipment service call searching for parts, homeowners waiting days for parts on systems that are their only heat source in winter, and shop owners absorbing unbillable search time that erodes the profitability of legacy equipment service.

## Impact If Fixed
Reduces parts sourcing time from 30-90 minutes to 5-10 minutes per call, recovering $75-200 in billable time per legacy equipment service call. Reduces "can't find the part" scenarios that force expensive full-system replacements when a $45 part would have solved the problem. Preserves the rural HVAC contractor's ability to service legacy equipment profitably as the market shrinks.
