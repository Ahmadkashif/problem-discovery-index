# Agreement Profitability Visibility

**Niche:** [[niches/hvac-contractors/maintenance-agreement-management/profile|Maintenance Agreement Management]]
**Industry:** [[industries/hvac-contractors|HVAC Contractors]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** HVAC companies sell maintenance agreements as a profit center but have no per-agreement P&L — a $299/year agreement on a 22-year-old system that requires 5 discounted repair visits may cost $600 to service, and nobody knows until the annual financials reveal that the agreement program's margins have eroded from 40% to 15% over three years.
**Tags:** #automation #data-integration #revenue-impact

## The Problem
Maintenance agreement programs are designed to be profitable: the homeowner pays $150-350/year for 2 seasonal tune-ups (cost to deliver: $80-120 each in labor and travel) plus a 10-15% repair discount and priority scheduling. On a young, well-maintained system, the math works — 2 tune-ups cost $200 to deliver, the customer pays $299, and the 15% repair discount is rarely used because the system doesn't break. On an old, failing system, the math inverts — 2 tune-ups cost $200, plus the customer uses the 15% discount on 3-5 repair visits ($500-1,500 in discounts given), plus priority scheduling means bumping a full-price customer to accommodate the agreement customer. The agreement generates $299 in revenue and $700-1,700 in total cost-to-serve. Most HVAC companies don't track this at the individual agreement level. They see: "Agreement program: 800 agreements, $220K revenue." They don't see: "153 of those 800 agreements are net-negative, costing us $65K more to service than they generate, and they're dragging the program's blended margin from 40% to 15%."

## Why It's Still Broken
Per-agreement cost-to-serve requires connecting data that lives in separate parts of the business. Agreement revenue is in the billing system. Tune-up labor cost is in the dispatch/payroll system. Repair discounts are in the invoicing system (the discount percentage is applied to the invoice, but the dollar value of the discount isn't tracked as an agreement cost). Priority scheduling displacement — the revenue lost when a full-price customer waits because an agreement customer got priority — is never calculated. ServiceTitan reports agreement revenue and can show service history per customer, but it doesn't calculate per-agreement profitability because it requires a cost model that the platform doesn't have: how much does a tune-up actually cost (technician hourly rate + drive time + truck cost), and what is the dollar value of discounts given? This is an accounting exercise that most HVAC companies' bookkeepers don't perform because the data isn't in QuickBooks — it's in ServiceTitan.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A per-agreement profitability calculator that connects ServiceTitan data (agreement fees, tune-up completions, repair invoices with discounts applied) with cost inputs (technician fully loaded hourly cost, average drive time per call, truck cost per mile) to produce a per-agreement P&L. For each agreement: revenue (annual fee), costs (tune-up labor + travel cost, discounts given on repairs, parts costs for tune-ups), and net contribution. Agreements are then color-coded: green (profitable, healthy), yellow (marginal, watch), red (unprofitable, action needed). Red agreements trigger recommendations: reprice the agreement at next renewal, move the customer to a higher-tier agreement that covers their repair frequency, or have a candid conversation about equipment replacement. The system also projects forward: "If this customer's repair frequency continues at the current rate, this agreement will cost you $800 net over the next 12 months. Replacing the system would eliminate $600/year in repair costs and convert this to a profitable agreement."

## Who Feels the Pain
HVAC company owners who built their agreement program as a profit center and watch margins erode without understanding why, office managers who process agreement renewals without knowing which ones the company should actually want to renew, and service managers who prioritize agreement customers without knowing which agreements are worth the priority.

## Impact If Fixed
Identifies 10-20% of agreements that are net-negative ($30K-150K in hidden annual losses for a 500-1,000 agreement program), enabling repricing or restructuring. Provides data-driven justification for tiered agreement pricing based on equipment age and expected cost-to-serve. Prevents margin erosion in the agreement program by surfacing unprofitable agreements before renewal rather than discovering the problem in year-end financials.
