# Commercial Installation

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/hvac-contractors|HVAC Contractors]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $30-35B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~26-30%
**Digital Adoption:** Medium — Design tools (Wrightsoft, Elite RHVAC, Trane Trace) handle load calculations. Project management is mostly spreadsheets and email. Commissioning documentation is paper-heavy. BIM adoption growing but limited to large commercial projects.
**Target Buyer:** Commercial HVAC contractor owner or project manager overseeing new construction and major retrofit projects
**Automation Potential:** High — load calculations follow engineering formulas that can be automated from building plans, commissioning is a repeatable documentation process, and equipment selection is a constrained optimization problem

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Commercial HVAC installation is a project-based business with 3-18 month timelines, not a dispatch-driven service business. A commercial HVAC contractor bids on new construction or major retrofit projects — installing rooftop units, chillers, VRF (variable refrigerant flow) systems, air handlers, and ductwork in office buildings, retail spaces, schools, and hospitals. The critical workflow is: read architectural/mechanical plans, perform Manual J (load calculation) and Manual D (duct design), select equipment, estimate labor and materials, bid the project, then execute over months with coordination against other trades (electrical, plumbing, fire protection). The engineering phase — load calculations, equipment selection, duct design — is where value is created or destroyed. Undersizing a system creates comfort complaints and warranty callbacks for years. Oversizing wastes $10K-50K in unnecessary equipment cost and increases operating costs through short-cycling. Experienced engineers develop intuition for building loads that software doesn't capture: a south-facing glass curtain wall in Phoenix needs different treatment than the same wall in Seattle, and the software handles this theoretically but experienced designers know where the software's assumptions break down.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Wrightsoft and Elite RHVAC handle Manual J/D calculations for residential-scale systems but struggle with commercial complexity — multi-zone VRF systems, chilled water plants, and central air handling units. Trane Trace and Carrier HAP are commercial-grade but require extensive manual input and don't integrate with estimating or project management. No tool takes a set of commercial building plans (PDF or CAD) and automatically extracts the inputs needed for load calculation: wall areas, window areas and orientations, ceiling heights, occupancy types, lighting loads, and equipment heat loads. This extraction is done manually by engineers, taking 20-40 hours per project, and errors in extraction cascade into wrong load calculations and wrong equipment selections.
