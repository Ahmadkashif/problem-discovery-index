## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Churches depend on volunteers more than any other organizational type — a typical 300-member church has 80-120 active volunteers filling 15-25 roles every Sunday (greeters, ushers, nursery, children's ministry, worship team, sound/video, parking, coffee, prayer team, communion prep). Scheduling these volunteers weekly, respecting their availability, managing swaps and no-shows, and filling gaps requires significant coordination. Unlike paid staff scheduling (where you can mandate shifts), volunteer scheduling must accommodate preferences, avoid burnout, and maintain engagement — you can't fire a volunteer, and if you over-schedule them, they quit.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Planning Center Services provides robust volunteer scheduling with auto-scheduling, conflict detection, and mobile confirmations — but it's used by only 40% of churches (mostly mid-to-large). Smaller churches use email, text chains, or the Sunday bulletin to recruit volunteers week by week. Even churches using Planning Center struggle with the "last-mile" problems: finding substitutes when someone cancels Friday night, onboarding new volunteers into the scheduling system, and balancing load across experienced and new volunteers.
