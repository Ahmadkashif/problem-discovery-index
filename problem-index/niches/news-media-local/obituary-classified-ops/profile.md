# Obituary & Classified Operations

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/news-media-local|Local News Media]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $2.1B
**Share of Parent Industry:** 5%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Obituaries are typically received via email or phone, manually reformatted, manually proofed against funeral home records, and manually posted to both print and web — the most labor-intensive per-unit revenue process in local news.
**Target Buyer:** Classified/Obituary Desk Manager / Office Manager
**Automation Potential:** High — Obituary intake, formatting, proofing, billing, and cross-platform publishing follow rigid rules with minimal editorial judgment required.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Obituaries and classifieds are the last reliable revenue streams for many community papers — obituaries especially, because they're driven by deaths (recession-proof demand) and families will pay premium rates ($200-800 per obituary). But the operational workflow is shockingly manual: funeral homes call or email obituary text, the classified desk reformats it to match the paper's style, proofs for errors (misspelled names are career-ending mistakes), calculates billing based on word count and add-ons (photos, color borders, web inclusion), and publishes to both print and the paper's website. Classified ads (garage sales, help wanted, legal notices) follow similar intake-format-bill-publish patterns. A single obituary can take 30-45 minutes of staff time.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Legacy classified systems (Naviga, SCS) handle the billing side but not the intake or formatting. Most obituary intake is done through a general-purpose email inbox. Some papers use Legacy.com or Tributes.com for web-only obituary publishing, but these don't integrate with the print workflow. No end-to-end system handles the full obituary lifecycle: intake from funeral homes, formatting to print style, family review/approval, print pagination placement, web publishing, billing, and archive.
