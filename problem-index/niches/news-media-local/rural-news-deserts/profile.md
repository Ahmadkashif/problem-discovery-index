# Rural News Deserts

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/news-media-local|Local News Media]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1.2B
**Share of Parent Industry:** 3%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Many communities that lost their newspaper have no local digital news presence; those that do often rely on a single volunteer-run Facebook page or a regional paper's sparse county coverage.
**Target Buyer:** Civic Publisher / Nonprofit Executive Director / Community Foundation Program Officer
**Automation Potential:** Medium-High — Government meeting coverage, public records aggregation, and community event calendars are highly automatable and represent the most critical information gaps.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Over 1,800 US communities have lost their local newspaper since 2005, creating "news deserts" — areas with no dedicated local news coverage. These are predominantly rural counties where the economics of traditional journalism (ad sales to local businesses, print distribution over long distances) never penciled out at scale. The consequence is civic invisibility: no coverage of county commission meetings, school board decisions, court proceedings, or local business developments. New models are emerging — nonprofit news startups, community foundation-funded reporters, university journalism partnerships — but they face the chicken-and-egg problem of generating content with minimal staff while simultaneously building an audience from zero.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Emerging rural news operations typically use free or low-cost tools: WordPress, Mailchimp free tier, Facebook for distribution, Google Docs for collaboration. They have no budget for paid CMS, analytics, or audience development tools. The critical gap is not better tools but lower-effort content creation: these operations need ways to cover essential civic information (meeting agendas, public records, election filings) without requiring a reporter to attend every meeting in person.
