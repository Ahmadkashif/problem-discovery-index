**Parent Industry:** [[industries/video-production-smb|Video Production SMB]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $500M
**Share of Parent Industry:** 4%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Court reporting technology has evolved but legal video remains surprisingly analog in its workflow, with tapes and DVDs still common for evidence delivery.
**Target Buyer:** Owner / Court Videographer
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Transcript synchronization, evidence compilation, and deposition indexing are rule-driven; court procedures require human judgment.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Legal video specialists record depositions, day-in-the-life videos, settlement documentaries, and trial presentations. This is a compliance-heavy niche where chain-of-custody, timestamp integrity, and procedural correctness determine whether footage is admissible as evidence. A deposition videographer must follow specific protocols: recording the opening/closing statements, maintaining continuous timecode, capturing clear audio of all parties, and producing a synchronized transcript-video file. The client (law firm) may not view the footage for months or years, then need instant access for trial preparation. Pricing is premium ($500–$2,000 per deposition) but volume is limited by the legal calendar.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Recording uses professional cameras with timecode generators. OnCue and TrialDirector handle trial presentation. Veritext and Huseby provide deposition services at scale. But small independent legal videographers (500–1,000 in the U.S.) manage their workflow with paper logs, DVDs/USB drives for delivery, and personal hard drives for storage. No tool purpose-built for small legal video operations handles: deposition scheduling with attorney coordination, chain-of-custody documentation, transcript-video synchronization, evidence compilation for trial teams, or long-term archive management for footage that may be needed 5+ years later.
