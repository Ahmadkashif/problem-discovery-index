# DEI & Culture Training Programs

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/corporate-training|Corporate Training]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $3-5B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~3-5%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — most DEI training is delivered as live facilitated workshops or off-the-shelf e-learning (Traliant, Emtrain), with near-zero measurement of whether training changes attitudes, behaviors, or organizational outcomes
**Target Buyer:** Chief People Officer or DEI Director at mid-to-large enterprises
**Automation Potential:** Low-Medium — the sensitive nature of DEI content resists full automation, but measurement, follow-up, and behavior-change tracking are highly automatable

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
DEI training occupies a politically sensitive position in the corporate training landscape: it is often legally mandated (sexual harassment training in CA, NY, IL), strategically important to leadership (employer brand, talent retention), and deeply personal for participants. Yet it is the training domain with the weakest measurement infrastructure. Companies spend $50,000-$500,000 annually on DEI programs and can report only two data points: completion rates and post-session satisfaction scores. Whether the training actually changed workplace behaviors, reduced bias in hiring decisions, improved retention of underrepresented employees, or shifted cultural survey scores is unmeasured.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Emtrain, Traliant, and Kantola provide DEI e-learning libraries. Paradigm and include offer facilitated DEI workshops and consulting. Culture Amp and Glint provide employee engagement surveys that can track DEI-related sentiment over time. The gap is the connection between DEI training interventions and measurable behavioral/organizational outcomes — no platform ties training completion to downstream metrics like hiring diversity ratios, promotion equity, retention differentials, or bias-related complaint rates.
