# Manufacturing Floor Skills Training

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/corporate-training|Corporate Training]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $8-12B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~8-12%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — manufacturing floor workers train through buddy-pairing, tribal knowledge transfer, and paper-based SOPs; digital training tools designed for desk workers are inaccessible and irrelevant to hands-on skill development
**Target Buyer:** Plant Manager or Training Coordinator at discrete/process manufacturing facilities with 100-1,000 floor workers
**Automation Potential:** Medium — hands-on skills resist digitization, but knowledge assessment, SOP access, and competency tracking are highly automatable

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Manufacturing floor training is fundamentally different from corporate desk-based training: the skills are physical and procedural (operating a CNC machine, performing quality inspections, executing lockout/tagout procedures), the learning environment is noisy and equipment-centric, workers may not have literacy levels suitable for text-based training, and the consequences of inadequate training are immediate and physical (injury, scrap, equipment damage). Current corporate LMS platforms assume a literate, desk-based learner with 30-60 minutes of uninterrupted time — none of which applies to a floor worker running a press brake.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Poka and SwipeGuide provide visual work instruction platforms for manufacturing — digitizing SOPs with photos and videos accessible on tablets at workstations. Dozuki offers step-by-step visual guides. These address the SOP access problem but do not solve the skills assessment challenge: how do you verify that a machine operator can actually set up a 5-axis CNC correctly, not just watch a video about it? Skills matrices are still maintained in spreadsheets by production supervisors.
