# Long-Term Care Pharmacy

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/pharmacy-independents|Independent Pharmacies]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1-1.5B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~12%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — LTC-specific software (FrameworkLTC, QS/1 LTC module) handles dispensing and billing but cycle fill optimization, facility coordination, blister pack production scheduling, and consultant pharmacist review workflows rely on manual processes, paper communication, and phone/fax
**Target Buyer:** LTC pharmacy owner serving 5-20 nursing homes and assisted living facilities, managing census-driven demand that changes daily as residents are admitted, discharged, hospitalized, or pass away
**Automation Potential:** High — blister packaging production scheduling, medication pass coordination with facility nursing staff, and consultant pharmacist medication reviews are labor-intensive processes with strong pattern-based optimization potential

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Long-term care pharmacies serve institutional clients — nursing homes, assisted living facilities, memory care units — rather than walk-in patients. The workflow is fundamentally different from retail: medications are packaged in blister cards or strip packaging organized by administration time (8am meds, 12pm meds, 6pm meds, 9pm meds) so nursing staff can perform medication passes efficiently. Orders come from facility physicians and nurse practitioners, not patients. The pharmacy must track each resident's medication regimen, produce cycle fills (typically monthly or biweekly), handle emergency medication orders ("stat" deliveries when a new admission arrives at 2am), manage narcotic reconciliation with facility records, and provide consultant pharmacist medication regimen reviews as mandated by CMS for Medicare-certified facilities. Census changes daily — a 100-bed facility might have 3-5 admissions and discharges per week — requiring constant production schedule adjustments.

## Current Tools & Gaps
FrameworkLTC and QS/1's LTC module handle dispensing, billing, and basic cycle fill scheduling. These systems track resident medication profiles and generate blister card labels, but production scheduling — sequencing which facilities' blister cards are produced on which days, optimizing packaging machine changeovers, and adjusting for census changes — is done manually by the production manager using whiteboards and spreadsheets. Facility coordination happens via fax and phone: new orders faxed from the facility, delivery confirmations called in, medication discrepancy reports mailed. Consultant pharmacist reviews are documented in separate systems or paper forms. The gap is production-level workflow optimization and digital facility coordination.
