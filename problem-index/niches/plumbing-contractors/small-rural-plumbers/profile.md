# Small & Rural Plumbers

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/plumbing-contractors|Plumbing Contractors]]
**Category:** Underserved

## Profile
**Market Size:** $8-10B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~6%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — most 1-2 person shops use paper invoices, a personal phone for scheduling, and QuickBooks for bookkeeping; FSM tools are either too expensive (ServiceTitan) or too generic (Jobber doesn't know what a septic system is)
**Target Buyer:** Owner-operator plumber running a 1-2 person shop serving a rural county or small town, typically covering a 30-60 mile radius
**Automation Potential:** Medium — the plumber does everything (answer phone, drive, diagnose, repair, invoice, bookkeep), so any tool that saves 30 minutes/day has outsized impact, but the diverse job mix and poor cell coverage in rural areas constrain digital solutions

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Rural plumbing is generalist plumbing — the same plumber who replaces a kitchen faucet in the morning installs a well pump at noon, repairs a septic tank baffle in the afternoon, and runs a gas line to a barn heater before dark. There's no specialization because there aren't enough people to specialize. The customer base is spread across a wide geographic area with 30-60 minute drives between jobs. Parts availability is limited — the nearest supply house may be 45 minutes away, so the truck must be stocked for anything. The plumber also handles work that urban plumbers rarely touch: well pump systems (submersible pumps, pressure tanks, well seals), septic systems (inspection, repair, pumping coordination), agricultural plumbing (livestock watering systems, dairy barn plumbing, irrigation), and off-grid water systems. Code enforcement varies dramatically — some rural jurisdictions have no inspections, others follow IPC/UPC strictly. The business challenge is that these plumbers can't afford $300/month FSM software, have no office staff, and need tools that work without reliable cell service.

## Current Tools & Gaps
ServiceTitan starts at $300/month per tech and requires a dedicated office person — overkill and overpriced for a solo plumber. Jobber ($50-100/month) handles scheduling and invoicing but has no plumbing-specific features — it doesn't know well pump sizing, septic system terminology, or rural code variations. Most rural plumbers use a paper calendar, handwritten invoices (or a free Invoice2go account), and QuickBooks Self-Employed. Reference materials for well pumps, septic design, and agricultural plumbing are scattered across manufacturer manuals, state health department guidelines, and plumbing code books — no mobile-friendly consolidated reference exists.
