# Residential Service & Repair

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/plumbing-contractors|Plumbing Contractors]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $45-50B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~35%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — most mid-size shops run ServiceTitan or Housecall Pro for dispatching and invoicing, but smaller operators still use paper tickets and QuickBooks; analytics, technician performance tracking, and inventory optimization are rarely touched
**Target Buyer:** Residential plumbing service company owner running 3-15 trucks who handles dispatching, pricing, and hiring while still occasionally running calls
**Automation Potential:** High — dispatching is done by gut feel, truck inventory is untracked, and emergency call prioritization is manual

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Residential service plumbing is the emergency medicine of the trades — the phone rings because a pipe burst at 2 AM, a water heater died on a Sunday morning, or a toilet is overflowing during Thanksgiving dinner. Roughly 60% of revenue comes from unplanned emergency and urgent calls, with the remainder split between scheduled repairs (faucet replacements, garbage disposal installs) and upsell conversions (a drain cleaning call that becomes a full repipe). The business model depends on three things: answering the phone before the homeowner calls the next company, dispatching the right plumber with the right parts fast enough to capture the job, and converting a $200 service call into a $2,000-8,000 sale (water heater replacement, whole-house repipe, sewer line replacement). ServiceTitan dominates the mid-market but its dispatching relies on a human dispatcher matching technicians to calls by memory and availability — no severity scoring, no skills matching, no parts availability check.

## Current Tools & Gaps
ServiceTitan ($300-500/month per tech) handles scheduling, dispatching, flat-rate pricing, invoicing, and basic reporting. FieldEdge and Housecall Pro serve the lower end. All three require dispatchers to manually match incoming calls to available technicians, with no consideration of truck inventory (does the tech have a 50-gallon gas water heater on the truck?), technician specialty (who is best at tankless installs?), or route efficiency (which tech is closest to the call?). Truck inventory is completely untracked — plumbers drive back to the supply house 1-3 times per day because they don't have the right fitting, wasting 45-90 minutes per tech per day. No FSM platform offers truck-level parts inventory management or predictive restocking based on call type patterns.
