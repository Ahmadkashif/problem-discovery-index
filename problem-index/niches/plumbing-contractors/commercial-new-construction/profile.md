# Commercial New Construction Plumbing

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/plumbing-contractors|Plumbing Contractors]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $35-40B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~28%
**Digital Adoption:** Medium — larger firms use Procore or PlanGrid for project management and AutoCAD/Revit for design, but pipe sizing calculations, spec compliance tracking, and as-built documentation remain manual or semi-manual
**Target Buyer:** Commercial plumbing contractor owner or project manager bidding plan-and-spec projects for hospitals, schools, multi-story office buildings, and retail developments
**Automation Potential:** Medium — plan takeoff and pipe sizing involve codified engineering rules (IPC/UPC fixture unit calculations) well-suited to automation, but spec compliance and coordination with other trades require human judgment

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Commercial new construction plumbing is an engineering-driven, bid-driven business fundamentally different from residential service. Projects range from $200K tenant improvements to $15M hospital plumbing packages. The workflow is: receive architectural/mechanical plans, perform pipe sizing calculations per International Plumbing Code (IPC) or Uniform Plumbing Code (UPC), produce shop drawings and isometric diagrams, submit bid with labor and material estimates, and — if awarded — manage a 6-24 month installation with 3-50 plumbers on site coordinating with HVAC, electrical, and fire protection trades. Revenue is project-based with 90-120 day payment cycles. Labor is often union with strict jurisdictional rules. The key technical differentiator from residential: DWV (drain-waste-vent) systems in multi-story buildings require complex pipe sizing calculations accounting for fixture units, building drainage loads, vent stack sizing, and code-mandated clearances that vary by jurisdiction. Getting pipe sizing wrong means failed inspections and expensive rework.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Revit MEP and AutoCAD MEP handle plumbing design but require trained drafters ($60-90K/year) and 40-120 hours per project for shop drawings. Procore and PlanGrid manage project documents and RFIs but have no plumbing-specific intelligence. Pipe sizing is done manually using IPC/UPC fixture unit tables in spreadsheets — a calculation that is entirely rule-based and automatable but hasn't been productized for plumbing contractors (mechanical engineers have MEP calculation software, but it's priced and designed for engineering firms, not contractors). Specification compliance — tracking which pipe materials, fitting types, insulation requirements, and backflow prevention devices are required per project — is managed via highlighted spec books and manual checklists, with errors caught only at inspection.
