# AI Sewer Camera Footage Analyzer with Real-Time Defect Detection

## The Problem
Sewer camera inspection generates 15-60 minutes of video per job, and every frame requires interpretation. An experienced sewer technician performing a camera push simultaneously controls the camera, reads pipe condition in real time, identifies defects, estimates severity, and mentally composes a repair recommendation — all while the homeowner watches over their shoulder. This is the highest-concentration tacit knowledge task in plumbing. The experienced tech sees a subtle change in pipe color and knows it's a developing fracture. They see roots entering at a joint and judge — by root type, density, and penetration depth — whether the joint needs a spot repair or the entire section needs replacement. They read the pipe's reaction to the camera push (does it flex? is there standing water indicating a belly?) and integrate multiple observations into a single diagnosis. A junior tech with the same camera sees pipe and says "looks fine" or "there are roots" without the granular assessment that drives accurate repair scoping.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
The data collection challenge is severe: training a CNN to identify sewer pipe defects requires thousands of labeled video frames from real inspections, annotated by experienced technicians who can accurately classify and grade each defect. Municipal pipeline assessment has standardized defect coding (NASSCO PACP), but residential sewer laterals (3-6" diameter) have different defect presentations, materials (cast iron, clay, Orangeburg, ABS, PVC), and severity implications than municipal mains. Camera footage quality varies enormously — lighting, lens cleanliness, water level, and push speed all affect visibility. The labeling challenge is real: two experienced technicians looking at the same footage may disagree on severity grading (is this root intrusion "moderate" or "severe"?), requiring consensus labeling protocols. Deployment requires real-time inference on a tablet or phone at the job site, often without reliable internet connectivity, which constrains model size.

## What to Build
A computer vision system that processes sewer camera footage (live stream or recorded video) and produces: (1) real-time defect detection with bounding boxes or segmentation masks identifying root intrusion, offset joints, cracks/fractures, bellied sections, grease/scale buildup, pipe material transitions, and collapsed sections, (2) severity scoring (1-5 scale) for each detected defect calibrated to repair urgency, (3) timestamped defect log with frame captures, (4) automated repair recommendation based on defect type, severity, location, and pipe material (cleaning vs. spot repair vs. lining vs. excavation). The CNN backbone is trained on a dataset of 50,000+ labeled sewer camera frames from residential and commercial laterals, with transfer learning from municipal pipeline inspection datasets (NASSCO-labeled data). Deployed as a tablet app that connects to the camera system via USB or WiFi, with on-device inference for offline capability. Revenue model: $199-399/month per camera system, positioned as a "second expert" that lets any technician perform expert-level inspections.

## Target Customer
Drain and sewer companies running 2-10 camera crews, struggling to hire experienced camera operators, where the accuracy of camera interpretation directly determines whether a $300 cleaning or a $15,000 excavation is recommended — and getting it wrong means either leaving money on the table or losing the customer's trust.

## Impact If Built
Enables junior technicians to perform expert-level sewer inspections from day one, addressing the skilled labor shortage in drain/sewer work. Increases inspection accuracy and consistency, reducing under-diagnosis (missed defects that lead to callbacks) and over-diagnosis (recommending excavation when lining would suffice). For a 5-crew operation performing 15-25 camera inspections per day, even a 10% improvement in repair conversion rate from better diagnosis adds $150K-300K in annual revenue.
