## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Leak detection is plumbing's detective work. A homeowner reports high water bills, water stains on a ceiling, or a warm spot on a slab floor. The leak detection specialist must find the leak location without tearing apart walls or jackhammering floors — using acoustic listening equipment (to hear water escaping under a slab), thermal imaging cameras (to see temperature differentials from leaking hot water lines), pressure testing (to isolate which pipe section is leaking), and visual inspection (condensation patterns, foundation cracks indicating long-term water intrusion, discoloration patterns on drywall). An experienced leak detection plumber integrates these signals in real time: "The acoustic signature is strongest here, the thermal image shows a heat plume tracking east, the foundation has a hairline crack at 45 degrees — the hot water line under the slab has a pinhole leak at approximately this point." A junior plumber with the same equipment hears water sound everywhere, can't distinguish supply leak from drain leak, and ends up cutting test holes in the wrong locations.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Acoustic listening equipment (TMIC, Vivax-Metrotech) amplifies underground water sounds but provides raw audio — the plumber interprets what they hear. Thermal imaging cameras (FLIR, Milwaukee) show temperature differentials but the plumber decides what the pattern means. Pressure testing equipment isolates leaking line sections but the plumber designs the test sequence. Tracer gas systems (hydrogen-nitrogen mix) can locate leaks in hard-to-reach areas but require experience to interpret detector readings. No product combines these data streams into a diagnostic decision support system. The interpretation layer is entirely human, entirely tacit, and entirely dependent on individual experience.
