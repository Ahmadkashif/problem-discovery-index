# Non-English Homebuyers

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/home-inspection|Home Inspection]]
**Category:** Underserved

## Profile
**Market Size:** $0.2-0.3B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~5%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — no inspection platform offers multilingual report generation. Inspectors serving non-English speakers either hire interpreters, use bilingual staff, or rely on the buyer's agent to translate verbally.
**Target Buyer:** Home inspector serving markets with significant non-English-speaking buyer populations (Hispanic, Chinese, Korean, Vietnamese, Russian communities)
**Automation Potential:** High — report translation is a well-understood NLP task, and inspection terminology has a finite, domain-specific vocabulary that can be accurately translated with specialized models

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Twenty-two percent of US homebuyers speak a language other than English at home, and 8-10% have limited English proficiency. These buyers go through the same inspection process but receive English-only reports they cannot read. The inspection walkthrough — where the inspector explains findings verbally — is the buyer's primary understanding of their future home's condition. Without effective communication, the buyer either (1) relies entirely on their agent's interpretation (agents have financial incentive to minimize concerns), (2) brings a family member to translate (who lacks technical vocabulary — "the flashing is compromised" doesn't translate directly), or (3) nods along without understanding. The consequence: non-English-speaking buyers are more likely to purchase homes with undisclosed or misunderstood defects because they couldn't engage with the inspection findings.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Spectora, HomeGauge, and Home Inspector Pro generate reports exclusively in English. Google Translate can translate individual sentences but produces unreliable results with technical inspection terminology ("galvanized supply lines with active corrosion" becomes nonsensical in machine translation). No inspection platform offers professionally translated templates. Bilingual inspectors exist but are rare — fewer than 5% of inspectors speak Spanish, and virtually none speak Chinese, Korean, or Vietnamese.
