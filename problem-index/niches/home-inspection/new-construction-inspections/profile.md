# New Construction Inspections

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/home-inspection|Home Inspection]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $0.3-0.5B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~7%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — new construction phase inspections use paper checklists or generic notes apps. Residential inspection software is designed for existing homes, not construction-in-progress. No tool handles multi-phase inspection tracking or builder punch list management.
**Target Buyer:** Home inspector offering new construction phase inspections as an add-on service, or buyer's inspection agent specializing in new builds
**Automation Potential:** Medium — code compliance checklists vary by jurisdiction but are structured and automatable; punch list tracking is a workflow problem with well-understood patterns

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
New construction inspections differ fundamentally from pre-purchase inspections of existing homes. Instead of evaluating existing conditions, the inspector verifies that construction meets building code and manufacturer specifications at critical stages: pre-pour/pre-slab (rebar placement, vapor barrier, plumbing rough-in), framing (structural connections, fire blocking, window flashing), and final/pre-drywall (insulation, HVAC duct sealing, electrical rough-in). Each phase has a different checklist governed by the applicable building code (IRC/IBC) and local amendments. The inspector must catch defects before they're covered up — a missing fire block behind drywall is invisible after completion, and an improperly flashed window that leaks three years later will cost the homeowner $5,000-15,000 to repair. The relationship dynamic is also different: the buyer is the client but the builder is the party whose work is being evaluated, creating tension that doesn't exist in resale inspections.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Spectora and HomeGauge are designed for existing-home inspections — their checklists, defect categories, and report formats don't apply to construction-phase evaluations. Inspectors performing new construction work typically use paper checklists, generic photos in a notes app, and custom Word documents. No tool maintains the multi-phase continuity needed for new construction: tracking that the framing defect identified in Phase 2 was corrected before the Phase 3 inspection, or that the plumbing rough-in in Phase 1 used the materials specified. Builder punch list tracking (items the builder agreed to fix) is managed via email and text messages with no structured follow-up.
