# Home-Based PT Providers

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/physical-therapy|Physical Therapy]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $4-6B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~8%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — most home-based PTs use paper forms or basic mobile documentation; standard PT EMRs are designed for clinic-based workflows
**Target Buyer:** Home health agency PT director or independent PT contractor
**Automation Potential:** High — route optimization, home environment assessment standardization, and homebound status documentation are all structured problems with clear automation paths

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
PTs performing home visits for patients who cannot travel to a clinic — post-surgical elderly patients, homebound individuals with mobility limitations, and patients recovering from hip/knee replacements who aren't yet safe to drive. The clinical environment is radically different from a clinic: limited equipment (resistance bands, portable tools, no gym), patient home environment assessment is part of the job (fall hazards, bathroom accessibility, stair navigation), and travel time between patients consumes 30-40% of the workday. Documentation must justify homebound status for Medicare coverage — a clinical-legal requirement that doesn't exist in outpatient settings. Scheduling is a geographic optimization problem, not just a calendar problem.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Standard PT EMRs (WebPT, Clinicient) are designed for clinic-based sessions with stable internet, multiple treatment rooms, and consistent equipment. They lack: offline-capable mobile documentation, GPS-based mileage tracking for reimbursement, home environment assessment templates, homebound status documentation support, and geographic patient clustering for route planning. Home health-specific EMRs (Axxess, WellSky) are designed for nursing agencies and poorly adapted to PT-specific documentation needs like outcome measures and treatment plan progression.
