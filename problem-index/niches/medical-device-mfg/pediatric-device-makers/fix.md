# Pediatric Clinical Evidence Gap

**Niche:** [[niches/medical-device-mfg/pediatric-device-makers/profile|Pediatric Device Manufacturers]]
**Industry:** [[industries/medical-device-mfg|Medical Device Manufacturing]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Building clinical evidence for pediatric devices — where randomized controlled trials are often ethically impractical and patient populations are tiny — requires alternative evidence strategies (registries, case series, computational modeling) that no platform helps manufacturers plan, execute, or compile.
**Tags:** #compliance #data-integration #revenue-impact

## The Problem
FDA requires clinical evidence of safety and effectiveness for pediatric devices, but the standard clinical trial model breaks down: patient populations are small (sometimes fewer than 100 patients nationally for rare pediatric conditions), randomization with sham/control arms raises ethical concerns in children, growth and development create long-term endpoints that adult trials don't face, and parental consent dynamics differ from adult informed consent. Manufacturers must assemble evidence from heterogeneous sources — small case series, registry data, computational modeling, adult extrapolation studies — and present it as a coherent evidence package. This evidence assembly is ad hoc, with each company reinventing the approach.

## Why It's Still Broken
The pediatric device evidence landscape is fragmented. Patient registries (e.g., STS Congenital Heart Surgery Database, POSNA pediatric orthopedic registry) exist but are not designed for manufacturer evidence collection. Computational modeling for pediatric devices is emerging but not yet accepted as primary evidence by FDA. No platform connects manufacturers to relevant registries, helps design alternative evidence strategies, or compiles multi-source evidence into the format FDA reviewers expect for pediatric submissions.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A clinical evidence planning platform for pediatric devices that: (1) maps the manufacturer's device to relevant pediatric registries and case series repositories, (2) helps design an alternative evidence strategy (real-world evidence from registries + computational modeling + adult extrapolation, weighted by FDA's stated preferences for that device type), (3) provides templates for pediatric-specific sections of regulatory submissions (growth impact analysis, long-term follow-up plans, ethical justification for study design), and (4) tracks evidence accumulation against submission requirements with gap analysis.

## Who Feels the Pain
Clinical affairs directors and regulatory professionals at pediatric device companies who spend months designing evidence strategies from scratch. Clinicians who use adult devices off-label in children because no pediatric version has cleared the evidence hurdle. Patients — children who lack access to appropriately sized, designed, and tested medical devices.

## Impact If Fixed
Evidence strategy planning time drops from 6-12 months to 6-12 weeks. More pediatric devices complete the regulatory pathway — directly addressing the 10,000+ children per year who undergo procedures with devices not designed or tested for their anatomy. Time-to-market for pediatric devices decreases by 6-18 months.
