# Instructor Recruitment from Industry

**Niche:** [[niches/vocational-schools/rural-trade-schools/profile|Rural Trade Schools]]
**Industry:** [[industries/vocational-schools|Vocational Schools]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Rural trade schools cannot hire instructors because qualified tradespeople earn more in the field than in the classroom — and no pathway exists to convert experienced practitioners into part-time teachers without full career switches.
**Tags:** #worker-facing #revenue-impact #automation

## The Problem
A master electrician in a rural area earns $70,000-$90,000 working for a contractor. The local trade school offers $40,000-$55,000 for a full-time instructor position. The school can't fill the position. Meanwhile, several experienced tradespeople in the community might teach 1-2 evenings per week if the opportunity were structured as supplemental income rather than a career change. But the school's accreditation requires documented instructor qualifications, the scheduling is rigid, and there's no system for managing a roster of part-time industry professionals as adjunct instructors.

## Why It's Still Broken
Accreditation requirements (ACCSC, COE) mandate specific instructor credentials — typically a combination of trade experience, certifications, and instructional training hours. Converting a working tradesperson into a qualified instructor requires documenting their industry experience, arranging instructional methodology training, and managing an ongoing compliance record. Rural schools lack the HR infrastructure to manage this pipeline. The tradesperson-to-instructor pathway doesn't exist as a structured process; it depends on the school director personally knowing and convincing individual practitioners.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A structured adjunct instructor recruitment and qualification pipeline: (1) outreach to local trade professionals through employer partners, trade associations, and certification body databases; (2) a credentialing pathway that maps industry experience and certifications to accreditor instructor requirements, identifies gaps, and provides the minimum instructional training needed; (3) flexible scheduling templates allowing 4-8 hour/week teaching commitments alongside full-time industry employment; (4) ongoing compliance tracking to maintain accreditor documentation. The system makes becoming a part-time instructor a simple, structured process rather than an ad-hoc personal favor.

## Who Feels the Pain
School directors who cannot find qualified instructors and must either teach all sections themselves (burning out), limit program enrollment (losing revenue), or hire under-qualified instructors (risking accreditation).

## Impact If Fixed
Expands the available instructor pool by 3-5x by tapping part-time industry practitioners. Enables program enrollment growth of 20-40% at schools currently constrained by instructor availability. Improves instructional quality by bringing current industry practitioners into the classroom alongside full-time faculty.
