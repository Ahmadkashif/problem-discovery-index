# Welding & Skilled Trades Programs

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/vocational-schools|Vocational Schools]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $5-6B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~28%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Most programs use paper-based competency rubrics, manual attendance tracking, and spreadsheet-driven placement records; AWS certifications are tested by human inspectors with no digital capture of student weld quality progression
**Target Buyer:** Program director or school owner at private welding/trades schools (Lincoln Tech, UTI, independent schools)
**Automation Potential:** High — Visual weld inspection, competency tracking across multi-position certification paths, and employer matching all follow structured evaluation criteria

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Welding and skilled trades programs (welding, HVAC, electrical, plumbing) represent the largest single category of vocational enrollment. These programs have uniquely strong visual/physical assessment requirements — a welding student's competency is evaluated by inspecting the finished bead, testing joint integrity, and observing technique during execution. This makes them the highest-value target for computer vision and tacit knowledge ML. The student pipeline is also distinctive: many enrollees are career changers in their 20s-30s, not recent high school graduates, creating different retention dynamics and employer matching needs compared to other vocational segments.

## Current Tools & Gaps
AWS (American Welding Society) certification testing is standardized but manual — human inspectors evaluate weld coupons visually and with destructive testing. Training-phase assessment is entirely instructor-dependent with no consistency across evaluators. SIS platforms (Campus Cafe, SONIS) track enrollment but have no competency module for multi-position welding certification progression (1G through 6G, multiple processes like SMAW/GMAW/GTAW).
