# Asset Management Lite for Small-Town Infrastructure

**Niche:** [[niches/engineering-consultants/municipal-infrastructure-consulting/profile|Municipal Infrastructure Consulting]]
**Industry:** [[industries/engineering-consultants|Engineering Consultants]]
**Type:** Buy & Customize (Vertical Adaptation)
**One-liner:** Full-scale asset management platforms (Cartegraph, Lucity) exist but are designed for cities with $100K+ IT budgets — small towns need a $200/month version that tracks pipe age, road condition ratings, and remaining useful life without a GIS department.
**Tags:** #data-integration #automation #time-series-forecasting #tabular-ml

## The Problem
Small municipalities own $50-500M in infrastructure assets (roads, water mains, sewer lines, bridges, stormwater systems) but have no systematic inventory or condition tracking. The public works director knows the worst roads and the oldest pipes from experience, but there's no written record of installation dates, materials, condition assessments, or replacement timelines. When the consulting engineer needs to prioritize projects for a CIP or grant application, they start from scratch — driving the town, reviewing GIS data (if it exists), and interviewing the DPW crew. This assessment takes 40-80 hours per engagement.

## What Already Exists
Cartegraph ($15,000-50,000/year), Lucity (now Brightly, similar pricing), and CityWorks are full-featured asset management platforms used by cities with populations over 50,000. They integrate with GIS, support mobile inspection workflows, and model asset lifecycle costs. For smaller municipalities, these platforms are prohibitively expensive, require GIS infrastructure that doesn't exist, and demand dedicated staff to maintain.

## The Customization Gap
Small towns need a drastically simplified version: a spreadsheet-like interface (not a GIS-centric platform) that tracks asset type, location (address or intersection, not geodatabase coordinates), installation year, material, last inspection date, condition rating (1-5 scale), and estimated replacement cost. The system calculates remaining useful life based on standard asset life expectancy tables (AWWA for water, ASCE for roads) and generates a prioritized replacement schedule. The consulting engineer populates the database during their annual assessment and the municipality's DPW director updates condition ratings after inspections. No GIS required. No IT department required.

## Target Customer
Municipal consulting engineers who need a structured way to inventory and prioritize infrastructure for their small-town clients, and DPW directors who need to justify capital spending to elected officials with data instead of anecdotes.

## Impact If Solved
Replaces the 40-80 hour baseline assessment with a persistent database that the consulting engineer updates incrementally. Gives small municipalities data-driven capital planning for the first time, improving their competitiveness for grant funding (grant applications that include condition assessment data score significantly higher).
