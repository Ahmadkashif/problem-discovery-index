# Project Budget Forecasting & Change Orders

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/engineering-consultants|Engineering Consultants]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $5-7B (embedded in firm overhead — represents the margin erosion that better forecasting would prevent)
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~2% (as a cost center / revenue leakage problem)
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — firms track hours in timesheets and fees in proposals but rarely connect the two in real time
**Target Buyer:** Project manager or firm principal at any engineering firm running fixed-fee projects
**Automation Potential:** High — budget burn rate monitoring, scope creep detection, and change order generation follow deterministic rules but are performed manually or not at all

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Every engineering firm — regardless of discipline, size, or geography — faces the same financial management challenge: they sell fixed-fee services but deliver them with variable-cost labor. The gap between proposal estimate and actual cost is the primary determinant of firm profitability, yet most firms don't know whether a project is on budget until the final invoice. Project managers are supposed to monitor budget burn, file change orders for out-of-scope work, and adjust staffing when projects run hot — but they lack real-time data, the tools to predict overruns before they happen, and standardized change order workflows. This is not a design problem or a discipline-specific problem — it is a universal business operations problem that cuts across all engineering consulting niches.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Deltek Vantagepoint, Ajera, and BST Global all provide budget-vs-actual reporting, but the reports are backward-looking, require clean timesheet data (which small firms lack), and are buried in ERP interfaces that project managers rarely access. No tool predicts budget outcomes based on burn rate trajectory and historical comparables. Change order generation is manual — the PM writes a letter to the client, often weeks after the scope expansion occurred, without a standardized calculation of the additional fee.
