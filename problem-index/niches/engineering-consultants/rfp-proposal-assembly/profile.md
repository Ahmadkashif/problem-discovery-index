# RFP/Proposal Assembly Workflows

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/engineering-consultants|Engineering Consultants]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $6-8B (embedded in firm overhead)
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~2% (as a cost center consuming 5-10% of firm revenue)
**Digital Adoption:** Low — most firms assemble proposals in Word/InDesign from shared drives of past proposals, with no structured content repository or reuse system
**Target Buyer:** Marketing coordinator, proposal manager, or firm principal at a 10-50 person engineering firm pursuing 30-80 proposals per year
**Automation Potential:** High — 60-70% of proposal content is reusable boilerplate (firm qualifications, team resumes, past project descriptions) that is manually copied and adapted for each submission

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Engineering firms win work through competitive proposals responding to RFPs/RFQs from public agencies, private developers, and other clients. A typical 20-person firm pursues 40-80 proposals per year, winning 20-35%. Each proposal requires assembling firm qualifications, relevant project experience, team resumes, project approach narratives, organizational charts, and fee schedules — content that is 60-70% reusable but stored in unstructured Word documents across shared drives. The principal or marketing coordinator spends 15-40 hours per major proposal hunting for the best past project description, updating resumes, and adapting approach language. With a 25-30% win rate, 70% of this effort produces no revenue. The proposal assembly workflow is the single largest non-billable time sink at most engineering firms.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Enterprise AEC proposal tools (RFPIO, Cosential/Unanet CRM, OpenAsset for images) exist but cost $200-500/month per user and require dedicated marketing staff to maintain content databases — a staffing model that firms under 50 people can't support. Most firms use Word templates, InDesign layouts, and shared drives organized by project number. Finding "our best water treatment plant project description" requires searching through 20 past proposal folders or asking the principal who remembers which one they wrote.
