# Route Density Optimizer for Residential Lawn Routes

**Niche:** [[niches/landscaping/residential-maintenance/profile|Residential Maintenance]]
**Industry:** [[industries/landscaping|Landscaping]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** A route optimization engine purpose-built for recurring residential lawn maintenance that clusters properties by geography, crew capability, and equipment requirements — replacing the operations manager's mental map with a system that adapts weekly to cancellations, new clients, and seasonal service changes.
**Tags:** #tacit-knowledge-ml #gradient-boosting #time-series-forecasting #automation #revenue-impact

## The Problem
Residential lawn maintenance companies run 3-10 crews across 200-800 properties on weekly cycles, with each crew hitting 15-25 properties per day. Route design is the single largest lever on profitability — a poorly routed crew wastes 60-90 minutes daily in windshield time between stops. Today, routes are built by an operations manager who knows the territory: which neighborhoods cluster well, which properties take 20 minutes vs. 45 minutes, which ones require the smaller walk-behind mower that only fits on Truck 3, which clients have gate codes that change monthly. This knowledge accumulates over 2-3 years of adjustments and lives entirely in one person's head. When that person leaves — which happens frequently in a high-turnover industry — the company can lose 15-25% of its crew productivity for months while someone else rebuilds route knowledge through trial and error. New client additions and cancellations happen weekly, requiring constant route rebalancing that compounds the cognitive load.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
Generic route optimization tools (OptimoRoute, Routific, Google OR-Tools) solve the traveling salesman problem well but miss landscaping-specific constraints that make the difference between a theoretically optimal route and a practically efficient one. These constraints include: crew composition (a three-person crew with a 60" zero-turn and a 36" walk-behind can handle large lots but is overkill for small townhome strips), property access rules (gated communities with specific entry hours, backyard gates requiring client notification, HOA-mandated service windows), equipment-to-property matching (some properties require specific mower deck widths based on gate width or terrain slope), and time-per-property variance that depends on season (spring growth doubles mowing time), weather (wet grass takes 30% longer), and service level (full-service vs. mow-only). Capturing the ops manager's tacit knowledge requires structured data collection from GPS traces, job completion timestamps, and crew feedback over hundreds of route-days — data that exists in fragments across Jobber, phone GPS, and crew texts but has never been unified.

## What to Build
A route optimization platform that ingests property data (address, lot size, service level, equipment requirements, access constraints), crew data (truck assignment, equipment loadout, crew size, skill level), and historical job data (actual time-per-property by season and weather conditions from GPS and job completion timestamps). A gradient-boosted regression model predicts time-per-property as a function of lot size, service type, season, recent precipitation, grass growth rate, and equipment type — capturing the experienced ops manager's intuitive time estimates. The optimizer then solves a constrained vehicle routing problem daily: minimizing total drive time while respecting crew-equipment matching, property access windows, crew work-hour limits, and property visit frequency requirements. When a rain day forces rescheduling, the system cascades the changes across the week automatically, rebatching missed properties into the next available day with minimal drive-time impact. Weekly reports show per-property profitability (revenue vs. actual crew time allocated), flagging money-losing properties for price increases or service adjustments.

## Target Customer
Residential lawn maintenance companies with 3-15 crews and 200-800+ properties on recurring contracts, generating $500K-5M in annual revenue. These companies have enough route complexity that manual optimization is a full-time cognitive task for one person, and enough data volume (3,000-15,000 job completions per season) to train property-level time prediction models within one season.

## Impact If Built
Reduces average daily drive time per crew by 20-35% (saving 45-75 minutes per crew per day), which translates to 2-4 additional properties per crew per day at current staffing — worth $150K-500K in incremental annual revenue for a 5-crew company without hiring. Eliminates the single-point-of-failure risk of losing the operations manager's route knowledge, which currently costs companies $50K-150K in lost productivity during the 3-6 month rebuilding period.
