# Small Mow & Blow

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/landscaping|Landscaping]]
**Category:** Underserved

## Profile
**Market Size:** $10-12B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~10%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Most solo operators run their business from a personal phone: scheduling in the calendar app, invoicing via Venmo/Zelle request or handwritten invoices, and client records in the contacts list. Jobber and Housecall Pro exist but are perceived as too complex and too expensive ($50+/month) for someone mowing 20-40 yards.
**Target Buyer:** Solo operator or two-person crew with a truck, trailer, and a handful of mowers servicing 20-40 residential clients
**Automation Potential:** High — the business is simple enough that a single integrated app could handle scheduling, routing, invoicing, and client communication, replacing 4-5 disconnected tools

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
The small mow-and-blow operator is the most common business model in landscaping: one person with a truck, a trailer, a zero-turn mower, a string trimmer, and a blower, servicing 20-40 residential properties on weekly rotation. Revenue is $40K-120K/year, margins are tight (60-70% after fuel, equipment, and insurance), and the operator is simultaneously the technician, the sales team, the bookkeeper, and the customer service department. The workflow is deceptively simple — mow, edge, blow, collect payment — but the business management overhead is crushing for someone who entered the trade to work outdoors, not to manage a small business. Scheduling changes arrive as text messages scattered across client conversations. Payment collection is inconsistent — some clients pay via Venmo, some mail checks, some "forget" for weeks. Route planning is mental: the operator knows their Tuesday route by memory but can't easily add a new client without mentally re-sequencing the day. The biggest pain point is the transition from "guy with a mower" to "legitimate small business" — getting an LLC, commercial insurance, a separate business bank account, quarterly tax estimates — which most solo operators defer until forced by a liability event or tax problem.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Jobber ($60/month Core plan) is the most recommended tool for small landscapers, but its feature set is built for 5-15 employee companies: crew management, multiple calendar views, and permission settings that a solo operator will never use. The onboarding friction alone loses most solo operators — entering 30 client records, setting up recurring jobs, and configuring invoicing templates takes 3-4 hours that a solo operator doesn't have in their 10-hour work day. Yardbook is free but ad-supported and limited. LawnPro and other niche apps target this market but have poor UX and limited payment integration. The real competitor is "text messages + Venmo + Google Calendar" — a free, familiar, zero-learning-curve stack that works well enough until the operator hits 30-40 clients and starts dropping balls.
