# Snow Removal

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/landscaping|Landscaping]]
**Category:** Underserved

## Profile
**Market Size:** $15-18B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~15% (seasonal overlay, October-April in northern markets)
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Snow-specific software barely exists. Most landscaping companies that add snow removal track contracts in Excel, dispatch crews via group text, and document per-event service with timestamped truck photos that live in phone galleries. Generic field service tools don't model snow-specific workflows (per-push vs. per-season pricing, salt application tracking, trigger depth thresholds).
**Target Buyer:** Landscaping company owner in northern markets who adds snow removal as a seasonal revenue overlay, or a dedicated snow removal company owner managing commercial contracts
**Automation Potential:** High — snow events are predictable (weather data), service triggers are quantifiable (accumulation thresholds), and per-event documentation is critical for billing disputes and slip-and-fall liability defense

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Snow removal is the seasonal counterpart to landscape maintenance in northern US and Canadian markets — the same trucks, the same crews, and often the same commercial clients, but with completely different operational dynamics. Snow events are unpredictable in timing and duration: a crew might sit idle for three weeks then work 36 hours straight during a major storm. Pricing models are unique: per-push (paid per service event, triggered at specific accumulation thresholds like 2", 4", 6"), per-season (flat fee regardless of snowfall — a gamble for both parties), or time-and-materials (hourly rate plus salt/sand costs). Commercial contracts specify trigger depths, response times (e.g., "parking lot cleared within 4 hours of 2-inch accumulation"), and de-icing requirements. The liability exposure is extreme: a slip-and-fall lawsuit on a commercial property can produce $500K-2M in damages, and the snow contractor's defense depends entirely on documenting that they serviced the property according to the contract terms — what time they arrived, what time they finished, how much salt they applied, and what conditions existed during and after service. Today, this documentation is a timestamp on a truck GPS track and maybe a phone photo of the cleared lot — evidence that is often insufficient in court.

## Current Tools & Gaps
There is no dominant snow-specific operations platform. LMN and Aspire handle snow as an add-on to landscaping but their scheduling models assume predictable recurring service, not weather-triggered dispatch. Yeti Snow and SnowEx have attempted snow-specific fleet management but have limited adoption. Most companies use a combination of weather apps, group texts for crew dispatch, Excel for contract tracking, and phone cameras for documentation. The critical gap is event-level documentation that connects weather data (official accumulation readings), service timestamps (GPS arrival/departure), material usage (salt/sand quantities), and photographic evidence (before/during/after conditions) into a defensible service record per property per event — the document a lawyer needs to defend against a slip-and-fall claim.
