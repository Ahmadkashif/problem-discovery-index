# Telecom & Fiber

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/utility-contractors|Utility Contractors]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $20-25B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~15%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — OSP (Outside Plant) design tools handle fiber route engineering but construction sequencing and make-ready coordination are managed through spreadsheets and email chains with pole owners.
**Target Buyer:** Telecom/fiber contractor owner, OSP engineer, or construction project manager
**Automation Potential:** High — fiber route optimization is a graph problem, make-ready pole attachment tracking is a workflow management challenge, and construction sequencing is an optimization opportunity

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Telecom and fiber construction is experiencing explosive growth driven by federal broadband funding (BEAD program: $42.5B), 5G small cell densification, and private fiber-to-the-home deployments. The construction involves aerial fiber (attached to existing utility poles) and underground fiber (directional bore or open trench), with aerial being faster and cheaper where pole infrastructure exists. The critical bottleneck is make-ready: before a fiber company can attach to a utility pole, the pole owner (typically the electric utility) must verify that the pole has capacity for additional attachments and may need to rearrange existing attachments, replace the pole, or add a taller pole. Make-ready processing takes 3-18 months per pole, and a fiber-to-the-home project may involve 5,000-20,000 poles. Managing this make-ready process — application, engineering review, permit, construction, inspection — across thousands of poles with multiple pole owners is the defining operational challenge of fiber construction.

## Current Tools & Gaps
OSP design tools (3-GIS, Vetro FiberMap) handle fiber network design. Katapult Engineering handles pole loading analysis. But make-ready tracking — managing the status of thousands of pole attachment applications across multiple pole owners — is done in Excel spreadsheets. Construction sequencing — determining which fiber segments to build first based on make-ready completion, permitting status, and customer demand — is manual planning.
