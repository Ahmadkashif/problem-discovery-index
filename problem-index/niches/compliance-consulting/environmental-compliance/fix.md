# Emissions Reporting Data Assembly and EPA Template Formatting

**Niche:** [[niches/compliance-consulting/environmental-compliance/profile|Environmental Compliance]]
**Industry:** [[industries/compliance-consulting|Compliance Consulting]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Emissions reports require pulling data from continuous emissions monitors, production logs, fuel consumption records, and material safety data sheets, then applying EPA-published emission factors and formatting to agency-specific XML/CSV templates — a process that takes 20-40 hours per facility and is 90% mechanical data assembly.
**Tags:** #data-integration #automation #compliance #worker-facing

## The Problem
Annual emissions inventory reporting is the most labor-intensive recurring deliverable in environmental compliance consulting. Every facility with an air quality permit must report actual emissions to their state environmental agency, using calculations prescribed by EPA (AP-42 emission factors, continuous emissions monitoring data, or mass balance methods). The inputs come from multiple systems: continuous emissions monitoring systems (CEMS) produce hourly pollutant concentration data in proprietary formats, production management systems track throughput (tons of material processed, units manufactured), fuel consumption records show natural gas, diesel, and propane usage, and material safety data sheets (MSDS) document the chemical composition of raw materials. The consultant must: (1) extract data from each source, (2) reconcile units and time periods, (3) apply the correct calculation methodology for each pollutant and emission source, (4) generate results in the state agency's required format (often a specific XML schema or CSV template), and (5) validate totals against permit limits. For a facility with 10-15 emission sources and 5-8 pollutants, this takes 20-40 hours. The calculations themselves are straightforward algebra — the time is consumed by data extraction, format conversion, and cross-checking.

## Why It's Still Broken
Every state has a different reporting format. Texas TCEQ uses a specific online portal with its own data schema. California CARB requires emissions reported through the Cal e-GGRT system. Ohio EPA uses an Excel-based emissions inventory form. The consultant must know each state's format and manually transfer calculated results into the correct template. Additionally, the source data systems (CEMS, ERP, fuel invoices) have no standard export format — the consultant develops custom extraction procedures for each client's specific equipment and software. A consultant managing 20 clients across 3 states maintains 20 different data extraction procedures, 3 different state reporting formats, and hundreds of emission factor lookups. This is deeply manual work that resists automation because of the heterogeneity of inputs and outputs, not because of computational complexity.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A data pipeline tool that connects to common CEMS platforms (Envision, ESC, SICK), ERP systems (SAP, Oracle, QuickBooks for smaller facilities), and fuel vendor portals, normalizes the extracted data into a standard internal format, applies the correct EPA emission factors based on the facility's source registration, performs the prescribed calculations, and formats results to the target state agency's template. The tool doesn't replace the consultant's judgment on methodology selection (which calculation method applies to which emission source) but eliminates the 15-30 hours of data extraction, unit conversion, and template population that surround the 5-10 hours of actual analytical work. Pre-built connectors for the 10 most common CEMS platforms and 5 most common state reporting formats would cover 60-70% of engagements.

## Who Feels the Pain
The junior environmental consultant who spends January through March pulling data from client systems, debugging format mismatches, and manually entering numbers into state reporting templates, and the senior consultant who reviews the submissions but can't verify the data extraction steps without retracing the entire process.

## Impact If Fixed
Reduces emissions reporting labor from 20-40 hours to 5-15 hours per facility by automating data extraction and template population. For a consulting firm managing annual emissions reports for 20 industrial clients, this recovers 300-500 hours during the January-March reporting season — the equivalent of adding a temporary hire without the recruitment and training burden. Error rates in data transcription (currently 5-10% of fields require correction after review) drop to near-zero for automated extractions.
