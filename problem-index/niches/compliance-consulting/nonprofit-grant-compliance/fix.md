# Single Audit Evidence Assembly for 2 CFR 200 Subpart F

**Niche:** [[niches/compliance-consulting/nonprofit-grant-compliance/profile|Nonprofit & Grant Compliance]]
**Industry:** [[industries/compliance-consulting|Compliance Consulting]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Nonprofits spending $750K+ in federal funds must undergo a single audit — and the evidence assembly process takes 60-120 hours because the required documentation lives in 5-10 disconnected systems with no standard packaging format.
**Tags:** #large-language-models #data-integration #compliance #automation #worker-facing

## The Problem
The single audit (2 CFR 200 Subpart F) is the most resource-intensive compliance event for federally funded nonprofits. The audit firm tests compliance across 12 compliance types for each major federal program: activities allowed, allowable costs, cash management, eligibility, matching, period of performance, procurement, program income, reporting, subrecipient monitoring, equipment management, and special tests. For each compliance type, the nonprofit must produce evidence: procurement records showing competitive bidding, personnel activity reports showing time allocation to federal programs, eligibility documentation for program participants, subrecipient monitoring records, equipment inventories, and financial reports reconciled to the general ledger. This evidence lives in: the accounting system (QuickBooks, Sage Intacct), the case management system (Salesforce, Apricot), the HR system (payroll records, timesheets), the procurement files (paper purchase orders, vendor quotes), email (subrecipient communications), and shared drives (policies, procedures, board minutes). The compliance consultant's job is to locate, extract, organize, and present this evidence to the audit firm — a process that takes 60-120 hours per audit and is essentially a treasure hunt through disconnected systems.

## Why It's Still Broken
Single audits happen annually but the evidence generation happens continuously throughout the year — every procurement decision, every eligibility determination, every timesheet creates evidence that must be retrievable 6-18 months later. Nonprofits don't have document management systems designed for audit evidence — they file procurement records by vendor rather than by compliance type, store personnel activity reports in the HR system rather than with the grant file, and keep eligibility documentation in case management rather than in a compliance-accessible format. The consultant addresses this retroactively: starting 2-3 months before the audit, they request evidence across all 12 compliance types, chase down missing documents, and organize everything into the audit binder. This is a fundamentally reactive process applied to a fundamentally predictable requirement — the same 12 compliance types are tested every year, the same evidence categories are requested, and the same systems contain the same types of documentation.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A single audit evidence manager that pre-configures the evidence requirements for each major federal program based on the Compliance Supplement (the OMB's guide to audit requirements by federal program), then provides a year-round collection workflow that tags and stores evidence as it's generated. When procurement decisions are made, the relevant documents (quotes, bid evaluations, purchase orders) are uploaded and tagged to the applicable grant and compliance type. When personnel activity reports are completed, they're linked to the time-and-effort compliance category. When eligibility determinations are made, the supporting documentation is captured in the case management workflow. By the time the audit begins, the evidence binder is substantially complete — the consultant reviews for gaps rather than assembling from scratch. A large language model assists with evidence categorization: when a document is uploaded, it classifies which compliance type(s) it supports and flags documents that are incomplete (a procurement file without competitive quotes, a subrecipient agreement without required federal provisions).

## Who Feels the Pain
The finance director who spends 3 months before the audit pulling records from every system in the organization, the compliance consultant who charges $15K-25K for audit preparation that is 80% evidence assembly and 20% strategic guidance, and the program directors who are interrupted for "just one more document" every week during audit season.

## Impact If Fixed
Reduces single audit evidence assembly from 60-120 hours to 15-30 hours by shifting from retrospective gathering to continuous collection. The compliance consultant's audit preparation engagement becomes a review-and-validate exercise rather than a locate-and-assemble project, reducing cost to the nonprofit by 50-60% while maintaining the same quality. Evidence completeness improves from 70-80% at initial assembly (requiring 2-3 rounds of follow-up requests) to 90-95% at audit start, compressing the audit timeline and reducing the audit firm's hours (which the nonprofit also pays for).
