# Board-Level HR Decision Documentation and Compliance

**Niche:** [[niches/hr-consultants/nonprofit-hr-services/profile|Nonprofit HR Services]]
**Industry:** [[industries/hr-consultants|HR Consultants]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Nonprofit boards make HR decisions (executive compensation, terminations, policy approvals) that require specific documentation for IRS compliance and fiduciary protection — but board minutes rarely capture the required level of detail, and the HR consultant discovers the gap during the next 990 filing or, worse, during litigation.
**Tags:** #compliance #automation #worker-facing #quick-win

## The Problem
IRS regulations require nonprofits to follow a "rebuttable presumption" process for executive compensation: an independent body (the board or compensation committee) must review comparability data, make a determination, and document the decision contemporaneously in meeting minutes. If this process isn't followed, the board loses its presumption of reasonableness, and individual board members can face personal excise tax liability. The same documentation requirements apply to conflict-of-interest decisions, employment agreements, and severance packages. In practice, board secretaries write minimal minutes ("The board approved the ED's salary for next year"), omitting the comparability data reviewed, the discussion of factors considered, the recusal of interested parties, and the specific motion and vote. The HR consultant advising on compensation discovers months later that the minutes don't document the process sufficiently.

## Why It's Still Broken
Board members are volunteers with limited governance training. They understand that they need to approve executive compensation but not that the approval process has specific documentation requirements under IRS rules. The board secretary (often a volunteer) takes informal notes focused on decisions, not process. HR consultants advise on the substantive compensation decision but may not attend board meetings to ensure proper documentation. Governance training is sporadic, and the consequences of poor documentation (IRS intermediate sanctions, personal liability for board members) are abstract until an audit or lawsuit materializes.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A board meeting documentation tool with pre-built agenda templates for HR decisions: compensation approval (with fields for comparability data source, organizations compared, percentile analysis, conflicts disclosed, recusals, and specific motion language), conflict-of-interest disclosures (annual and per-transaction), employment agreement approvals, and termination decisions. The HR consultant provides the template before the meeting; the board secretary fills in the structured fields during the meeting; the system generates IRS-compliant minutes with all required documentation elements. Post-meeting, the HR consultant reviews the documentation for completeness before minutes are approved.

## Who Feels the Pain
HR consultants who must ensure board decisions are properly documented for IRS compliance but have no control over how board meetings are conducted, and board members who face personal liability for improperly documented compensation decisions.

## Impact If Fixed
Ensures IRS rebuttable presumption documentation is complete for every executive compensation decision. Protects individual board members from excise tax liability under IRC Section 4958 (25% tax on the excess benefit transaction amount). Prevents the governance documentation failures that employment attorneys exploit in wrongful termination litigation against nonprofits.
