# Unstructured Delivery Service That Loses Money Per Trip

**Niche:** [[niches/independent-retailers/senior-shoppers-accessibility/profile|Accessibility-Focused Retail for Senior Shoppers]]
**Industry:** [[industries/independent-retailers|Independent Retailers]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** The store delivers to homebound seniors because it's the right thing to do, but without route optimization, minimum order requirements, or delivery fee structures, each delivery costs $15-25 in staff time and vehicle expense against $30-50 in merchandise — making every delivery a loss leader with no plan to break even.
**Tags:** #automation #revenue-impact #worker-facing #quick-win

## The Problem
Independent retailers in senior-heavy communities deliver groceries and supplies to homebound customers as an informal service. The owner or a staff member loads orders into their personal car and drives to 3-8 addresses per delivery run. There are no delivery fees (the owner feels uncomfortable charging seniors who can't leave their homes), no minimum order requirements (a delivery might be $15 worth of groceries), and no route optimization (stops are visited in the order the orders came in, not by geographic efficiency). The cost per delivery — staff time (30-45 minutes round trip), vehicle costs ($0.67/mile IRS rate), and opportunity cost (the store is short-staffed during the delivery run) — typically exceeds the margin on the delivered merchandise. The owner knows the service loses money but can't bring themselves to stop it.

## Why It's Still Broken
The delivery service exists as a personal relationship between the store and the customer, not as a business function. Formalizing it (adding fees, setting minimums) feels like betraying the community trust the store has built over decades. The owner has no data showing the actual cost per delivery because they've never tracked it — the cost is invisible, buried in overall payroll and vehicle expenses. Without visibility into the true economics, the owner can't design a delivery model that serves customers sustainably.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A delivery economics tool that: (1) calculates the true cost per delivery (staff time at actual hourly rate + mileage at IRS rate + opportunity cost estimate), (2) shows the owner the monthly/annual cost of their delivery service ("you're spending $850/month on deliveries that generate $1,200 in sales at 35% margin = $420 gross margin — you're losing $430/month"), (3) models break-even scenarios: "A $3 delivery fee with a $25 minimum order brings the service to breakeven; a $5 fee with a $30 minimum generates $200/month profit," (4) provides route optimization for delivery runs (Google Maps-based route planning that reduces drive time 20-30%), and (5) batches deliveries by zone and day ("Tuesday and Friday deliveries, northside on Tuesday, southside on Friday"). The tool gives the owner data to make informed decisions about pricing rather than operating on guilt.

## Who Feels the Pain
The store owner who knows the delivery service isn't financially sustainable but has no data to redesign it, and the staff member who spends their afternoon driving deliveries instead of serving in-store customers.

## Impact If Fixed
Transforms a $5,000-10,000/year loss into a break-even or modest-profit service by introducing minimum orders, delivery fees, and route optimization. Route optimization alone saves 20-30% on delivery drive time. Having delivery economics visible enables the owner to make sustainable decisions about service expansion rather than subsidizing indefinitely.
