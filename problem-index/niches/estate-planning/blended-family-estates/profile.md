# Blended Family Estates

## Profile
**Market Size:** $0.5-0.8B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~10%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — document assembly tools don't flag beneficiary designation conflicts, and no tool models the competing interests inherent in blended family planning
**Target Buyer:** Estate planning attorney serving remarried clients with children from prior marriages
**Automation Potential:** Medium-High — beneficiary designation audits, conflict detection across documents and accounts, and QTIP trust modeling are all systematizable

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Blended family estate planning is adversarial by nature: the new spouse's interests conflict with the children from the prior marriage. Without careful planning, one side gets everything and the other gets nothing — a remarried person who dies with a simple will leaving everything to the new spouse effectively disinherits children from the first marriage. The planning tools are different (QTIP trusts, prenuptial/postnuptial agreements integrated with estate plans, beneficiary designation coordination), the emotional dynamics are different (attorneys must navigate competing interests without taking sides), and the failure modes are different (the #1 cause of estate plan failure in blended families is outdated beneficiary designations on retirement accounts and life insurance that override the will or trust). An IRA beneficiary designation naming the ex-spouse trumps a new will — and most clients don't know this.

## Current Tools & Gaps
WealthDocx and similar drafting tools generate QTIP trusts and other blended-family-appropriate documents, but they operate at the document level. They don't see the client's full picture: retirement account beneficiary designations, life insurance beneficiaries, joint account titling, transfer-on-death designations. The most common estate plan failure in blended families happens outside the documents the attorney drafts — in the beneficiary designations the client set up years ago and forgot about. No tool provides a holistic view of how a client's estate would actually distribute at death, accounting for both the estate plan documents and the non-probate transfers (beneficiary designations, joint accounts, TOD registrations) that override them.
