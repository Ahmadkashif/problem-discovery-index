# Basic Wills & POA

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/estate-planning|Estate Planning]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1-1.5B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~25%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — WealthDocx and HotDocs handle document drafting but the intake-to-draft pipeline is manual, and client acquisition relies on referrals and word-of-mouth
**Target Buyer:** General practice attorney offering basic estate planning as a service line, or solo estate planning attorney serving middle-class families
**Automation Potential:** Very High — simple wills, POAs, and healthcare directives are template-driven with limited customization, making the entire workflow from intake questionnaire to signed document highly automatable

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Basic estate planning (simple wills, durable powers of attorney, healthcare directives, basic revocable trusts) serves the vast middle market — families with $500K-$5M in assets who need standard documents, not complex trust structures. The economics are fundamentally different from HNW planning: flat fees of $1,500-$4,000 per plan vs. hourly billing at $500+/hr. Volume matters. The competitive threat is existential: LegalZoom, Trust & Will, and Willful offer DIY estate plans for $89-$399, pulling price-sensitive clients out of the attorney market entirely. Attorneys in this niche must either match DIY speed and convenience while adding attorney review value, or accept shrinking market share.

## Current Tools & Gaps
WealthDocx and HotDocs generate individual documents from templates, but the full workflow — client intake questionnaire, data validation, document generation, review, signing coordination, and plan delivery — requires manual orchestration between separate tools. Practice management software (Clio, Tabs3) handles billing and calendaring but doesn't connect to document assembly. No tool automates the end-to-end pipeline from "client answers questionnaire" to "complete plan package ready for attorney review." The DIY competitors (LegalZoom, Trust & Will) have solved the client-facing UX but lack attorney review and state-specific customization.
