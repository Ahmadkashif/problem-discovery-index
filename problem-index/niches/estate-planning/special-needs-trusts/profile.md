## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Special needs trusts (SNTs) serve a population where getting it wrong has devastating consequences: a single improper distribution can disqualify a disabled beneficiary from SSI (up to $943/month) and Medicaid (healthcare coverage worth $10K-$50K/year). There are two types — first-party SNTs (funded with the disabled person's own assets, typically from a personal injury settlement or inheritance, with a Medicaid payback provision) and third-party SNTs (funded by family members, no payback). Each has different rules for allowable distributions, reporting requirements, and termination provisions. Trustees must understand what SSA considers "income" vs. "in-kind support and maintenance" and navigate the difference between distributions that reduce SSI by $1 and distributions that eliminate it entirely.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Trust accounting software (FAS, Fiduciary Accounting Solutions, Surety) handles trust financial administration — transactions, statements, tax reporting — but has no awareness of SSI/Medicaid rules. The software doesn't flag when a proposed distribution (e.g., paying rent directly) would constitute in-kind support and maintenance, reducing the beneficiary's SSI. No tool connects trust administration to benefit eligibility monitoring. Attorneys and professional trustees rely on SSA's POMS (Program Operations Manual System) — a dense, poorly organized online manual — and their own experience to determine what distributions are safe.
