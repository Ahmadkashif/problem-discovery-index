# Commercial Vehicle Collision

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/auto-body-shops|Auto Body Shops]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $7B
**Share of Parent Industry:** 14%
**Digital Adoption:** Medium — Commercial fleet managers use telematics and fleet management platforms, but the body shop receiving the damaged commercial vehicle operates with consumer-oriented collision tools.
**Target Buyer:** Body shop owner with bays large enough for commercial trucks, vans, and box trucks
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Downtime cost calculation, fleet-manager communication, and DOT compliance documentation are automatable but currently handled manually.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Commercial vehicle collision repair (box trucks, sprinter vans, work trucks with upfit equipment, dump trucks) requires different facilities (larger bays, higher lifts, specialized frame equipment), different parts sourcing (commercial vehicle parts supply chains), and different customer dynamics (fleet managers who need downtime calculations, rental replacements, and DOT compliance documentation). A damaged FedEx delivery van has a measurable cost-per-day of downtime ($400-$800) that fundamentally changes the repair prioritization calculus. Most body shops are built for passenger cars and lack the physical space, equipment, and workflow to serve commercial vehicles efficiently.

## Current Tools & Gaps
CCC ONE and Mitchell estimating platforms cover commercial vehicles but with less comprehensive parts and labor data than passenger cars. Fleet management platforms (Samsara, Fleetio) track vehicle status but don't integrate into the body shop's repair workflow. No platform bridges the fleet manager's downtime tracking with the body shop's production scheduling to optimize repair priority based on fleet impact.
