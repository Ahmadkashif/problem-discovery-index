# Classic & Specialty Car Restoration

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/auto-body-shops|Auto Body Shops]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $8B
**Share of Parent Industry:** 16%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Restoration shops run on verbal agreements, handwritten progress notes, and photo albums shared via text message. Project management is entirely manual.
**Target Buyer:** Restoration shop owner managing 5-15 concurrent long-term projects
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Project milestones, parts sourcing, and customer communication are automatable, but the craft itself requires irreplaceable human skill.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Restoration projects span 6 months to 3 years, involve $20K-$200K+ budgets, and progress through dozens of distinct phases (disassembly, media blasting, metalwork, bodywork, primer, paint, reassembly, trim, electrical, upholstery). Unlike collision repair where an insurer dictates scope and timeline, restoration scope evolves continuously as hidden damage is discovered during disassembly. Customer communication is critical — owners are emotionally invested in their vehicles and demand regular photo updates and milestone transparency. The entire workflow model (long-duration, scope-evolving, customer-funded) is opposite to collision repair (short-duration, scope-fixed, insurer-funded).

## Current Tools & Gaps
Collision repair management systems (CCC ONE, Mitchell) are designed for insurance claims with fixed scopes and short timelines — completely wrong for restoration. Generic project management tools (Monday.com, Asana) lack automotive-specific phase templates, parts tracking, and photo documentation workflows. Most restoration shops use whiteboards for project status, text messages for customer updates, and spiral notebooks for scope tracking.
