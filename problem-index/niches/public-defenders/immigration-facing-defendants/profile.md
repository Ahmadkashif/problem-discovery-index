# Immigration-Facing Defendants

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/public-defenders|Public Defenders]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $450M
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~6%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Immigration consequences analysis is done manually by the few defenders who know immigration law; most defenders don't assess immigration impact at all.
**Target Buyer:** Immigration-Certified Defender / Chief Public Defender
**Automation Potential:** High — Immigration consequence screening is rule-based (categorical match between criminal charge and immigration provision) and highly automatable.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Since Padilla v. Kentucky (2010), public defenders are constitutionally required to advise noncitizen clients about the immigration consequences of criminal convictions — deportation, inadmissibility, bars to naturalization. This creates a specialized demand within public defense: every case involving a noncitizen client requires immigration-criminal law crossover analysis. Most public defenders have no immigration law training, the intersection of criminal and immigration law is extremely complex (a misdemeanor theft conviction can trigger mandatory deportation), and the consequences of bad advice are catastrophic (permanent exile from the U.S.).

## Current Tools & Gaps
A handful of resources exist: the Immigrant Legal Resource Center's "Quick Reference Chart" maps criminal convictions to immigration consequences, and ILRC's online tools provide basic screening. But these are reference materials, not workflow tools — they don't integrate with case management systems, don't auto-flag noncitizen clients at intake, and require the defender to already know enough immigration law to ask the right questions. No tool exists that proactively screens a client's charges and immigration status to generate a case-specific consequence analysis.
