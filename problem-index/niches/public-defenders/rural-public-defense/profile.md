# Rural Public Defense

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/public-defenders|Public Defenders]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $600M
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~8%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Many rural jurisdictions use contract defenders (private attorneys paid per case) with no shared case management system, no standardized intake, and limited internet connectivity.
**Target Buyer:** Contract Defender / County Board of Supervisors
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Remote client communication and document management are automatable, but the decentralized contract-defender model makes adoption difficult.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Rural public defense is structurally different from urban practice. Instead of staffed offices, most rural counties contract with private attorneys who handle indigent defense as a side practice — often a solo practitioner taking cases at flat rates of $300-800 per case regardless of complexity. These contract defenders work in isolation, have no support staff, no investigators, and no access to shared case management systems. They may cover multiple counties, driving hours between courthouses. The digital infrastructure that urban offices rely on (even inadequate tools like LegalServer) simply doesn't exist in rural practice.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Contract defenders typically use whatever tools they have for their private practice — Clio, MyCase, or just paper files and a personal calendar. There is no shared system for the county to track case assignments, monitor caseloads, or verify representation quality. Court records may be accessible only in person at the courthouse. Legal research access depends entirely on the individual attorney's subscriptions. The gap is total: rural indigent defense has no purpose-built technology whatsoever.
