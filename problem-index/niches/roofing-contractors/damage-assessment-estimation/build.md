# AI Damage Classifier with Severity Scoring

**Niche:** [[niches/roofing-contractors/damage-assessment-estimation/profile|Damage Assessment & Estimation]]
**Industry:** [[industries/roofing-contractors|Roofing Contractors]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** A computer vision system that classifies roof damage from drone imagery — hail hits, wind-lifted shingles, missing shingles, flashing failures, ponding water, membrane punctures — with per-instance severity scores and facet-level damage maps that auto-generate Xactimate scope items, replacing the most judgment-dependent and error-prone step in roofing estimation.

## The Problem
Roof damage classification is the single highest-value tacit knowledge task in roofing. An experienced inspector identifies and classifies 15-20 distinct damage types from visual inspection: hail bruises (circular granule displacement on shingles), wind creases (horizontal fold lines where adhesive strips failed), lifted tabs, missing shingles, cracked ridge cap, deteriorated pipe boot flashing, exposed nail heads from improper installation, blistering from manufacturing defects, algae/moss growth causing granule loss, ponding areas on flat roofs, membrane seam separation, and more. For each damage type, they assess severity — a few scattered hail hits are cosmetic (no claim), while dense hail coverage across multiple facets is a full replacement. They also distinguish cause — insurance covers storm damage but not aging, maintenance neglect, or manufacturing defects. This classification and severity assessment determines whether a roof is a $300 repair, a $3,000 partial replacement, or a $15,000 full replacement. Inspectors with 10+ years of experience agree with each other 75-80% of the time; junior inspectors agree with experienced inspectors only 50-60% of the time.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
Four compounding data challenges. First, labeled training data at the per-damage-instance level barely exists — most roof inspection documentation captures "before" and "after" photos, not annotated damage instances with type and severity labels. Building a training dataset requires experienced inspectors manually annotating thousands of images, which is expensive and slow. Second, inter-rater reliability is low — experienced inspectors disagree on severity 20-25% of the time, making ground truth labels noisy. Third, damage appearance varies dramatically by shingle type (3-tab vs. architectural vs. designer), shingle color (dark shingles hide hail damage), shingle age (new shingles show hail more clearly), and weather conditions at time of capture (wet roofs look different than dry roofs). Fourth, the adversarial dynamics between contractors and insurance carriers create a moving target — a model that consistently finds damage will be challenged by carriers, and a model that consistently misses damage will be rejected by contractors. The acceptable operating point depends on who the customer is.

## What to Build
A multi-stage damage classification pipeline. Stage 1: a semantic segmentation model that identifies roof components (shingle fields by facet, ridge caps, flashing, vents, pipe boots, valleys, gutters, drip edge) from drone imagery captured at 1-3cm/pixel resolution using a standardized flight pattern. Stage 2: an object detection model that locates individual damage instances within each component, classifying type (hail, wind, aging, mechanical, manufacturing) and severity (cosmetic/functional/replacement-required). Stage 3: a damage density calculator that aggregates per-instance detections into facet-level damage scores and generates a roof-level damage map. Stage 4: an Xactimate scope generator that translates the damage map into line items with quantities — "Replace 24 squares architectural shingles (facets 2, 3, 5, 7), replace 120 LF ridge cap, replace 3 pipe boot flashings." The system should output confidence scores per classification and flag low-confidence detections for human review rather than making definitive calls on borderline cases.

## Target Customer
Roofing companies with 5-30 inspectors/sales reps performing 500-5,000 inspections per year, already using or willing to adopt drones for roof capture. The primary value proposition is consistency — every inspection produces the same quality assessment regardless of inspector experience — and speed — reducing per-inspection time from 30-45 minutes on the roof to a 5-minute drone flight.

## Impact If Built
Eliminates the $3,000-8,000 per-claim revenue gap between junior and senior inspectors by standardizing damage detection. Increases inspection throughput 3-4x (5-minute drone flight vs. 30-45 minute roof walk), critical during the 2-4 week post-storm window when speed determines market share. Reduces liability from missed safety hazards (damaged decking, compromised structural elements) that inspectors currently discover only during tear-off.
