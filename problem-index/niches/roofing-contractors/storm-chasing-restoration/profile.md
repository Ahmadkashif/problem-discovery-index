# Storm Chasing & Restoration

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/roofing-contractors|Roofing Contractors]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $5-7B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~10-12%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Storm chasers operate out of trucks and hotel rooms in unfamiliar cities, use basic CRM (often just spreadsheets and phone contacts), and rely on word-of-mouth storm tracking rather than systematic market analysis. Most technology decisions are made on the road with poor connectivity.
**Target Buyer:** Mobile restoration crew owner or storm chaser operator running 2-10 crews across multiple states
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Storm path prediction is a data-rich problem, permit compliance across jurisdictions can be systematized, but the door-to-door sales model resists automation

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Storm chasing is roofing's most nomadic business model. When a major hailstorm or hurricane hits a region, dozens of roofing companies from other states deploy crews to the affected area, set up temporary operations (rented office space, hotel blocks, local material accounts), and canvass neighborhoods door-to-door signing homeowners to insurance-funded roof replacements. A successful storm chaser operation deploys within 48-72 hours of a major storm, operates in the affected market for 3-9 months, and generates $2-10M in revenue before moving to the next storm. The business requires rapid market entry decisions (which storm is worth chasing — not every hailstorm justifies a 500-mile deployment), temporary infrastructure setup (local permits, material supplier accounts, subcontractor networks), and compliance with unfamiliar local regulations (many municipalities have post-storm solicitation ordinances, cooling-off period requirements, and contractor licensing reciprocity rules that vary by state and county). The competitive dynamics are intense: 20-50 companies may descend on the same market, and the first movers who knock doors within 48 hours capture 60-70% of available contracts. Regulatory scrutiny is increasing — several states have passed "storm chaser" laws restricting out-of-state contractor solicitation practices after consumer complaints about abandoned jobs and warranty failures.

## Current Tools & Gaps
AccuLynx and JobNimbus handle basic CRM but are designed for companies with a fixed service area — they don't support multi-market temporary operations with different permit requirements, material suppliers, and subcontractor networks per market. Weather tracking apps (RadarScope, Storm Shield) show real-time weather but don't predict which storms will produce insurable hail damage at the density that justifies deployment. No tool aggregates local contractor licensing requirements, solicitation ordinances, or permit processes across the hundreds of jurisdictions a storm chaser might enter. The compliance gap is the most dangerous: a storm chaser operating without proper local permits or violating a solicitation ordinance faces fines, job shutdowns, and reputational damage in a market they'll never return to.
