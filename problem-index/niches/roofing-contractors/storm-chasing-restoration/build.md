# Storm Path Prediction & Market Entry Timing Tool

**Niche:** [[niches/roofing-contractors/storm-chasing-restoration/profile|Storm Chasing & Restoration]]
**Industry:** [[industries/roofing-contractors|Roofing Contractors]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** A decision-support platform that combines weather prediction, historical storm damage density, insurance claim filing rates, competitor deployment tracking, and local regulatory requirements to tell storm-chasing roofers exactly which storms are worth deploying to, when to arrive, and how long the market will sustain profitable operations.
**Tags:** #gradient-boosting #time-series-forecasting #feature-engineering #optimization-fundamentals #evaluation-metrics #cross-validation #revenue-impact

## The Problem
Storm chasing roofing companies make $500K-2M deployment decisions based on gut feel and informal networks. An owner watches the weather, calls contacts in affected areas, checks social media for damage reports, and decides within 24 hours whether to load trucks and drive 300-800 miles with 5-15 crew members. The variables that determine whether a deployment is profitable include: hail size and density (1" hail rarely produces insurable damage; 1.75"+ almost always does), affected housing density (rural hailstorms damage fewer roofs per square mile), housing age and roof material (older architectural shingles show hail damage more readily than impact-resistant shingles), insurance carrier mix (some carriers approve claims faster), competitor density (too many storm chasers in one market drives down per-job revenue), and local regulatory environment (states with anti-storm-chaser laws add compliance costs). Experienced storm chasers carry this multi-variable assessment intuitively — they "know" which storms are worth chasing after 10+ years of deployments. New entrants deploy to the wrong storms 40-50% of the time, burning $50K-150K per failed deployment in travel, lodging, and lost opportunity costs.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
The prediction requires integrating data sources that have never been connected: NOAA severe weather data (hail size reports, storm track), county-level building permit data (housing age and density), insurance industry data (carrier market share by geography, claim filing rates post-storm), competitor intelligence (which companies are deploying where — currently tracked through informal networks and social media), and regulatory databases (contractor licensing reciprocity, solicitation ordinances by municipality). Historical deployment outcome data — which storms generated profitable operations and which didn't — exists only in the memories and accounting records of experienced storm chasers who treat it as competitive intelligence. The evaluation metric is complex: a storm might be "worth chasing" for a 20-crew operation but not for a 5-crew operation, and profitability depends on how quickly the company can mobilize relative to competitors.

## What to Build
A storm deployment decision platform with three components. First, a real-time storm assessment model that ingests NOAA storm reports within hours of a severe weather event and predicts insurable damage density (damaged roofs per square mile) using hail size, wind speed, housing density, housing age distribution, and roof material mix for the affected area — output is a heat map of expected claim density. Second, a market viability model that overlays the damage assessment with competitive intelligence (social media monitoring for competitor deployment announcements, historical deployment patterns from SBA permit applications), local regulatory requirements (licensing, permits, solicitation rules), and available subcontractor/material capacity to predict net revenue opportunity by market. Third, a deployment timeline optimizer that recommends arrival timing (too early wastes pre-canvassing days; too late means competitors have locked up the market) and market exit timing (when the remaining unsigned homeowners no longer justify crew costs).

## Target Customer
Storm chasing restoration companies with 5-30 crew members making 3-8 deployment decisions per storm season, with annual revenue of $3-15M. These companies currently rely on owner intuition and informal intelligence networks to make deployment decisions that represent 30-50% of their annual revenue each.

## Impact If Built
Reduces failed deployment rate from 40-50% (industry average for mid-experience operators) to 10-15%, saving $100K-500K annually in wasted deployment costs. Improves market entry timing by 12-24 hours on average, capturing 15-25% more contracts through earlier door-knocking in validated markets.
