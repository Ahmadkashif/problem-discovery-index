# AI Roof Damage Assessment from Drone/Satellite Imagery

**Niche:** [[niches/roofing-contractors/residential-storm-insurance/profile|Residential Storm & Insurance]]
**Industry:** [[industries/roofing-contractors|Roofing Contractors]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** A system that analyzes drone and satellite roof imagery to classify damage type (hail, wind, aging, mechanical), locate affected areas, and score severity — replacing the 30-minute manual roof walk with a 5-minute drone flight and automated report that junior inspectors can deliver at expert-level accuracy.
**Tags:** #tacit-knowledge-ml #cnns #object-detection #semantic-segmentation #transfer-learning #evaluation-metrics #revenue-impact

## The Problem
Storm damage roof inspection is a tacit knowledge bottleneck. An experienced inspector walks a roof after a hailstorm and identifies: hail hits on shingles (circular depressions with granule displacement), wind-lifted tabs (adhesive strip failure visible at shingle edges), damaged pipe boot flashing, cracked ridge caps, and compromised drip edge. They mentally map the damage across roof facets and translate their findings into Xactimate line items — "Replace 24 squares of architectural shingles, replace 120 LF ridge cap, replace 3 pipe boots." A junior inspector on the same roof misses the subtle hail hits on darker shingles, overlooks wind damage on the leeward side, and doesn't check flashing details. The result: the experienced inspector's claim comes in at $16,000, the junior's at $9,000, for identical damage. There is no objective, repeatable damage assessment process — it is entirely dependent on the inspector's trained eye and years of pattern recognition across thousands of roofs.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
The data collection challenge is severe. Training a damage classifier requires thousands of labeled roof images with per-damage-type annotations — hail hit vs. blistering (manufacturing defect) vs. normal granule loss (aging) are visually similar and experts disagree on borderline cases 15-25% of the time. Hail damage appearance varies by shingle manufacturer, color, age, and hail size. Wind damage presentation differs between 3-tab and architectural shingles. Satellite imagery resolution (typically 30-50cm/pixel) may not capture individual hail hits — drone imagery at 1-2cm/pixel is needed for granular damage classification but adds a capture step. Insurance carriers would resist automated damage reports that consistently find more damage than their adjusters, creating an adversarial deployment environment. The labeling problem requires experienced inspectors to annotate images — the same scarce resource the tool aims to replace.

## What to Build
A two-stage damage assessment pipeline. Stage 1: a CNN-based semantic segmentation model trained on high-resolution drone imagery (DJI Mavic-class, 1-2cm/pixel) that identifies roof components (shingle fields, ridge caps, flashing, vents, pipe boots, valleys, drip edge) and damage indicators (hail impacts, lifted tabs, missing shingles, cracked components, exposed underlayment). Stage 2: an object detection model that counts and locates individual damage instances per roof facet, calculates affected area as a percentage of each facet, and assigns severity scores (cosmetic, functional, replacement-required) based on damage density and type. Output: a structured damage report with annotated imagery, facet-by-facet damage maps, and auto-generated Xactimate line items with quantities derived from EagleView measurements. The system should flag borderline cases for human review rather than over-classifying, maintaining credibility with adjusters.

## Target Customer
Residential storm-damage roofing companies with 5-30 sales reps/inspectors running 50-300 insurance claims per storm season. These companies already use drones for marketing photos and would redirect existing drone flights into a standardized damage capture protocol. The sweet spot is companies scaling beyond what their 2-3 experienced inspectors can handle — they need to send junior reps to inspections but lose revenue on every claim those juniors document.

## Impact If Built
Closes the $5,000-8,000 per-claim revenue gap between junior and experienced inspectors, worth $500K-2M annually for a 15-rep storm damage company. Reduces roof inspection time from 30-45 minutes of physical roof walking to a 5-minute drone flight plus 10-minute review, enabling each inspector to handle 3-4x more inspections per day during critical post-storm windows when speed determines market share.
