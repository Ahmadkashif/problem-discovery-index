# Post-Install Production Underperformance Diagnosis

**Niche:** [[niches/solar-installers/residential-rooftop/profile|Residential Rooftop Solar]]
**Industry:** [[industries/solar-installers|Solar Installers]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Twenty to thirty percent of residential solar systems produce 10-25% less energy than projected — but installers have no diagnostic tool to determine whether underperformance is caused by shading changes, soiling, equipment degradation, inverter issues, or inaccurate initial modeling, leaving them unable to resolve customer complaints or fulfill production guarantees.
**Tags:** #gradient-boosting #time-series-forecasting #worker-facing #revenue-impact #change-point-detection

## The Problem
When a homeowner's solar system produces less energy than the installer's proposal projected, they call the installer expecting a fix. The installer's diagnostic process is primitive: check the monitoring portal for obvious errors (inverter offline, communication failure), visually inspect the array for damage or soiling, and compare actual production to projected production. If there's no obvious equipment failure, the installer has no way to determine the cause of underperformance. Common causes include: tree growth that increased shading since the initial site assessment, soiling from nearby construction or agricultural activity, module degradation exceeding manufacturer specifications, inverter clipping losses from undersized inverter design, and inaccurate initial production modeling (the design tool overestimated production). Each cause requires a different remedy — tree trimming, panel cleaning, warranty claim, system redesign, or an honest conversation about modeling error — but without diagnosis, the installer guesses, often performing unnecessary truck rolls (panel cleaning when the issue is shading, or inverter replacement when the issue is soiling).

## Why It's Still Broken
Monitoring platforms (Enphase Enlighten, SolarEdge monitoring, Tesla app) show actual production but don't compare it to expected production under actual weather conditions. A system producing 20% less than projected may be performing exactly as expected for a cloudier-than-average year — or it may have a real problem masked by weather variability. Isolating equipment issues from weather variability from design errors requires comparing actual production against a weather-normalized expected production model — which requires both the original design parameters and actual local irradiance data for the monitoring period. No monitoring platform performs this comparison. Installers who offer production guarantees (increasingly common) face financial liability when systems underperform but can't determine whether the guarantee shortfall is their responsibility (design error, equipment issue) or not (weather, customer-planted trees).

## What a Fix Looks Like
A production diagnostic platform that: (1) imports the original system design parameters from Aurora (array orientation, tilt, shade profile, module specs, inverter specs); (2) imports actual production data from monitoring platforms (Enphase, SolarEdge, Tesla) via API; (3) imports actual weather data (irradiance, temperature, precipitation) from the nearest weather station for the monitoring period; (4) calculates expected production under actual weather conditions — what this system should have produced given the weather that actually occurred; (5) compares actual vs. weather-normalized expected production to quantify the underperformance gap; and (6) diagnoses the likely cause by analyzing production patterns — uniform underperformance across all hours suggests soiling or degradation; afternoon-only underperformance suggests new shading; clipping at peak hours suggests inverter sizing issues; specific module underperformance suggests equipment failure.

## Who Feels the Pain
Installers who receive underperformance complaints and have no diagnostic capability beyond "everything looks fine on the monitoring portal." Operations teams who dispatch expensive truck rolls ($200-500 each) for issues that don't require a site visit. Homeowners whose systems underperform and who get no explanation or resolution from their installer.

## Impact If Fixed
Reduces unnecessary diagnostic truck rolls by 40-60%, saving $100K-300K annually for a mid-size installer doing 500+ installations per year. Enables accurate production guarantee accounting — installers can determine whether shortfalls trigger guarantee payments or are weather-related. Resolves customer underperformance complaints with data-driven diagnosis instead of guesswork, improving customer satisfaction scores by 15-25%.
