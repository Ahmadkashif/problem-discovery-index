# Community Solar Programs

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/solar-installers|Solar Installers]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1-2B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~5% and growing rapidly
**Digital Adoption:** Low — community solar subscriber management is done through custom databases, spreadsheets, and manual billing reconciliation. No turnkey subscriber management platform dominates the market.
**Target Buyer:** Community solar developer, subscriber manager, or program administrator
**Automation Potential:** High — subscriber management, credit allocation, and billing reconciliation follow structured rules that are currently implemented manually

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Community solar allows renters, condo owners, and homeowners with shaded or unsuitable roofs to subscribe to a share of a centralized solar array and receive credits on their utility bill. The business model is fundamentally different from rooftop solar: the developer builds a 1-5 MW array, sells subscriptions to 100-500 individual customers, and the utility allocates production credits to each subscriber's bill based on their subscription share. The operational complexity is in subscriber management — tracking subscription allocations, processing subscriber changes (move-ins, move-outs, credit reassignments), reconciling utility credit allocations against subscription agreements, and managing the billing relationship between the developer, subscribers, and the utility. Subscriber churn is the business's primary risk: when a subscriber moves or cancels, their share must be quickly reallocated or the developer loses revenue on unsubscribed capacity.

## Current Tools & Gaps
No dominant community solar subscriber management platform exists. PowerMarket and Arcadia handle portions of the workflow. Most developers use custom-built databases or heavily customized CRM systems (Salesforce with community solar adaptations). Subscriber billing reconciliation — verifying that the utility allocated the correct credit amount to each subscriber based on their subscription share and the array's actual production — is typically done in Excel by a dedicated billing analyst.
