# HTS Classification Guide for Handmade Products

**Niche:** [[niches/customs-brokers/indigenous-artisan-importers/profile|Indigenous & Artisan Product Importers]]
**Industry:** [[industries/customs-brokers|Customs Brokers]]
**Type:** Buy & Customize (Vertical Adaptation)
**One-liner:** HTS classification databases (Descartes, USITC) are designed for commercial products with standardized descriptions — a "hand-woven basket made from palm fronds and natural dyes by indigenous artisans" doesn't match any standard product description in the classification lookup, and the duty rate difference between "baskets" (free) and "handbags" (8-20%) is material for thin-margin fair trade importers.
**Tags:** #bert #large-language-models #compliance #revenue-impact

## The Problem
Handmade products often fall between HTS classifications in ways that manufactured products don't. A handwoven bag made from seagrass: is it a basket (Chapter 46, often duty-free), a bag (Chapter 42, 4-20% duty), or a textile article (Chapter 63, varying rates)? The answer depends on the product's "essential character" — a judgment call that HTS lookup tools don't guide the user through. An experienced customs broker applies the General Rules of Interpretation to determine the classification; an artisan importer who self-classifies (to save brokerage fees) often picks the wrong code and either overpays duty or faces a CBP reclassification that results in a penalty.

## What Already Exists
Descartes CustomsInfo and the USITC's online HTS database provide classification lookup by keyword or HTS code. CBP's CROSS database contains rulings on specific product classifications. None of these tools provide category-specific guidance for handmade products where the classification hinges on material, construction method, and essential character analysis.

## The Customization Gap
A handmade product classification guide needs: (1) a curated database of CBP rulings on handmade and artisan product classifications (there are hundreds of rulings on baskets, bags, textiles, pottery, and jewelry); (2) a decision tree that guides the user through essential character analysis for common artisan product types; (3) material-based classification guidance (seagrass vs. palm vs. cotton vs. leather changes the chapter); (4) duty rate comparison showing the financial impact of classification alternatives.

## Target Customer
Artisan importers who self-classify to save brokerage fees, and customs brokers who handle artisan imports and need quick reference for handmade product classification.

## Impact If Solved
Correct classification prevents duty overpayment (5-20% on misclassified artisan products) and CBP reclassification penalties ($500-$5,000 per incident). For an artisan importer doing $200K in annual imports, correct classification saves $10K-$40K in duty — potentially the difference between a viable and non-viable business.
