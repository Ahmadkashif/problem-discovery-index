# Small Importer & E-Commerce Sellers

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/customs-brokers|Customs Brokers]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $400-600M
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~10%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — small importers (Alibaba/1688 buyers, Amazon FBA sellers, DTC brands sourcing overseas) have no customs knowledge, no understanding of HTS classification, and typically discover they need a customs broker when their first shipment is held at the port
**Target Buyer:** E-commerce seller or small business owner importing for the first time, or the freight forwarder who recommends a broker to them
**Automation Potential:** Medium-High — for standard consumer goods imports, the customs process is largely predictable and could be significantly simplified for non-expert importers

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
The explosion of small-scale importing — driven by Alibaba, 1688, direct-from-factory purchasing, and Amazon FBA sourcing — has created a large population of importers who have zero customs knowledge. They don't know what an HTS code is, don't understand duty rates, don't know what a customs bond is, and often don't realize they need a licensed customs broker until their $15,000 container is sitting at the port with a CBP hold. These importers are economically unattractive to traditional brokers: the brokerage fee on a $3,000 entry is $150-$250, but the hand-holding required (explaining what an ISF is, what a bond is, why they need a POA) consumes 2-3 hours of broker time. Many small importers are served by freight forwarders who bundle brokerage as a black box.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Flexport provides a tech-forward import experience but targets mid-market and up ($100K+ annual import spend). Customs brokers serve small importers reluctantly. No platform provides the self-service import education and simplified customs workflow that would make small importers profitable to serve at scale.
