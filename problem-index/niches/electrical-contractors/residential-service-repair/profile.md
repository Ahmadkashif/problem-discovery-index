# Residential Service & Repair

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/electrical-contractors|Electrical Contractors]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $65-70B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~30%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — ServiceTitan adoption is growing but most shops under 10 employees still use paper invoices, whiteboards for scheduling, and Excel for pricing. Flat-rate pricebooks are often photocopied binders updated annually if at all.
**Target Buyer:** Residential electrical service company owner (typically a master electrician who started the company)
**Automation Potential:** High — service calls follow repeatable diagnostic patterns, pricing is structured around pricebook entries, and upselling is a pattern recognition task that experienced technicians do intuitively

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Residential electrical service is a dispatch-driven business: a homeowner calls about a tripped breaker, a flickering light, or a dead outlet, and a technician drives to the home, diagnoses the problem, prices the repair from a flat-rate book, and completes the work in 1-3 hours. The revenue model depends on two things — call volume and average ticket size. Average ticket increases primarily through upselling: a technician called for an outlet repair who identifies that the panel is a fire-risk Federal Pacific Stab-Lok unit and recommends a $3,500 panel upgrade. Experienced technicians generate 2-3x the revenue of juniors not because they work faster, but because they identify and close larger scope work. This upsell judgment — scanning a panel, noticing aluminum wiring, recognizing knob-and-tube in an attic — is pure tacit knowledge that takes 5-10 years to develop. The industry also faces a massive EV charger installation wave: every Level 2 home charger requires a 240V circuit and often a panel upgrade, creating a new service category that didn't exist five years ago.

## Current Tools & Gaps
ServiceTitan dominates the mid-market ($400+/month per tech) with dispatching, flat-rate pricebook, invoicing, and basic reporting. Housecall Pro and Jobber serve smaller shops at lower price points but with less electrical-specific functionality. All three handle scheduling and invoicing but none optimize pricing — the pricebook is a static document. No tool helps junior technicians identify upsell opportunities; that knowledge transfer happens through ride-alongs with experienced techs that take months. Estimating labor for panel upgrades and whole-house rewires is still mental math based on experience.
