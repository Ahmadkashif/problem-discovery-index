# AI Panel Inspection from Photos

**Niche:** [[niches/electrical-contractors/panel-inspection-diagnostics/profile|Panel Inspection & Diagnostics]]
**Industry:** [[industries/electrical-contractors|Electrical Contractors]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** Upload 3-5 photos of an open electrical panel and receive an immediate assessment — manufacturer recall status, double-tapped breakers, heat damage, wire gauge mismatches, and code violations — with severity ratings and NEC references, replacing the tacit knowledge that takes master electricians a decade to develop.

## The Problem
Panel inspection is the highest-value tacit knowledge task in electrical contracting. A master electrician with 15 years of experience opens a panel cover and in 10 seconds identifies: this is a Federal Pacific Stab-Lok panel (fire risk, recommend replacement — $3,500-5,000 job), there are three double-tapped breakers (code violation, $150-300 per fix), the main feed wire shows heat discoloration (overload history, investigate load balance), there's 14-gauge wire on a 20A breaker in position 7 (fire risk, replace breaker with 15A), and there's green oxidation on the neutral bus bar (water intrusion, seal panel). A junior electrician on the same panel sees "it's a panel" and maybe catches the most obvious double-tap. A home inspector sees "it's old" and notes "recommend electrician evaluation." This knowledge gap means dangerous conditions go unidentified in millions of homes inspected by less experienced professionals. The data collection challenge is severe: training a model requires paired data of panel photos with expert annotations, and experts disagree on severity 20-30% of the time. The labeling challenge is compounded by visual variability — panels are photographed in basements with poor lighting, at angles, with varying degrees of dirt and age.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
Three barriers. First, data: there is no existing labeled dataset of electrical panel photos with defect annotations. Building one requires photographing thousands of panels with cover removed (which requires a licensed electrician) and having multiple experts annotate each photo (expensive and slow). Second, visual complexity: panels vary enormously by manufacturer, age, configuration, and installation quality — the model must generalize across hundreds of panel types. Third, liability: if the system misses a defect that causes a fire, the product liability exposure is significant. This means the system must achieve very high recall (catching nearly all real defects) even at the cost of some false positives, and it must be positioned as an assistive tool, not a replacement for professional judgment.

## What to Build
A mobile application where the user (electrician, home inspector, property manager) takes 3-5 photos of an open panel: full panel view, close-up of breakers, close-up of wire terminations, and panel label/manufacturer plate. The system runs three CNN models: (1) manufacturer identification — classifies the panel brand and model from visual features and label OCR, cross-referencing a database of recalled and fire-risk panels (Federal Pacific, Zinsco, Pushmatic, Challenger with specific date codes); (2) defect detection — object detection model identifying double-tapped breakers, wire gauge mismatches (visual diameter comparison), heat discoloration, corrosion, improper knockouts, missing cable clamps; (3) severity scoring — each detected defect is rated by severity (safety hazard / code violation / maintenance recommendation) with NEC article references. Output is a structured inspection report with annotated images highlighting each finding, suitable for inclusion in a home inspection report or service proposal.

## Target Customer
Home inspectors performing 200-400 inspections per year who want to provide better electrical assessments without being licensed electricians. Residential electrical service companies training junior technicians. Property management companies assessing electrical condition across hundreds of units. Insurance companies evaluating electrical risk during underwriting inspections.

## Impact If Built
Raises the defect identification rate for non-expert panel inspectors from 20-40% to 80-90%, catching dangerous conditions (Federal Pacific panels, double-taps, wire gauge mismatches) before they cause fires. Accelerates junior electrician training from 5-10 years of pattern recognition development to immediate baseline competency. Creates a standardized, documented panel assessment that can be compared across inspectors and tracked over time.
