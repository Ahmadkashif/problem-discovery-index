# AI Labor Hour Estimator

**Niche:** [[niches/electrical-contractors/estimating-job-costing/profile|Estimating & Job Costing]]
**Industry:** [[industries/electrical-contractors|Electrical Contractors]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** A system that takes project parameters — residential/commercial, new construction/retrofit, number and type of circuits, wall construction, access conditions — and produces labor hour estimates calibrated to the specific firm's actual productivity data, replacing the NECA-tables-plus-gut-feel approach that determines whether a job makes or loses money.
**Tags:** #tacit-knowledge-ml #gradient-boosting #feature-engineering #evaluation-metrics #cross-validation #decision-trees #data-integration #revenue-impact

## The Problem
Labor hour estimation is the highest-stakes tacit knowledge task in the electrical contracting business. An estimator bidding a 50,000 SF commercial office buildout must estimate labor for 500+ line items: receptacles, switches, lighting fixtures, panels, feeders, conduit runs, fire alarm devices, data outlets. NECA publishes standard labor units, but these are averages across the industry — a specific firm's actual productivity varies by 0.5x to 2.0x depending on crew experience, tool inventory, work conditions, and project complexity. The experienced estimator applies project-specific multipliers: "This is a retrofit in an occupied building — add 40% for protection of existing finishes and after-hours work." "This is a hospital — add 30% for infection control and escort requirements." "This crew is running at 115% of NECA book on new construction." These adjustments are learned over 10-20 years of estimating and comparing bids to actual job costs. An estimator who consistently misses by 10% in either direction costs the firm hundreds of thousands of dollars annually — underbidding loses money on won jobs, overbidding loses jobs to competitors.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
The training data exists inside every electrical contracting firm but is never structured for analysis. To build a firm-specific labor model, you need: (1) detailed bid breakdowns by line item (not just total hours, but hours per task category); (2) actual job cost data at the same level of detail (hours by task category, not just total hours per job); (3) project characteristic metadata (building type, construction type, access conditions, crew composition). Most firms have #1 in their estimating software and #3 in their project files, but #2 — actual hours by task category — is rarely tracked. Time cards show total hours per day per worker, not hours per task. This means the feedback loop from actual performance back to estimates is broken. Building the model requires instrumenting job cost tracking to capture hours at the task category level, which requires changing how foremen and journeymen report their time.

## What to Build
A two-phase platform. Phase 1 (data collection): a foreman-friendly mobile time tracking app where workers log hours against task categories (rough-in, trim, panel termination, conduit installation, wire pull, testing) per project, building the firm-specific labor database over 12-18 months. Phase 2 (estimation): a gradient-boosted model trained on the firm's own labor data, with project characteristics as features (building type, construction type, wall type, access conditions, ceiling height, union vs. non-union, crew experience level). The estimator enters project parameters and the system produces firm-calibrated labor estimates with confidence intervals: "Panel termination: 6.2 hours (5.1 - 7.8 at 90% confidence). Your firm runs 15% faster than NECA book on panel work in new construction." The model explains its adjustments: "Added 35% for retrofit in occupied space based on 12 similar projects in your history." Over time, as the firm completes more projects with detailed cost tracking, the model's accuracy improves.

## Target Customer
Electrical contractors doing $3M+ in annual revenue with dedicated estimators, bidding 10-50 projects per year. The ROI case requires enough project volume to build a meaningful training dataset and enough revenue at risk from estimating errors to justify the investment in structured time tracking.

## Impact If Built
Reduces labor estimation error from typical +/- 15-25% to +/- 5-10%, avoiding $50K-200K annually in overbid losses and underbid margin erosion. Preserves institutional estimating knowledge when experienced estimators retire — the model captures their adjustments as learned patterns. Reduces estimating time by 30-50% on repeat project types where the model has high confidence.
