# Estimating & Job Costing

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/electrical-contractors|Electrical Contractors]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $3-5B (embedded across all electrical contracting segments)
**Share of Parent Industry:** Touches all segments — every bid, proposal, and job involves estimating and post-completion cost analysis
**Digital Adoption:** Medium — ConEst and Accubid (Trimble) handle material takeoff for commercial work, but labor estimation (the profit-determining variable) is manual. Residential estimating has no standard tool.
**Target Buyer:** Estimator or EC owner responsible for bidding and job profitability
**Automation Potential:** Very High — labor hour estimation is the most experience-dependent business function in electrical contracting. The difference between open-wall and finished-wall labor, conduit vs. Romex routing, and simple vs. complex panel configurations creates multiplicative estimation complexity that experienced estimators handle intuitively.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Electrical estimating is a two-part problem: material takeoff (counting every device, fixture, wire run, and panel from plans or site surveys) and labor application (estimating how many hours each item will take to install). Material takeoff is largely solved by ConEst and Accubid for commercial work — they digitize plans and count devices. Labor application is where the money is made or lost, and it remains an art. NECA publishes standard labor units (e.g., "Install duplex receptacle in existing wall: 0.35 hours"), but these are industry averages. A specific firm's actual installation time depends on their crew experience, tool inventory, access conditions, wall construction type, and work environment. An experienced estimator adjusts NECA units by 0.5x to 2.0x based on project specifics — "this is a hospital renovation, so add 30% for infection control barriers and escort requirements" or "this is new construction with open walls, so take 40% off the NECA unit." These adjustments are pure experience-based judgment. When an experienced estimator retires, the firm's bid accuracy drops immediately.

## Current Tools & Gaps
ConEst and Accubid handle commercial material takeoff and apply NECA labor units, but the labor unit adjustments that determine profitability are manual. Residential estimating tools are minimal — most residential ECs estimate from experience, sometimes using flat-rate pricebooks. No tool analyzes historical job cost data (estimated vs. actual hours) to improve future estimates. The job cost variance analysis that would identify systematic estimating errors — "we consistently underestimate labor on finished-wall work by 25%" — is almost never performed because the data isn't structured for analysis.
