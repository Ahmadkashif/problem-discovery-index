# Small & Rural Electricians

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/electrical-contractors|Electrical Contractors]]
**Category:** Underserved

## Profile
**Market Size:** $10-15B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~6%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — most 1-3 person shops run on paper invoices, phone-based scheduling, and handwritten estimates. QuickBooks is the most common "software" in use, and it's used only for invoicing and tax prep.
**Target Buyer:** Owner-operator of a 1-3 person electrical shop serving a rural or small-town market
**Automation Potential:** Medium — the core need is lightweight business management, not advanced ML. But material procurement optimization has real automation potential given the long drive times to supply houses.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
A rural electrician is a generalist by necessity: they wire new homes, troubleshoot agricultural equipment (grain dryers, irrigation pumps, livestock ventilation), install commercial lighting in small-town businesses, and handle service calls across a geographic area that might span 50-100 miles. They are often the only licensed electrician for miles, which means no competition but also no specialization — every call is different. The business model is fundamentally different from urban residential or commercial shops: lower call density means more drive time per job (30-60 minutes each way is normal), limited supply house access means ordering materials 2-3 days ahead or driving an hour to pick up, and the customer relationship is personal (these are neighbors, not anonymous homeowners). ServiceTitan at $400+/month per technician is absurd for a one-person shop doing $150-300K in annual revenue. These electricians need a tool that costs $50-100/month and runs entirely on a phone.

## Current Tools & Gaps
ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, and Jobber are designed for multi-truck operations and priced accordingly. Jobber is the closest fit at $49-129/month but still assumes a dispatcher-technician model that doesn't apply to a one-person shop. QuickBooks handles invoicing but not scheduling, job costing, or customer management. Google Calendar handles scheduling but doesn't connect to invoicing. Many rural electricians use nothing — they keep a spiral notebook in the truck, give handwritten estimates, and send invoices from QuickBooks at the end of the month. For NEC code reference, they carry a physical codebook in the truck. For material pricing, they call the supply house.
