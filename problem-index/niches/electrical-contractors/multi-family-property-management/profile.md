# Multi-Family Property Management Electrical

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/electrical-contractors|Electrical Contractors]]
**Category:** Underserved

## Profile
**Market Size:** $8-12B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~5%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — property management software (AppFolio, Buildium, Yardi) tracks maintenance requests but has no electrical-specific functionality. Electrical compliance status across hundreds of units is tracked in spreadsheets or not tracked at all.
**Target Buyer:** Property management company, multi-family building owner, or the electrical contractor who holds the building's service contract
**Automation Potential:** High — unit turnover electrical inspection is a repeatable checklist, code compliance tracking across hundreds of units is a structured data problem, and common-area lighting/power maintenance follows predictable schedules

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Multi-family electrical work is a recurring-revenue business distinct from one-time residential service calls. A property management company or building owner needs three categories of electrical work: (1) unit turnover — when a tenant moves out, the electrical system must be inspected and brought to current code before the next tenant moves in (GFCI protection in kitchens/bathrooms, AFCI protection in bedrooms per current NEC, functional smoke/CO detectors, no tenant-caused damage like painted-over outlets or overloaded circuits); (2) common area maintenance — hallway and parking lot lighting, elevator power, emergency generator testing, fire alarm system testing; (3) code upgrade projects — older buildings may need building-wide GFCI retrofits, panel upgrades in units still on original 60A or 100A service, or aluminum wiring remediation. The electrician serving this market needs to track compliance status across hundreds of units, maintain inspection records for liability protection, and coordinate access with property managers and tenants.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Property management platforms (AppFolio, Buildium, Yardi) handle maintenance work orders, tenant communication, and vendor management. They can assign a "fix broken outlet in Unit 304" work order to an electrician, but they have no concept of electrical compliance status, inspection history, or code requirements. The electrician's field service tool (if they have one) tracks jobs but not building-level compliance. No tool tracks "which of these 200 units has GFCI protection in the kitchen?" or "when was the last time each unit's smoke detectors were tested?" This information, when it exists, lives in the property manager's spreadsheet or the electrician's memory.
