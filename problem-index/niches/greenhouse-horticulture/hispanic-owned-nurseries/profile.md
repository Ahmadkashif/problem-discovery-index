# Hispanic-Owned Nurseries & Growers

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/greenhouse-horticulture|Greenhouse Horticulture]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $2-4B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~15%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — language barriers, immigration status concerns, and tools designed exclusively for English-speaking operators create compounded barriers to technology adoption
**Target Buyer:** Owner / Patrón at a family-owned nursery or greenhouse operation, often first or second generation
**Automation Potential:** Medium — the same operational functions (crop planning, sales, compliance) need automation, but delivery must account for Spanish-first interfaces, multigenerational family business dynamics, and cash-heavy business models

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Hispanic-owned nurseries and greenhouses represent a substantial share of US greenhouse production, particularly in the ornamental and landscape plant segments in California, Texas, Florida, and the Southeast. Many of these operations were started by immigrants with deep horticultural expertise from Mexico's greenhouse sector and have grown from backyard operations into 2-20 acre commercial nurseries. The owners operate in Spanish as their primary business language, employ family members and Spanish-speaking workers, and often sell through a combination of cash-based transactions at on-site retail, informal wholesale relationships with landscapers (often also Hispanic-owned), and occasional broker sales to garden centers. Technology tools designed for English-speaking, credit-card-transacting, ERP-using commercial growers are culturally and linguistically inaccessible. USDA programs and extension services that could support these businesses have historically underserved non-English-speaking operators.

## Current Tools & Gaps
No greenhouse management, POS, or crop planning tool offers a Spanish-language interface designed for this market. Generic Spanish-language POS systems exist (Square has a Spanish mode) but lack plant-specific features. USDA's Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS) provides Spanish-language outreach in some regions but technology transfer programs assume English literacy and computer proficiency. The result: profitable, growing nursery businesses running on paper, cash registers, and mental note-keeping — not because the owners resist technology, but because no technology speaks their language or fits their business model.
