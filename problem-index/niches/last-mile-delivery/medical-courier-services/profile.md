# Medical Courier Services

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/last-mile-delivery|Last-Mile Delivery]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $5-7B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~6%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — most medical couriers use basic dispatch software or phone/text-based coordination; chain-of-custody tracking is often paper-based; HIPAA compliance adds barriers to adopting general-purpose delivery tools
**Target Buyer:** Medical courier fleet owner or dispatch coordinator
**Automation Potential:** Medium — chain-of-custody documentation, temperature logging for specimens, and regulatory compliance tracking are automatable, but the time-sensitivity and handling requirements of medical specimens limit full automation

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Medical couriers transport laboratory specimens, pharmaceuticals, medical devices, blood products, and medical records between hospitals, clinics, labs, and pharmacies. This segment operates under unique constraints: chain-of-custody requirements (every handoff must be documented for regulatory compliance), specimen viability time limits (blood draws must reach the lab within 2-4 hours, some within 30 minutes), HIPAA compliance for patient-identified specimens, temperature requirements for biologics, and no-fail delivery obligations (a failed delivery of a surgical instrument delays a procedure). Medical couriers are paid premiums ($15-50 per delivery vs. $3-8 for parcel delivery) but face regulatory liability that parcel carriers don't encounter.

## Current Tools & Gaps
MedSpeed and Stat providers offer enterprise medical logistics. Basic dispatch tools (Onfleet, Tookan) handle routing but lack chain-of-custody tracking, specimen viability timers, HIPAA-compliant documentation, or integration with hospital/lab systems. No purpose-built platform exists for small-to-mid-size medical courier operations (5-20 drivers) that combines dispatch, chain-of-custody, temperature logging, and regulatory compliance.
