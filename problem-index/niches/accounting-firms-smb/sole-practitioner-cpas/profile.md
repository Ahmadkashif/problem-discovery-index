# Sole Practitioner CPAs

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/accounting-firms-smb|SMB Accounting Firms]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $15-20B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~15%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — many still run desktop tax software (Drake, ProSeries), use local file storage instead of cloud portals, and manage client communication through personal email and phone calls
**Target Buyer:** Solo CPA running a home office or small-suite practice with 75-200 clients
**Automation Potential:** Medium — the practitioner is the bottleneck for every task, making any automation directly impactful but adoption is slow due to cost sensitivity and tech resistance

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Sole practitioners represent roughly 40% of all CPA firms by count but capture only 15% of revenue. They operate fundamentally differently from multi-staff firms: there is no delegation, no review layer, no administrative support. The CPA does everything — intake, preparation, review, client communication, billing, marketing. Technology adoption is driven by personal preference and budget, not by firm policy. Many practitioners are 50+ years old and have run their practice on the same desktop software for 15-20 years. The buying decision is made by one person, the switching cost is felt personally, and the tolerance for complexity is near zero.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Desktop installations of Drake, ProSeries, or TaxAct dominate. Some have moved to cloud-based TaxDome (an all-in-one practice management + portal tool designed for solo practitioners) or Canopy. The gap is that most solo CPAs have not adopted any practice management tool at all — they track deadlines in personal calendars, store files on local drives or Dropbox, and communicate through a mix of personal email, text messages, and phone calls. No single tool integrates the full solo-practitioner workflow at a price point and simplicity level that drives adoption.
