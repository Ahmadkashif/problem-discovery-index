# Grant Research & Opportunity Matching

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/grant-writers|Grant Writers]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1.1B (time cost pool)
**Share of Parent Industry:** 10% (of total grant writer time)
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — Writers use Grants.gov, Foundation Directory, and Google — but systematic opportunity matching is manual.
**Target Buyer:** Grant writer or development director
**Automation Potential:** High — Grant opportunity databases are structured, and matching against organizational profiles follows rule-based and pattern-based logic.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Grant opportunity research — finding grants that match a client's mission, geography, eligibility, and funding needs — consumes 30-40% of a grant writer's time but generates zero direct revenue (it's pre-proposal work, often unbilled or underpriced). A grant writer serving a youth mentoring nonprofit must monitor Grants.gov for federal opportunities, Foundation Directory Online for private foundations, state grant portals for state funding, and Google Alerts for corporate giving programs. Each source has different search interfaces, different update schedules, and different data formats. The writer reviews 50-100 opportunities per week to identify 3-5 worth pursuing. This curation work is high-volume, pattern-based, and poorly compensated.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Grants.gov provides federal opportunity search. Foundation Directory Online (Candid) covers 140,000+ foundations. Instrumentl and GrantStation offer grant database search. These tools list opportunities but don't match them against a client's specific profile with learning-over-time intelligence. A writer searching "youth mentoring" gets 200 results and manually evaluates each for fit — geography, funding range, eligibility, deadline, and alignment with the client's specific approach. No tool says "here are the 5 best matches for THIS client, ranked by likelihood of success."
