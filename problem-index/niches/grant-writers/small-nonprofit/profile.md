# Small Nonprofit Grant Writers

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/grant-writers|Grant Writers]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1.8B
**Share of Parent Industry:** 16%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Solo practitioners and small firms serving small nonprofits use Word, Google Docs, and email with almost no grant-specific tooling.
**Target Buyer:** Solo grant writer or 2-3 person firm serving nonprofits with budgets under $5M
**Automation Potential:** High — Small-grant proposals follow simpler templates and shorter formats than federal grants, making them more amenable to AI-assisted drafting.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Small nonprofit grant writers serve organizations with $500K-5M annual budgets — food banks, community centers, after-school programs, domestic violence shelters, and environmental advocacy groups. These nonprofits pursue grants of $5,000-100,000 from local foundations, community trusts, and corporate giving programs. The proposals are shorter (2-10 pages) but the volume is higher — a small nonprofit might submit 30-50 grant applications per year to sustain operations. The grant writer's challenge is throughput: producing high-quality proposals at volume for clients who cannot afford premium rates ($50-100/hour vs. $150-300/hour for federal grant specialists).

## Current Tools & Gaps
Small nonprofit grant writers use Google Docs for writing, Google Sheets for deadline tracking, Gmail for funder communication, and Google Drive for file storage. Some use GrantHub ($50-100/month) for basic tracking. No tool is designed for the high-volume, small-grant workflow: rapid proposal drafting from templates, deadline management across 30-50 concurrent applications, client-shared workspace for review and approval, or standardized outcome reporting that satisfies multiple funders from one data set.
