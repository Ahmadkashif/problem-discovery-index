# Rural Permitting Offices

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/municipal-services|Municipal Services]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $6B
**Share of Parent Industry:** 2%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Most rural counties still issue permits on multi-part carbon forms, track inspections on paper calendars, and store plans in rolled-up paper tubes.
**Target Buyer:** County Clerk / Permit Technician / Building Official
**Automation Potential:** High — Permit application intake, fee calculation, inspection scheduling, and certificate of occupancy issuance are rule-based workflows with clear decision trees.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Rural and exurban counties (populations under 25,000) process building, electrical, plumbing, and mechanical permits with 1–3 staff who may also serve as the zoning administrator, floodplain manager, and septic system inspector. Applicants — typically homeowners, small contractors, and farmers — drive to the courthouse to submit paper applications. The building official may cover a 500-square-mile territory, scheduling inspections by phone and routing by memory. Plan review happens on paper prints spread across a desk. The volume is low (200–2,000 permits per year) but the complexity is high: rural properties have well/septic systems, flood zones, agricultural exemptions, and road access issues that urban permitting software doesn't model.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Enterprise permitting platforms (Tyler Energov, Accela, OpenGov) cost $100K–$500K and are designed for jurisdictions processing 5,000+ permits annually. Community-developed tools like OpenCounter focus on business licensing, not building permits. General-purpose form tools lack the multi-step workflow (application → plan review → permit issuance → inspection scheduling → final inspection → certificate of occupancy) and the jurisdiction-specific fee calculations, setback requirements, and flood zone overlays that rural permitting requires.
