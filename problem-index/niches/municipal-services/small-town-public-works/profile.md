# Small-Town Public Works

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/municipal-services|Municipal Services]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $45B
**Share of Parent Industry:** 13%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Most towns under 10,000 people have a public works director who is also the road crew foreman, using a whiteboard and a pickup truck to manage operations.
**Target Buyer:** Public Works Superintendent / Town Manager
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Work order management, road condition tracking, and equipment maintenance scheduling are automatable, but the small team size limits ROI of complex systems.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
In towns under 10,000 residents, "public works" means 2–8 employees who do everything: plow snow, patch potholes, mow road shoulders, maintain storm drains, manage the transfer station, fix park benches, and respond to sewer backups. There's no IT department, no procurement officer, and no budget for enterprise software. The superintendent is a hands-on worker who also handles scheduling, procurement, citizen complaints, and council reports. Decision-making is based entirely on institutional memory and driving around looking at what needs attention. When the superintendent retires, the town loses decades of knowledge about which culverts flood, which roads frost-heave first, and which equipment needs parts.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Most small-town public works departments use no software beyond email and maybe QuickBooks. A few have tried Cartegraph or Lucity (now Hexagon) but found them designed for cities 10x their size, requiring dedicated admin staff they don't have. Google Sheets serves as the work order system. Equipment maintenance is tracked by oil change stickers on the dashboard. Road condition assessments exist only in the superintendent's head.
