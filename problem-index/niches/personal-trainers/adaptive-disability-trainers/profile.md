# Adaptive & Disability Trainers

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/personal-trainers|Personal Trainers]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $400M
**Share of Parent Industry:** 2%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Exercise libraries in training apps assume able-bodied clients. No platform provides adaptive exercise alternatives, wheelchair-based movements, or disability-specific programming constraints.
**Target Buyer:** Adaptive Fitness Specialist
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Exercise adaptation suggestions and accessibility-constrained programming are automatable; individual assessment and rapport-building require intensive human interaction.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Adaptive fitness trainers work with clients who have physical disabilities (spinal cord injuries, amputations, cerebral palsy, multiple sclerosis, stroke recovery), intellectual/developmental disabilities, or sensory impairments. This is the most underserved population in personal training: 61 million US adults have a disability, but fewer than 5% of certified personal trainers have any adaptive fitness training. The technology gap is total — every exercise library, every programming template, every progress tracking tool assumes an able-bodied client. An adaptive trainer must mentally modify every standard exercise to accommodate their client's specific capabilities, often inventing movements on the fly.

## Current Tools & Gaps
No mainstream training platform (Trainerize, TrueCoach, TrainHeroic) includes adaptive exercise libraries or disability-specific programming features. The ACSM and NSCA publish adaptive exercise guidelines in textbook form, not software. A few organizations (Adaptive Training Academy, MedFit Network) provide education but not tools. The trainer improvises everything: exercise selection, progression protocols, equipment modifications, and progress metrics that are meaningful for clients whose functional baselines differ radically from able-bodied norms.
