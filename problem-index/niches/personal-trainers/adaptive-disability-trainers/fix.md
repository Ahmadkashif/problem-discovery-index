# Exercise Demo Videos Assuming Able-Bodied Execution

**Niche:** [[niches/personal-trainers/adaptive-disability-trainers/profile|Adaptive & Disability Trainers]]
**Industry:** [[industries/personal-trainers|Personal Trainers]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Every exercise demo video in every training app shows an able-bodied person performing the movement — an adaptive client assigned a "modified push-up" has no visual reference for what that looks like from a wheelchair, with one arm, or with limited range of motion.
**Tags:** #computer-vision #multimodal #worker-facing

## The Problem
When an adaptive trainer assigns an exercise through Trainerize or TrueCoach, the client sees a demonstration video of an able-bodied person performing the standard version. For a client in a wheelchair assigned a "seated chest press with band," the demo video shows a person sitting on a bench doing a cable chest press — similar in concept but completely different in execution. The client can't learn the movement from the video; they depend entirely on the trainer's in-person demonstration. This limits remote/online adaptive training (the client has no reference between sessions) and forces trainers to spend session time on instruction that a good demo video would handle.

## Why It's Still Broken
Creating adaptive exercise demo videos requires recruiting performers with specific disabilities, filming in accessible facilities, and producing variations for each disability type and exercise — a massive content production effort that no fitness technology company has undertaken because the market is too small to justify the investment. Some adaptive fitness organizations have produced limited YouTube content, but it's unstructured, unsearchable, and not integrated into any training platform. The result is that the most underserved client population also has the worst instructional content.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A crowdsourced adaptive exercise video library that: (1) partners with adaptive fitness trainers and their clients to film real adaptive exercise demonstrations (with client consent), (2) tags videos by disability category, equipment used, movement pattern, and difficulty level, (3) integrates into existing training platforms so that when a trainer assigns an adaptive exercise, the client sees a demo performed by someone with a similar disability, (4) includes multiple variations per exercise showing different adaptation levels, and (5) provides a contribution platform where trainers can upload their own demo videos.

## Who Feels the Pain
Adaptive fitness clients who receive exercise assignments with irrelevant demo videos and must memorize movements during sessions rather than referring to video guidance between sessions, and trainers who spend 30-50% of session time re-teaching movements because clients can't practice from videos.

## Impact If Fixed
Enables remote/online adaptive training (currently impractical for most adaptive clients), recovers 20-30% of session time currently spent on movement re-instruction, and makes adaptive fitness more accessible by providing visual references that help clients exercise independently between sessions.
