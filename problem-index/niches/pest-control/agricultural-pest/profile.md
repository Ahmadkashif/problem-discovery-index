# Agricultural Pest Management

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/pest-control|Pest Control]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $3B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~15%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — Precision agriculture tools exist for large-scale row cropping, but pest-specific scouting, threshold-based treatment decisions, and IPM compliance documentation remain manual for most operations.
**Target Buyer:** Crop pest management consultants and commercial pesticide applicators serving farms and orchards.
**Automation Potential:** High — Pest scouting follows grid-based sampling protocols, threshold calculations are rule-based, and spray application records are regulatory requirements.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Agricultural pest management operates at a completely different scale and regulatory framework than structural pest control. Consultants scout fields for insect pests, disease symptoms, and weed pressure, then make threshold-based treatment recommendations (spray only when pest populations exceed economic injury levels). The work is seasonal, weather-dependent, and governed by EPA pesticide application regulations (restricted use pesticides, buffer zones, worker protection standards). Revenue comes from scouting contracts and applicator fees. The relationship between pest consultant and farmer is advisory — the consultant recommends, the farmer decides.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Precision agriculture platforms (Climate FieldView, John Deere Operations Center) handle field mapping and equipment telemetry. Crop scouting apps (Agworld, Agrivi) digitize field observations. Neither category integrates scouting data with pest identification, economic threshold calculations, and pesticide selection with label compliance checking. Most pest consultants use paper scouting forms, reference books for identification, and memory for thresholds.
