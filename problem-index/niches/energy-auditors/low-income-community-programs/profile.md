## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
The Department of Energy's Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP) funds energy audits and retrofits for low-income households through a network of ~800 community action agencies (CAAs). These agencies use DOE-mandated audit tools (NEAT for site-built homes, MHEA for manufactured homes) that have not been substantially updated since the 1990s. The audit-to-retrofit pipeline is heavily regulated: auditors must follow DOE-specified protocols, use approved measure lists, document health and safety items, and pass state-level QA inspections. Wait lists for weatherization services average 12-24 months due to administrative bottlenecks, not funding shortages — many CAAs cannot process applications and complete audits fast enough to spend their annual allocation.

## Current Tools & Gaps
NEAT and MHEA are the mandated audit tools, running as desktop applications with 1990s-era interfaces. WxPROE was developed as a more modern alternative but has limited adoption. Neither integrates with field data collection tools, client intake systems, or state reporting databases. CAAs use separate systems for client eligibility determination (income verification, DOE 400.1 Priority List), audit data, production tracking, and state reporting — typically 4-5 disconnected systems plus spreadsheets. The result is that administrative processing per unit takes as long as the actual field work.
