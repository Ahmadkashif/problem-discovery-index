# Utility Weatherization Programs

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/energy-auditors|Energy Auditors]]

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1.5-2B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~30-40%
**Digital Adoption:** Medium — Utilities use program management platforms (CLEAResult, ICF portals), but auditors interface with these through manual form submissions; no seamless data flow from field to program database
**Target Buyer:** Utility program manager / weatherization agency director
**Automation Potential:** High — Utility programs have standardized audit protocols, defined measure lists, and structured QA requirements that lend themselves to automated data validation and reporting

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Utility-funded weatherization programs (Home Performance with ENERGY STAR, utility rebate programs, ratepayer-funded efficiency initiatives) represent the largest single source of residential energy audit demand. These programs define specific audit protocols (BPI, RESNET), approved measure lists, rebate schedules, and quality assurance requirements that auditors must follow precisely. The audit is not just a service — it is a compliance-driven workflow where deviations from program requirements trigger audit rejection, delayed payments, and potential contractor decertification. Auditors working in utility programs submit 50-200 audits per year per program, each requiring standardized data collection, modeling, and reporting that matches the program's specific templates and QA criteria.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Utilities manage programs through implementation contractors (CLEAResult, ICF, Franklin Energy) who maintain portals for project submission and tracking. Auditors use TREAT, Ekotrope, or REM/Rate for energy modeling, then manually transfer results into program portal submission forms. No tool automates the flow from field data collection through energy modeling to program-compliant submission. QA checks happen after submission, catching errors weeks later that could have been flagged during data entry.
