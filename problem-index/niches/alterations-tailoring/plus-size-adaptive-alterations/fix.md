# Service Refusal & Price Discrimination

**Niche:** [[niches/alterations-tailoring/plus-size-adaptive-alterations/profile|Plus-Size & Adaptive Clothing Alterations]]
**Industry:** [[industries/alterations-tailoring|Alterations & Tailoring]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Plus-size customers and disabled customers are routinely refused service or charged 50-200% markups by alteration shops that lack the skills or willingness to serve them — creating a trust crisis that suppresses demand.
**Tags:** #data-integration #recommendation #quick-win #worker-facing

## The Problem
Plus-size consumers report being turned away by alteration shops ("we don't work with that size"), charged undisclosed surcharges when they arrive for pickup ("it took more fabric/time"), or receiving poor results from tailors who apply standard-size techniques to extended-size garments. Disabled customers face similar barriers: refusal because the modification "sounds complicated," pricing that reflects the tailor's uncertainty rather than actual labor, and results that prioritize appearance over functionality (the garment looks fine but can't be put on independently). These experiences are humiliating, discouraging, and well-documented in plus-size and disability communities online.

## Why It's Still Broken
Most tailors aren't malicious — they're uncertain. They've been trained on standard sizes, they don't know how to estimate time and materials for work they've never done, and they'd rather refuse than do a bad job. The surcharging happens because they underestimated the work at intake and try to recover at pickup — a practice that feels like discrimination even when it's just poor estimation. There's no skills training, no pricing guidance, and no quality standards for plus-size or adaptive alteration work — so quality varies wildly between the few shops willing to try.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A two-sided fix: (1) a directory of vetted, plus-size-friendly and adaptive-capable alteration shops (verified by community reviews, not self-reported), so customers can find welcoming providers without the emotional labor of cold-calling shops and risking refusal; (2) a training and pricing guide for shops that want to expand into this market — covering the specific techniques, additional time estimates, and transparent pricing models that make plus-size and adaptive work profitable and repeatable. Shops that complete the training earn a directory listing.

## Who Feels the Pain
Plus-size and disabled consumers who need alteration services but have been burned by refusal or discrimination, and tailors who want to serve these customers but lack the confidence, skills, and pricing framework to do so profitably.

## Impact If Fixed
Expands the accessible alteration market by 30-50% for underserved consumers, creates a $50-100/garment premium service category for shops that invest in the skills, and reduces the emotional harm of service refusal — a significant quality-of-life issue for affected communities.
