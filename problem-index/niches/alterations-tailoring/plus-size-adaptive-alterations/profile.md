# Plus-Size & Adaptive Clothing Alterations

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/alterations-tailoring|Alterations & Tailoring]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $340M
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~5%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — No alteration tools account for extended sizing or adaptive modification needs; practitioners share knowledge via disability advocacy forums and plus-size fashion communities.
**Target Buyer:** Tailors specializing in plus-size garments or adaptive clothing modifications for people with disabilities
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Measurement systems and fit assessment tools need extension for non-standard body types; the alteration work itself is manual and highly skilled.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Standard alteration techniques assume garments designed for a size 0-14 body — the geometry of fabric, dart placement, and structural reinforcement changes fundamentally for extended sizes. A size 28 dress alteration involves different fabric behavior, weight distribution, and structural considerations than a size 8. Adaptive clothing modifications — converting standard garments for wheelchair users (seated cut), adding magnetic closures for people with limited dexterity, or adjusting hemlines for prosthetic limbs — require specialized knowledge that most tailors lack. These customers are currently underserved, often refused by shops that "don't work with that size range," and charged premium prices when they do find a willing tailor.

## Current Tools & Gaps
No alteration software accounts for extended sizing — measurement templates, fit assessment tools, and pricing calculators all assume standard size ranges. Adaptive modification techniques are taught through occupational therapy networks and disability organizations, not through tailoring education. There's no reference library of adaptive modification techniques, no directory of tailors experienced in plus-size or adaptive work, and no measurement tools designed for non-standard body geometries.
