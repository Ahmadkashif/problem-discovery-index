# Custom Tailoring & Bespoke

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/alterations-tailoring|Alterations & Tailoring]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $890M
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~14%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Master tailors use paper patterns, hand-recorded measurements, and physical fabric swatches; no digital tool captures their craft knowledge.
**Target Buyer:** Master tailor or bespoke clothier creating made-to-measure or fully bespoke garments
**Automation Potential:** Low-Medium — Pattern grading and measurement management have digital potential; the craft of cutting, fitting, and hand-finishing is irreducibly manual and tacit.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Custom tailoring is the highest-skill, highest-price segment of the alterations industry. A bespoke suit involves 15-25 measurements, a custom pattern drafted for the individual body, 2-4 fittings, and 40-80 hours of hand construction. The master tailor's knowledge is deeply tacit: they see how fabric drapes on a body and know where it will pull, wrinkle, or gap — before any cutting happens. This visual-spatial expertise takes 10-15 years to develop, and there are fewer master tailors every year as the craft ages out. The digitization opportunity isn't replacing the tailor — it's capturing and transmitting their knowledge.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Master tailors use paper measurement sheets, physical paper patterns (often hand-drafted and irreplaceable), fabric swatch books from mill representatives, and handwritten client cards maintained for decades. No digital system captures the relationship between measurements, body shape observations, fabric behavior, and pattern adjustments that constitute the tailor's expertise. CAD tools for fashion (Gerber, Lectra) are designed for mass production, not individual bespoke work.
