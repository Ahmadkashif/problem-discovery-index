# Pricing & Turnaround Estimation

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/alterations-tailoring|Alterations & Tailoring]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** Cross-segment (applies to all alterations businesses quoting prices and delivery dates)
**Share of Parent Industry:** Cross-cutting operational function
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Pricing is mental math by the tailor at intake; turnaround estimation is a guess based on current workload and gut feel.
**Target Buyer:** Any alterations business owner quoting prices and turnaround times at intake
**Automation Potential:** High — Pricing based on alteration type, garment complexity, and market data, plus turnaround based on current WIP, are both rule-based and data-driven.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Every garment that enters an alterations shop requires two assessments at intake: how much will this cost, and when will it be ready. Both assessments are made by the tailor using experience and judgment — there's no calculator, no rate card (or the rate card is outdated and incomplete), and no workload visibility system. Pricing inconsistency (the same alteration quoted at $15 on Monday and $20 on Thursday, or different prices by different staff members) erodes customer trust. Turnaround overcommitment (promising 3 days when the shop has 80 garments in the queue) creates missed deadlines that damage reputation.

## Current Tools & Gaps
No alteration-specific pricing or scheduling tool exists. Shops that have pricing guidance use laminated cards or handwritten lists that cover basic alterations but not the thousands of garment-specific variations (hemming pants is $12, but hemming lined wool trousers with a blind stitch is $22). Turnaround estimation is entirely in the tailor's head — they glance at the rack, estimate the queue, and guess. There's no system that shows current work-in-progress count, average completion rate, or capacity available for new commitments.
