# Bridal & Formal Wear Alterations

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/alterations-tailoring|Alterations & Tailoring]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1.8B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~28%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — Some bridal shops use wedding CRM tools but the alterations workflow itself (fitting notes, pinning documentation, multi-session tracking) is paper-based.
**Target Buyer:** Bridal shop owner with in-house alterations, or independent seamstress specializing in bridal and formal wear
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Multi-fitting scheduling and alteration tracking are automatable; the fitting judgment and construction work are deeply tacit.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Bridal alterations are the highest-stakes work in the alterations industry: the garment is irreplaceable (often $1,000-10,000+), the deadline is absolute (the wedding date), and the emotional pressure is extreme (the bride's vision of how she'll look on her biggest day). A bridal gown typically requires 2-4 fittings over 2-3 months, with alterations including hemming (often with multiple layers of tulle, lace, and satin), bodice adjustments, bustle construction, strap modifications, and bead/lace detail work. Each fitting builds on the last, creating a multi-session project with zero tolerance for error.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Bridal shops use wedding CRM tools (Aisle Planner, Planning Pod) for the sales side but manage alterations with paper fitting tickets pinned to each gown. Independent bridal seamstresses track clients in notebooks and communicate via text. No tool models the bridal alterations pipeline: multi-fitting scheduling with dependencies (fitting 2 can't happen until alteration 1 is complete), construction notes per fitting session, photo documentation of fitting progression, and deadline tracking working backward from the wedding date.
