# Budget & Invoice Reconciliation

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/event-planning|Event Planning]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $6B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~5%
**Digital Adoption:** Medium — Planners use spreadsheets for budgets and QuickBooks for invoicing, but the reconciliation between budgeted amounts, vendor invoices, and client billing is entirely manual.
**Target Buyer:** Event planners and corporate event managers tracking multi-vendor budgets against actuals across 10-40 events per year.
**Automation Potential:** High — Budget-to-actual comparison, vendor invoice matching, and client billing reconciliation follow standardizable patterns.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Event budgets are living documents that change 15-30 times between initial planning and final settlement. A wedding budget starts at $40,000, adds $3,000 for upgraded linens, drops $1,500 when the couple cuts a course, adds $2,000 for a late RSVP increase, and so on. Each change must be tracked against the master budget, communicated to the client (with updated total), and reconciled against vendor invoices post-event. Post-event reconciliation is the worst: the planner receives 15-25 vendor invoices over 2-4 weeks, must match each line item to the budgeted amount, verify accuracy (was the $800 linen charge for 25 tables or 30?), and compile the final client settlement showing budget vs. actual.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Excel/Google Sheets handle event budgets. QuickBooks handles invoicing and payments. Honeybook and Dubsado handle client contracts and payments. None connect budget line items to vendor invoices for automated reconciliation. The planner manually cross-references the budget spreadsheet with each vendor invoice, identifies discrepancies, and builds a final settlement document — a process that takes 3-5 hours per event.
