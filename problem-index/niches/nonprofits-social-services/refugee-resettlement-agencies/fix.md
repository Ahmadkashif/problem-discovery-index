# Housing Search for Large Families

**Niche:** [[niches/nonprofits-social-services/refugee-resettlement-agencies/profile|Refugee Resettlement Agencies]]
**Industry:** [[industries/nonprofits-social-services|Nonprofits Social Services]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Resettlement agencies spend 20-40 hours per large family (5+ members) searching for affordable housing that meets ORR standards, because rental listing platforms don't filter by the criteria that matter: landlord willingness to accept refugees, unit size, proximity to public transit, and lease flexibility.
**Tags:** #data-integration #workflow-orchestration #worker-facing #quick-win

## The Problem
Resettlement agencies must secure housing before a family arrives — typically 5-10 days' notice. For families of 5-8 (common in Afghan and Congolese populations), finding a 3-4 bedroom apartment at $1,200-$1,800/month that accepts a new-to-the-US family with no credit history, no rental history, and an agency co-sign is extremely difficult. Housing coordinators call 30-50 landlords per family, facing high rejection rates. They maintain informal lists of "refugee-friendly landlords" in personal notebooks, and when a staff member leaves, the institutional knowledge of which landlords will work with resettlement agencies leaves with them.

## Why It's Still Broken
Apartment listing platforms (Zillow, Apartments.com) don't track landlord willingness to accept refugee families or agency co-signs. The informal "friendly landlord" databases are maintained individually by each affiliate office and not shared across the network. No one has built a structured landlord relationship database because resettlement agencies view housing search as an unavoidable manual process rather than a systematizable workflow.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A shared landlord relationship database where housing coordinators log every landlord interaction — contacted, interested, leased, declined (with reason). The database tracks landlord capacity (number of units, sizes available), pricing, acceptance of agency co-signs, proximity to bus routes and schools, and historical reliability (did they maintain the unit, return deposits). When a new family arrives, the coordinator searches by family size and budget, sees ranked landlord options based on past success, and contacts 3-5 landlords instead of 30.

## Who Feels the Pain
Housing coordinators who spend entire weeks calling landlords, facing repeated rejection, and racing against the family's arrival date — one of the most stressful and time-consuming roles in resettlement.

## Impact If Fixed
Reduces housing search from 30 hours to 8 hours per family, decreases temporary hotel placements (costing $150-$250/night) by 50%, and preserves institutional knowledge when housing coordinators turn over.
