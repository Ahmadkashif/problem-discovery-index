# Inter-Examiner Reliability Crisis in Subluxation Detection

**Niche:** [[niches/chiropractic-practices/palpation-digitization/profile|Palpation & Tactile Assessment Digitization]]
**Industry:** [[industries/chiropractic-practices|Chiropractic Practices]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Two chiropractors examining the same patient find different subluxations — with inter-examiner reliability studies showing agreement rates as low as 40-60% — undermining the profession's scientific credibility and creating clinical inconsistency.
**Tags:** #gradient-boosting #binary-classification #tabular-ml #tacit-knowledge-ml #compliance

## The Problem
The chiropractic profession's foundational diagnostic method — motion palpation to detect subluxations — has consistently poor inter-examiner reliability in research studies. Two experienced DCs examining the same patient may agree on which segments are subluxated only 40-60% of the time. This isn't because palpation doesn't work — it's because each DC is detecting different tactile signals, interpreting them through different training frameworks, and applying different diagnostic criteria. When a patient sees a new DC, they may receive a completely different diagnosis and treatment plan — eroding patient trust and making it impossible to build a standardized evidence base.

## Why It's Still Broken
Chiropractic education teaches palpation through apprenticeship — students feel patients under supervision, receive verbal feedback ("feel deeper, toward the left"), and gradually develop sensitivity. The problem is that different instructors teach different palpation techniques, different diagnostic frameworks (Gonstead, Diversified, Activator, Upper Cervical), and different interpretive criteria. There has been no way to standardize what a "subluxation" feels like because there's no way to measure what each DC is actually detecting with their hands. Without objective measurement, standardization is impossible.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A multi-modal assessment protocol that combines palpation with objective measurement tools (sEMG, thermography, range of motion, algometry) to create a composite subluxation score. The composite score is validated against expert consensus (a panel of experienced DCs who agree on findings). Over time, the protocol identifies which objective measurements most closely correlate with expert palpation consensus, creating a reproducible assessment standard. Junior DCs follow the protocol to produce assessments that agree with expert consensus 80-90% of the time — even before their palpation skills are fully developed.

## Who Feels the Pain
Patients who receive different diagnoses from different chiropractors and lose confidence in the profession. Chiropractic researchers who can't conduct reliable studies because diagnostic assessment varies between examiners. Insurance companies that deny chiropractic coverage citing lack of diagnostic reliability.

## Impact If Fixed
Improves inter-examiner reliability from 40-60% to 80-90% for participating practices. Provides the evidence base needed for insurance coverage expansion and medical community acceptance. Reduces patient confusion from conflicting diagnoses, improving retention and referral rates.
