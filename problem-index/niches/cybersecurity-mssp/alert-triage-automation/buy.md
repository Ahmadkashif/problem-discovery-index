# Context-Enriched Alert Deduplication

**Niche:** [[niches/cybersecurity-mssp/alert-triage-automation/profile|Alert Triage & Escalation Automation]]
**Industry:** [[industries/cybersecurity-mssp|Cybersecurity MSSP]]
**Type:** Buy & Customize (Vertical Adaptation)
**One-liner:** SIEM platforms like Splunk and Sentinel group duplicate alerts by rule ID, but the same underlying incident generates alerts across multiple detection rules, log sources, and time windows — producing 20-50 separate tickets for what an analyst recognizes as one event.
**Tags:** #k-means #anomaly-detection #tabular-ml #automation #data-integration

## The Problem
A brute-force attack against a client's VPN gateway generates: 50 "failed login" alerts from the firewall, 10 "impossible travel" alerts from Azure AD, 5 "account lockout" alerts from Active Directory, and 3 "suspicious IP" alerts from threat intelligence feeds. The SIEM correlates within each detection rule (grouping the 50 failed logins into one alert) but does not correlate across rules — the analyst receives 4 separate tickets for one incident. For a complex attack like ransomware staging, the alert sprawl can reach 20-50 tickets across endpoint, network, identity, and email detection layers. Analysts spend 30-40% of their triage time recognizing and merging related alerts rather than investigating the underlying incident.

## What Already Exists
Splunk Enterprise Security and Microsoft Sentinel offer correlation rules and incident grouping. SOAR platforms provide case management with manual alert linking. But correlation rules are static — they match specific rule-ID combinations defined by the SIEM administrator. When a new detection rule is added or a new log source is integrated, the correlation rules must be manually updated to include the new source. Dynamic, cross-source alert grouping that recognizes "these 15 alerts from 5 different sources are all about the same incident" without pre-defined correlation rules does not exist in production SIEM platforms.

## The Customization Gap
The adaptation requires: (1) cross-source entity linking (associating alerts that share common entities — IP addresses, user accounts, hostnames — even when generated by different detection rules), (2) temporal clustering (grouping alerts within a time window that represents a single incident, accounting for detection latency differences across sources), (3) attack-stage mapping (recognizing that a phishing alert + credential compromise alert + lateral movement alert represent stages of a single attack, not three separate incidents), (4) per-client entity context (the same IP address may be benign for one client and malicious for another), and (5) incident deduplication metrics (reducing 50 tickets to 3 incidents with full alert lineage preserved for investigation).

## Target Customer
MSSPs running Splunk or Sentinel across 50+ clients, where SOC analysts spend 30-40% of their time recognizing and manually merging related alerts.

## Impact If Solved
Reduces effective alert volume by 60-70% through intelligent deduplication. Converts 50 individual alerts into 3-5 actionable incidents with complete context. Saves 15-20 hours of analyst time per week per 10,000 daily alerts. Improves mean time to detect (MTTD) by surfacing the full attack chain immediately rather than requiring manual correlation.
