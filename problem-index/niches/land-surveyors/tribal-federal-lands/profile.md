## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Surveying on federal and tribal lands operates under a separate legal and administrative framework from private land surveying. Federal surveys follow the Bureau of Land Management's Manual of Surveying Instructions (the "Manual"), which prescribes specific monument types, procedures, and record-keeping standards. Tribal land surveys add layers of jurisdictional complexity: trust land, fee land, allotted land, and restricted fee land each have different survey requirements and approval processes. Records are maintained by BLM, the Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA), and tribal land offices — three separate record systems that often don't communicate. The surveyors working this niche must understand federal survey law, tribal sovereignty, and the historical context of allotment-era boundaries.

## Current Tools & Gaps
BLM uses its own internal survey management systems (GeoCommunicator, NILS) for cadastral records, but these are not accessible to private surveyors. BIA land records are maintained in the Trust Asset and Accounting Management System (TAAMS), which is notoriously difficult to access. Tribal land offices vary from sophisticated GIS operations to paper-only filing cabinets. Private surveyors contracted for federal or tribal work must navigate three record systems, none of which integrate with commercial survey software. The gap is fundamental: the records needed for federal and tribal surveys exist in systems designed for federal administration, not surveyor access.
