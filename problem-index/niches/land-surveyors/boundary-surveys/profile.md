## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Boundary surveys are the core service of the surveying profession — determining where one property ends and another begins. This is not a measurement problem; it's an evidence-weighing problem. The surveyor must locate physical monuments (iron pins, stone markers, witness trees), compare them against the recorded legal description (metes and bounds, lot-and-block), evaluate occupation evidence (fences, walls, hedgerows, mowing lines), and resolve conflicts between these sources using centuries of case law (the "rules of construction"). A senior surveyor walks a property line and "reads" the landscape — they see a depression where a fence post rotted 40 years ago, they notice a tree line that follows a bearing from an 1890 deed. This tacit knowledge takes 10-20 years to develop.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Field measurement uses GPS/GNSS (Trimble, Leica) and robotic total stations. Office work uses CAD (AutoCAD, Carlson Survey, MicroSurvey) for drafting plats. Data collectors (Trimble Access, Leica Captivate) manage field points. But no tool helps with the interpretive work — weighing monument evidence against deed language, evaluating occupation patterns, or applying the hierarchy of calls (natural monuments > artificial monuments > courses and distances > area). The gap is between measurement technology (excellent) and boundary interpretation technology (nonexistent).
