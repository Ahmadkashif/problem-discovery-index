# Foreign Rights Departments

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/independent-publishers|Independent Publishers]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $600M
**Share of Parent Industry:** 5%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — Rights tracking is done in spreadsheets; deal memos are Word documents emailed between parties; territorial licensing maps exist only in the rights manager's head.
**Target Buyer:** Rights Manager / Foreign Rights Director
**Automation Potential:** High — Rights availability tracking, deal memo generation, royalty collection from sub-licensees, and territory mapping are rule-heavy and highly repetitive.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Foreign rights — selling the right to translate and publish a book in other languages/territories — is one of the highest-margin revenue streams for independent publishers, yet it is managed with the most primitive tools. A rights manager at a mid-size indie press oversees rights for 200–500 backlist titles across 30+ language territories, maintaining relationships with 50–200 foreign publishers. For each title, they must track: which territories have been sold, to whom, for how long, whether the licensee published within the contractual window, whether royalty payments are current, and when rights revert. This is a database problem managed in spreadsheets, with the rights manager as the single point of failure.

## Current Tools & Gaps
IPR License and PubMatch facilitate rights deal discovery at book fairs. Consonance and Klopotek have rights modules, but they're part of enterprise publishing platforms priced for large houses. Most indie rights managers use a master Excel spreadsheet (the "rights grid") showing titles vs. territories with deal status in each cell. This spreadsheet has no version control, no automated reversion tracking, no royalty collection reminders, and no way to generate a territory availability report for a foreign publisher who asks "what do you have available in Korean?" without manual filtering. When the rights manager leaves, the spreadsheet is the only institutional memory — and it's often incomplete.
