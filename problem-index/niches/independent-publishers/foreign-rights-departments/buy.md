# Rights Deal CRM with Book Fair Integration

**Niche:** [[niches/independent-publishers/foreign-rights-departments/profile|Foreign Rights Departments]]
**Industry:** [[industries/independent-publishers|Independent Publishers]]
**Type:** Buy & Customize (Vertical Adaptation)
**One-liner:** HubSpot and Salesforce track deals but don't model the rights-specific pipeline — interest expressed at book fair → sample chapters sent → offer received → contract negotiated → advance paid → publication confirmed → royalties flowing.
**Tags:** #workflow-orchestration #data-integration #automation #revenue-impact

## The Problem
A rights manager maintains relationships with 100–200 foreign publishers across 30+ countries. Interactions happen in concentrated bursts (book fairs in October/March) and slow drips (email follow-up between fairs). Notes from fair meetings are scribbled in a notebook, transferred to a spreadsheet (sometimes), and followed up on (inconsistently). The pipeline from initial interest to signed contract typically spans 3–12 months with multiple touchpoints. Without systematic tracking, the rights manager relies on memory to follow up — and deals worth $5K–$20K in advances fall through the cracks because nobody sent the sample chapters or responded to the offer.

## What Already Exists
HubSpot, Salesforce, and Pipedrive manage sales pipelines with stages, tasks, and contact tracking. These tools can be configured for rights sales, but the configuration burden is high and the publishing-specific terminology doesn't map cleanly to generic CRM concepts. IPR License provides a marketplace for rights discovery but not ongoing relationship management. PubMatch connects publishers but doesn't track deal flow. None of these tools model the rights-specific pipeline stages or integrate with book fair schedules (Frankfurt, Bologna, London, BookExpo) to auto-create meeting records.

## The Customization Gap
A rights CRM needs: (1) publisher contact records with territory, genre preferences, and past deal history, (2) a pipeline with rights-specific stages (pitch → sample sent → offer received → negotiation → contract signed → advance received → publication confirmed), (3) book fair integration that auto-creates meeting notes templates for each scheduled meeting and links them to the publisher record, (4) automated follow-up sequences ("send sample chapters 3 days after fair meeting," "follow up on outstanding offers at 30/60/90 days"), (5) deal tracking with currency conversion for multi-currency advances and royalties, and (6) a view that shows all active deals by territory to prevent double-selling. The key difference from generic CRM: each "deal" has a unique combination of title + territory + format rights, not just a dollar amount.

## Target Customer
Rights managers at indie publishers managing 50+ active rights relationships and attending 2+ international book fairs per year.

## Impact If Solved
Systematic follow-up captures 20–30% more deals from book fair meetings by preventing dropped conversations. Reduces post-fair follow-up time from 40 hours to 10 hours through templated communication. Identifies $20K–$50K in annual lost revenue from forgotten follow-ups and expired offers.
