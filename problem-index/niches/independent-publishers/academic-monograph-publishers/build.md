# Monograph Peer Review and Editorial Board Workflow Platform

**Niche:** [[niches/independent-publishers/academic-monograph-publishers/profile|Academic Monograph Publishers]]
**Industry:** [[industries/independent-publishers|Independent Publishers]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** No platform manages the monograph-specific peer review process — finding qualified reviewers for book-length manuscripts, managing 3–6 month review timelines, and coordinating editorial board votes on acquisition.
**Tags:** #workflow-orchestration #recommendation #nlp #data-integration #ai-agent

## The Problem
When a university press receives a monograph proposal, the acquisitions editor must identify 2–4 qualified peer reviewers — scholars in the exact subfield who have no conflicts of interest with the author, haven't reviewed for the press recently, and are willing to read a 300-page manuscript for a $200 honorarium. This search is done by memory, Google Scholar, and asking colleagues. Once reviewers agree, the 3–6 month review period begins — editors send reminders, chase late reviews, and manage the back-and-forth between author and reviewers. When reviews are complete, the proposal goes to the faculty editorial board for a vote, which requires assembling a packet of reviews, financial projections, and market analysis. Each press manages 20–60 of these reviews simultaneously, all tracked in email and spreadsheets.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
Journal peer review platforms (Editorial Manager, ScholarOne) are optimized for article-length manuscripts with 2-week review cycles and large reviewer pools. Monograph review is fundamentally different: book-length documents, months-long timelines, small reviewer pools in niche subfields, and an editorial board governance layer that journals don't have. The university press market (150 presses, average 30 titles/year) is too small for the large peer-review platform vendors to build a separate product. And each press has slightly different board governance procedures, making standardization challenging.

## What to Build
A workflow platform with: (1) a reviewer database that tracks past reviewers, their subfield expertise, response rates, and review quality ratings, with AI-assisted suggestions for new reviewers based on manuscript topic and citation networks, (2) a review management dashboard showing all active reviews with status, deadline, and reminder history, (3) automated reminder sequences calibrated to the monograph timeline (not the journal cadence), (4) editorial board packet generation that assembles reviews, acquisition rationale, and financial projections into a formatted document, and (5) a board vote tracking system with outcome recording. Designed for 1–5 acquisitions editors managing 20–60 simultaneous reviews.

## Target Customer
Acquisitions editors and directors at university presses and independent scholarly publishers managing 20+ peer reviews per year.

## Impact If Built
Reduces reviewer identification time from 2–3 hours to 15 minutes per manuscript. Cuts average peer review cycle from 5 months to 3 months through systematic follow-up. Saves acquisitions editors 8–12 hours per week on review management and board preparation. Shortens time-to-publication by 4–6 months — critical for authors whose tenure cases depend on timely publication.
