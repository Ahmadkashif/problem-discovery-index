# Regional Nonfiction Presses

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/independent-publishers|Independent Publishers]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $800M
**Share of Parent Industry:** 6%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Many operate from a home office with email, Word documents, and a checkbook. No CRM, no metadata management, no digital marketing tools.
**Target Buyer:** Owner-Publisher
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Distribution tracking, local bookstore outreach, and event coordination are repetitive but currently done entirely by hand.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Regional nonfiction presses publish books about local history, regional culture, nature guides, trail guides, cookbooks, and place-based memoir — titles that sell primarily within a geographic area and through local bookstores, gift shops, museums, and author events. These are typically 1–3 person operations run by a passionate publisher who does everything: acquisitions, editing, design (or outsourcing it), print management, distribution, marketing, bookkeeping, and event coordination. Revenue per title is modest ($10K–$50K lifetime), but backlist titles can sell steadily for decades — a trail guide to the Appalachian Mountains published in 2005 still sells 200 copies per year. The challenge is that the publisher is too busy producing books to market them.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Most regional presses use Word for editing, email for communication, a local printer for production, and a car trunk for distribution. Some use IngramSpark for print-on-demand and Amazon for online sales. Few have websites beyond a basic Squarespace page. None use CRM tools to track bookstore relationships, event schedules, or consignment inventory. The gap between what these publishers do (everything) and the tools they use (nothing purpose-built) is enormous.
