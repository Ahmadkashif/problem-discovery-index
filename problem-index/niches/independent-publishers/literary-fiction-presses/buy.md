# Production Pipeline Tracker for Small Editorial Teams

**Niche:** [[niches/independent-publishers/literary-fiction-presses/profile|Literary Fiction Presses]]
**Industry:** [[industries/independent-publishers|Independent Publishers]]
**Type:** Buy & Customize (Vertical Adaptation)
**One-liner:** Asana and Monday.com can track tasks but don't model the 12–18 month book production pipeline with its publishing-specific stages, freelancer handoffs, and catalog season deadlines.
**Tags:** #workflow-orchestration #data-integration #quick-win

## The Problem
A literary press producing 20 titles per year has 20 books in simultaneous production at different stages: manuscript editing, copyediting, proofreading, cover design, interior layout, ARC production, catalog copy, metadata submission (ONIX), print procurement, and warehouse delivery. Each stage involves a different freelancer with a different contract and deadline. The editorial director tracks all of this in a spreadsheet with 200+ rows, manually updating dates and chasing freelancers via email. When a copyeditor runs late, the cascade effect on layout, printing, and catalog season isn't visible until it's too late — and the book misses its publication date.

## What Already Exists
Asana, Monday.com, and Basecamp handle project management with task dependencies and timeline views. Consonance and Bibliocloud are publishing-specific platforms with production tracking. The generic tools don't understand publishing stages (they can be configured, but each press reinvents the wheel). The publishing-specific tools are priced for mid-to-large publishers — $500–$2,000/month — and include features (warehouse management, EDI ordering) that a 5-person press doesn't need and finds overwhelming.

## The Customization Gap
A literary press needs: (1) pre-built production stage templates (manuscript → developmental edit → copyedit → proofread → layout → ARC → print → ship) with standard durations that auto-calculate based on pub date, (2) freelancer management with contract status, payment tracking, and automatic reminders, (3) ONIX metadata generation that pulls from the production record rather than requiring duplicate entry, (4) catalog season awareness (Fall, Spring) with hard deadlines for metadata submission to distributors, and (5) a view that shows all 20 titles simultaneously with bottleneck highlighting. Price point: $100–$300/month for a team of 3–10.

## Target Customer
Editorial directors and production managers at independent presses publishing 10–50 titles per year with 3–15 staff.

## Impact If Solved
Reduces production delays by 30%, ensuring 95%+ of titles hit their pub dates (vs. a typical 75–80%). Saves the editorial director 5–8 hours per week on status tracking and freelancer follow-up. Prevents the $5K–$15K cost of a missed catalog season (rush printing, lost co-op placement, delayed reviews).
