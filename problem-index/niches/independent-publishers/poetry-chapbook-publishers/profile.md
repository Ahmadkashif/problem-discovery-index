# Poetry & Chapbook Publishers

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/independent-publishers|Independent Publishers]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $150M
**Share of Parent Industry:** 1%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Most poetry presses operate from a kitchen table with email, a word processor, and a relationship with a local letterpress printer.
**Target Buyer:** Editor-Publisher (often a single person)
**Automation Potential:** Low-Medium — Contest management and subscriber fulfillment are the most automatable workflows; editorial and design are inherently artisanal.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Poetry and chapbook publishers (Copper Canyon, Wave Books, Black Ocean, and hundreds of micro-presses) operate at the smallest viable scale in commercial publishing. A typical press publishes 4–12 titles per year with print runs of 500–2,000 copies. Revenue comes from a combination of book sales, contest entry fees, grants (NEA, state arts councils), and subscriptions. The editor-publisher is often a poet themselves, running the press as a cultural mission rather than a business. Production quality matters intensely — poetry readers care about paper stock, typography, and binding in ways that fiction readers don't. The combination of tiny scale, artisanal production, grant-dependent revenue, and passionate-but-non-technical operators makes this niche invisible to software vendors.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Submittable is the one widely-adopted tool — used for contest submissions and open reading periods. Beyond that, most poetry presses use Gmail, Word, and handwritten logs. Print production involves a direct relationship with a short-run printer (often letterpress for chapbooks). Distribution is split between direct sales (website, readings, AWP conference) and SPD (Small Press Distribution) or Ingram. No tool handles the full poetry press workflow: contest management with fee processing, subscriber list management, grant reporting, or the unique production requirements of poetry layout (line breaks, white space, page turns that affect meaning).
