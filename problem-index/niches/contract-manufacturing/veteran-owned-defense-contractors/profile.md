# Veteran-Owned Defense Contractors

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/contract-manufacturing|Contract Manufacturing]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $8-12B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~2-3%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — basic ERP is common but ITAR compliance, DFARS cybersecurity requirements (CMMC), and government contract management are handled through manual processes and consultants
**Target Buyer:** Program manager or contracts officer at service-disabled veteran-owned small businesses (SDVOSBs) holding defense manufacturing contracts
**Automation Potential:** Medium — ITAR export control compliance, DFARS cybersecurity self-assessment, and government contract reporting follow defined regulatory procedures amenable to automation

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Veteran-owned small businesses receive procurement preferences under federal acquisition regulations (FAR), with dedicated set-aside programs for SDVOSBs. These shops — typically 20-100 employees — win defense contracts but face regulatory compliance requirements designed for large contractors: ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations) export control, DFARS cybersecurity requirements (evolving toward CMMC Level 2 certification), and Cost Accounting Standards for government contracts. A 50-person machine shop winning its first DoD contract must suddenly implement export control procedures, cybersecurity infrastructure, and government-specific accounting practices — compliance domains that require specialized knowledge these businesses don't have and consultants charge $200-$500/hour to provide.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Standard job shop ERP (E2 Shop System, JobBOSS) handles production management. ITAR compliance is managed through written procedures and manual access controls. CMMC cybersecurity readiness is assessed through DIB-SCC self-assessment questionnaires. Government contract cost accounting is typically outsourced to specialized accountants. No integrated platform helps SDVOSBs manage the intersection of production operations and defense-specific regulatory compliance — the unique burden that defense manufacturing places on small businesses.
