# Small-Run Consumer Goods CMs

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/contract-manufacturing|Contract Manufacturing]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $25-35B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~6-8%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — many run on paper travelers, manual scheduling, and spreadsheet-based costing with no MES and minimal ERP usage
**Target Buyer:** Shop floor manager or owner-operator at contract manufacturers producing consumer products in runs of 500-10,000 units
**Automation Potential:** Medium — job tracking, costing, and scheduling are automatable but product variety and short runs limit the ROI of heavy automation investment

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Small-run consumer goods CMs produce everything from cosmetics and supplements to household products and consumer electronics accessories — typically for DTC brands, Amazon sellers, and small CPG companies. Runs are short (500-10,000 units), product changeovers are frequent (3-8 per day), and the CM manages 50-200 active products simultaneously. Unlike medical device or aerospace CMs, there are minimal regulatory documentation requirements (except for FDA-regulated cosmetics and supplements), so the economic value of digitization comes from production efficiency and costing accuracy rather than compliance. These shops operate on razor-thin margins (3-8% EBITDA) and compete on price, speed, and flexibility.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Many use QuickBooks for invoicing, spreadsheets for scheduling and costing, and paper travelers (printed job sheets that accompany each batch through production) for tracking. Some use basic ERP (Fishbowl, inFlow Inventory) for inventory management. No affordable, lightweight production management platform exists for the specific needs of high-changeover, short-run consumer goods manufacturing — where the overhead of setting up a job in an ERP system can exceed the production time of the job itself.
