# Country Conditions Evidence Database with Case Law Matching

**Niche:** [[niches/immigration-law/removal-defense-asylum/profile|Removal Defense & Asylum]]
**Industry:** [[industries/immigration-law|Immigration Law]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** Build a searchable database that maps country conditions evidence (State Department reports, human rights publications, news) to asylum case law by country, claim type, and protected ground — so an attorney preparing a Guatemalan gang-based asylum claim can instantly find what evidence and arguments have succeeded before.
**Tags:** #large-language-models #transformers #attention-mechanisms #transfer-learning #tacit-knowledge-ml #compliance #revenue-impact

## The Problem
An asylum attorney preparing a case for a client from Honduras claiming persecution based on membership in a particular social group (e.g., "young men who refuse gang recruitment") must assemble two bodies of evidence: (1) country conditions showing that Honduras has a pattern of persecuting people in that social group, and (2) case law showing that immigration judges and the BIA have recognized similar social groups as valid grounds for asylum. Currently, attorneys research country conditions by searching the State Department's annual human rights report, UNHCR publications, Human Rights Watch reports, news articles, and expert declarations — each from a different source, each requiring separate searches, none cross-referenced. For case law, attorneys search Westlaw for BIA decisions and circuit court opinions involving the same country and claim type — again manually, reading dozens of decisions to find the relevant holdings. Experienced asylum attorneys have built personal libraries of country conditions evidence and winning arguments by country, organized in filing cabinets and Word documents over years of practice. This tacit expertise — knowing that "for El Salvador, the 2019 Human Rights Watch report has the strongest language on femicide" or "Judge Martinez in New York has granted 12 cases with this social group formulation" — is the difference between winning and losing an asylum case. It takes 5-10 years to develop and is lost entirely when the attorney leaves practice.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
Country conditions evidence changes constantly — a report from 2020 may be outdated by 2024 if political conditions change. The evidence must be current, which requires continuous ingestion and updating. Immigration court decisions are partially published but many trial-level decisions are unpublished and available only through FOIA requests or attorney networks. The taxonomy of claim types is fluid — "particular social group" formulations evolve through case law (what was denied in 2015 may be accepted in 2023 after a circuit court ruling). Building a system that correctly maps evidence to claim types requires deep immigration law domain knowledge to structure the taxonomy. The user base (removal defense attorneys) skews toward solo practitioners and nonprofits with minimal software budgets, making commercial viability challenging.

## What to Build
A continuously updated evidence platform with three layers: (1) Country conditions aggregator — auto-ingests State Department reports, UNHCR publications, Human Rights Watch reports, Amnesty International reports, CRS reports, and major news sources, indexed by country, topic (violence, political repression, gender-based violence, LGBTQ persecution, gang activity), and date. (2) Case law mapper — ingests BIA and circuit court asylum decisions, extracts the country, protected ground, social group formulation, and outcome, and maps each decision to the relevant country conditions evidence it cited. A transformer model fine-tuned on asylum decisions classifies claim types and extracts holdings. (3) Strategy recommender — given a new case (country, claim type, protected ground), retrieves the strongest available country conditions evidence and the most favorable case law, ranked by recency and relevance, with judge-specific outcome data where available. The attorney gets a research package in hours instead of days, grounded in the same evidence base that experienced practitioners have built over decades.

## Target Customer
Asylum attorneys and nonprofit legal services organizations handling 20+ asylum cases per year, where country conditions research currently takes 10-20 hours per case and the quality of research directly determines case outcomes.

## Impact If Built
Reduces country conditions research from 10-20 hours to 2-4 hours per case. Equalizes the quality of representation between experienced attorneys with deep country knowledge and newer attorneys or pro bono volunteers. For a nonprofit handling 50 asylum cases per year, this recovers 400-800 attorney hours annually and directly improves grant rates for the most vulnerable clients — people facing deportation to countries where they may be killed.
