## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Unaccompanied minors (children who arrive in the U.S. without a parent or legal guardian) face a unique legal pathway that involves two separate court systems operating in parallel. In immigration court, the child faces removal proceedings and may apply for asylum, Special Immigrant Juvenile Status (SIJS), or other relief. For SIJS, the child must first obtain a state court finding (in juvenile or family court) that the child was abused, neglected, or abandoned by one or both parents, that it is not in the child's best interest to return to their home country, and that reunification with the abusive parent is not viable. This state court finding is then used to support the SIJS petition (I-360) filed with USCIS. The attorney must navigate both court systems simultaneously, with interdependent deadlines — the state court finding must be obtained before the immigration petition can be filed, but the immigration court may set a hearing date before the state court proceeding is complete. Additionally, each state has different requirements for SIJS findings — what constitutes "abuse, neglect, or abandonment" varies by state law, what court has jurisdiction varies, and the procedural requirements for obtaining the finding differ across all 50 states. The attorney must research the specific requirements for the state where the child resides, often from scratch.

## Current Tools & Gaps
LegalServer is used by many legal aid organizations for general case management. The National Immigrant Women's Advocacy Project (NIWAP) and the Vera Institute publish state-specific SIJS practice advisories, but these are PDF documents that must be located and read manually. No case management system tracks dual-court proceedings with interdependent deadlines. No tool maps SIJS requirements by state in a searchable, structured format. For guardian ad litem coordination, attorneys use email and phone — there is no workflow tool for managing the GAL's involvement, report deadlines, or court appearance scheduling.
