# Multilingual Client Intake Portal with Form Field Mapping

**Niche:** [[niches/immigration-law/non-english-client-intake/profile|Non-English Client Intake]]
**Industry:** [[industries/immigration-law|Immigration Law]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** Build an intake portal where immigration clients answer questions in their native language (Spanish, Chinese, Haitian Creole, etc.) and the system maps their responses to the correct English-language USCIS form fields — eliminating the bilingual staff bottleneck and reducing intake from 2-3 hours to 30-45 minutes.
**Tags:** #large-language-models #transformers #attention-mechanisms #transfer-learning #automation #data-integration #worker-facing #revenue-impact

## The Problem
A Spanish-speaking client walks into an immigration law office for an I-485 adjustment of status case. The paralegal (who must be bilingual) sits with the client for 2-3 hours, asking each question from the intake form in Spanish, writing down the response, and later entering the English translation into Docketwise or the firm's intake system. The client provides their name as "Maria de los Angeles Garcia Lopez" — the paralegal must determine how to map this to "First Name," "Middle Name," and "Last Name" on the USCIS form (USCIS requires a specific name format that doesn't match naming conventions in many Latin American countries). The client's address in their home country doesn't follow U.S. formatting conventions. Dates are provided in DD/MM/YYYY format but USCIS requires MM/DD/YYYY. Employment history descriptions don't translate directly to the job title categories USCIS expects. Each of these mapping decisions requires judgment from a bilingual staff member who understands both the client's cultural context and USCIS form requirements. Firms without bilingual staff must hire interpreters at $50-100/hour for intake sessions, adding $100-300 per case.

## Why Nobody Has Built This
Multilingual form systems exist (Google Forms, Typeform), but they don't understand USCIS form field requirements, naming conventions across cultures, or the specific mapping rules (e.g., "mother's maiden name" is a concept that doesn't exist in some naming traditions). Building accurate cross-cultural name mapping alone requires training data from thousands of intake sessions across dozens of nationalities. Date format conversion is trivial; name format mapping is not. The system must also handle follow-up questions that depend on prior answers (e.g., if the client indicates they were ever arrested, a detailed criminal history section appears — and the follow-up questions must be in their language and culturally appropriate). LLMs now have the multilingual capability to handle this, but fine-tuning for immigration-specific terminology and form field mapping requires domain expertise.

## What to Build
A client-facing intake portal (mobile-first, since many clients will complete intake on their phones before the office visit) with four components: (1) multilingual questionnaire — the client selects their language, and the intake questions appear in that language, organized by USCIS form section (not by form field number, which is meaningless to clients); (2) intelligent field mapping — an LLM layer processes the client's free-form responses and maps them to structured USCIS form fields, handling name format conversion (Latin American two-surname convention to USCIS first/middle/last), date format conversion, address format conversion, and employment description normalization; (3) attorney review interface — the attorney sees the client's original-language responses alongside the mapped English form fields, with confidence scores and flagged items requiring human judgment; (4) form population — approved field mappings flow directly into USCIS form population (Docketwise API or direct PDF filling), eliminating manual data entry. Support for Spanish, Chinese (Simplified and Traditional), Haitian Creole, Portuguese, Hindi, Arabic, Tagalog, and Vietnamese at launch.

## Target Customer
Immigration law firms where 50%+ of clients have limited English proficiency, currently relying on bilingual staff or paid interpreters for every intake session, processing 10+ new intakes per month.

## Impact If Built
Reduces intake time from 2-3 hours (bilingual paralegal) to 30-45 minutes (attorney review of pre-mapped data). Eliminates the bilingual staff bottleneck — firms can serve clients in any language, not just the languages their staff speaks. Reduces data entry errors (name format, date format, address format) that cause USCIS form rejections. For a firm processing 20 intakes per month, this saves 30-50 hours of bilingual staff time monthly ($4K-8K/month in labor cost or interpreter fees).
