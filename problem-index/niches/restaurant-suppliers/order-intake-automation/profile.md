# Order Intake Automation

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/restaurant-suppliers|Restaurant Suppliers]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $3-5B (embedded — order processing labor across independent restaurant supply)
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~6% of operating costs dedicated to order intake and processing
**Digital Adoption:** Low — over 70% of orders at independent restaurant suppliers arrive via phone call, text message, or voicemail; digital ordering portals (BlueCart, Cut+Dry) have under 30% penetration
**Target Buyer:** Operations Manager / Office Manager responsible for order entry and processing
**Automation Potential:** High — order intake from phone/text/voicemail is a well-defined NLP problem: extract customer, items, quantities, and delivery date from unstructured communication and map to catalog SKUs

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Order intake is the single most labor-intensive administrative function at independent restaurant suppliers. A typical $20M supplier receives 80-150 orders per day, of which 70%+ arrive by phone call, text message, or voicemail. Each order must be manually transcribed into the ERP — a process that takes 5-15 minutes per order and occupies 2-3 office staff members for 4-6 hours per day. The task is highly repetitive, error-prone (3-5% transcription error rate), and creates a bottleneck at order cutoff time (typically 5-6 PM for next-morning delivery). When order volume spikes (holiday weeks, catering events), the bottleneck causes missed orders, late processing, and warehouse picking delays. The irony: the information flow is simple (customer, items, quantities, date) but the communication channel (phone, text, voicemail) requires human interpretation.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Digital ordering portals (BlueCart, Cut+Dry, Pepper) offer online ordering that bypasses phone/text intake. But adoption among restaurant customers is under 30% — chefs prefer to call or text because it is faster for them and allows real-time substitution discussion. The portal does not replace phone ordering; it adds a parallel channel that the office must now manage alongside phone orders. No tool converts phone/text/voicemail orders into structured ERP entries automatically.
