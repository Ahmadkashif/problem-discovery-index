# Produce & Specialty Suppliers

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/restaurant-suppliers|Restaurant Suppliers]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $15-20B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~28%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — produce suppliers use basic ERP and lot tracking, but the buying side operates at market speed via phone calls, and the 3-5 day shelf life makes standard inventory tools inadequate
**Target Buyer:** Owner / Head Buyer at a produce and specialty food supplier serving restaurants
**Automation Potential:** High — demand forecasting for highly perishable items, supplier quality tracking, and chef menu-driven ordering are automatable but require produce-specific data models

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Restaurant-focused produce and specialty suppliers compete on freshness, quality, and the ability to source hard-to-find items (microgreens, heirloom tomatoes, exotic mushrooms, specialty herbs) that broadline distributors do not carry. The buyer is the most critical role — they visit terminal markets at 3-4 AM, evaluate product quality by sight and touch, negotiate daily spot prices, and assemble orders for 50-200 restaurant accounts that must be packed and delivered by 6 AM the next morning. The compressed timeline (buy at dawn, pack by noon, deliver by 5 AM) leaves zero margin for inventory errors. A case of arugula bought Monday morning that is not sold by Wednesday afternoon is compost. The buyer who carries a mental model of each chef's preferences, seasonal menu changes, and quality tolerance is the company's most valuable and irreplaceable asset.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Produce Pro and Edible Software handle lot tracking and invoicing. No tool helps the buyer predict daily demand by account, track which chefs are shifting their menus seasonally, or assess whether a supplier's quality has been declining over recent deliveries. The buyer's market intelligence and customer knowledge are entirely tacit — concentrated in one person's experience.
