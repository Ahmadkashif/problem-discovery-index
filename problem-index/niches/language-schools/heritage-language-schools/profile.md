# Heritage Language Schools

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/language-schools|Language Schools]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1-2B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~7-12%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — most heritage language schools are community-run weekend or after-school programs operating on donated space with volunteer teachers, paper-based curricula, and no educational technology beyond a whiteboard
**Target Buyer:** Director of a weekend/after-school program teaching Mandarin, Arabic, Hindi, Korean, Japanese, or other heritage languages to second-generation children
**Automation Potential:** Low-Medium — the community-driven model resists heavy digitization, but curriculum management, student tracking, and family engagement are automatable

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Heritage language schools serve a specific learner profile that neither ESL programs nor foreign language courses address: children who hear the language at home (from parents/grandparents) but are dominant in English, possessing passive comprehension with limited productive skills, often with non-standard register mixing. The pedagogical approach must leverage the learner's existing receptive knowledge while developing literacy and formal register — fundamentally different from teaching a language from zero. These schools are also structurally distinct: most are nonprofit community organizations operating on Saturday mornings in borrowed church basements or community centers, staffed by volunteer parent-teachers with native fluency but no formal pedagogy training.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Most heritage language schools use paper attendance sheets, photocopied textbook pages, and WhatsApp/WeChat groups for parent communication. A few larger programs use Google Classroom for homework distribution. There are essentially no purpose-built tools for heritage language education — the market is too small and fragmented for commercial interest. Textbooks are often imported from the heritage country and assume a learner profile (monolingual child in the home country) that does not match the bilingual heritage learner in the US.
