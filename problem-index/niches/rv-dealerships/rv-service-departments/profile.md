# RV Service Departments

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/rv-dealerships|RV Dealerships]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $9B
**Share of Parent Industry:** 20%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — RV service departments use basic work order systems (IDS Service, paper tickets) but lack the digital inspection, customer communication, and parts management tools that automotive service departments have adopted.
**Target Buyer:** RV dealership service manager overseeing 5-15 technicians
**Automation Potential:** High — Warranty claim filing, parts ordering for unique RV systems, and service scheduling for oversized units are all automatable but currently manual.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
RV service is fundamentally more complex than automotive service: an RV contains automotive systems PLUS residential systems (plumbing, HVAC, electrical, roofing, cabinetry, appliances). An RV service department needs technicians with both automotive and residential construction skills. RVs are physically large — scheduling requires accounting for bay sizes and outdoor work areas. Parts come from both automotive and residential supply chains. Warranty claims involve both the chassis manufacturer (Ford, Mercedes) and the RV manufacturer (Thor, Winnebago) — two separate warranty processes for the same vehicle. All of this complexity is managed with tools designed for passenger car service.

## Current Tools & Gaps
IDS Service module handles basic work orders and scheduling for RV dealers. But it lacks: RV-specific inspection templates (roof, slide-outs, leveling systems, LP gas), dual-warranty tracking (chassis vs. coach manufacturer), bay size management for oversized units, and multi-system diagnostic workflows. Auto repair tools (Mitchell 1, Tekmetric) handle the automotive side but not residential systems. No single platform manages the full RV service workflow.
