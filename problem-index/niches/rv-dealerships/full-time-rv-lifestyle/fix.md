# Full-Timers Buying Weekend-Grade RVs That Fail Under Daily Use

**Niche:** [[niches/rv-dealerships/full-time-rv-lifestyle/profile|Full-Time RV Lifestyle Buyers]]
**Industry:** [[industries/rv-dealerships|RV Dealerships]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Full-timers buy RVs designed for 30-40 days of annual use and everything breaks within 12 months of daily use — water heaters, faucets, furniture, slide mechanisms, and roof seals all have 10x the expected duty cycle.
**Tags:** #recommendation #revenue-impact #worker-facing

## The Problem
A manufacturer designs an RV for recreational use: 30-50 days per year of actual habitation. The water heater is sized for 50 showers per year, the faucet is rated for 5,000 cycles, and the slide-out mechanism is tested for 500 extensions. A full-timer puts the unit through 365 days of continuous use — 700+ showers, 15,000+ faucet cycles, and 700+ slide extensions in the first year alone. Components fail at 3-5x the rate of recreational use, and warranty coverage is limited to "defects in materials and workmanship" — not "you used it too much." Full-timers spend $3,000-$8,000 per year on repairs that recreational users would never encounter, and they blame the dealer for selling them a "lemon."

## Why It's Still Broken
Manufacturers market to full-timers ("live your dream!") while engineering for recreational use because that's 80%+ of their market. They don't publish duty-cycle ratings that would honestly communicate expected component life under daily use. Dealers don't disclose the difference between recreational-grade and residential-grade components because it would talk customers out of lower-margin units into higher-margin ones (which sounds like upselling). The information asymmetry is systemic.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A "full-time readiness assessment" that the dealer provides to any customer indicating full-time plans. The assessment lists specific components in the target unit and their expected life under daily use (based on manufacturer ratings and full-timer community feedback): "Water heater: rated for recreational use, expect replacement at 18-24 months of full-time use. Residential upgrade available: $1,200. Slide-out motor: commercial-grade, suitable for full-time use. Faucet: residential-style, will need replacement at 12-18 months, $150." This transparency sets expectations and lets the buyer budget for upgrades or choose a unit with better-grade components.

## Who Feels the Pain
Full-timers who spend $3,000-$8,000 per year on component failures they weren't warned about, and dealer service departments that handle warranty claims that get denied because "excessive use" isn't covered.

## Impact If Fixed
Reduces full-timer warranty frustration complaints by 60-70% by setting accurate expectations upfront. Generates $1,000-$3,000 per full-time sale in upgrade revenue (residential fridge, better faucets, upgraded water heater) that the buyer would have spent anyway — but at a repair shop 12 months later instead of at the dealer at purchase.
