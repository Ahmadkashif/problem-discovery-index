## Profile
**Market Size:** $890M
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~4%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — County indigent burial programs use paper applications, FEMA funeral assistance requires manual documentation, and Medicaid burial benefit claims are phone/fax-based.
**Target Buyer:** Funeral directors handling county/state indigent burial contracts, FEMA-assisted funerals, or Medicaid burial benefit cases
**Automation Potential:** High — Benefit applications, eligibility verification, and reimbursement claims are document-heavy and rule-based.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Low-income funerals include county indigent burials (where the deceased has no family or no resources), Medicaid burial benefits (capped at $1,500-5,000 depending on the state), FEMA funeral assistance (for pandemic or disaster deaths), and Social Security lump-sum death benefits ($255). Funeral homes that serve this population operate at razor-thin margins: reimbursement rates are fixed and often below cost, paperwork requirements are extensive, and payment timelines are 30-90 days. Yet someone must serve these families — often the same community funeral homes that serve everyone else.

## Current Tools & Gaps
There are no tools purpose-built for public-assistance funeral cases. Funeral directors navigate county welfare offices, FEMA portals, state Medicaid systems, and Social Security independently for each case. Eligibility rules, documentation requirements, and reimbursement rates differ by jurisdiction and program. The funeral home absorbs the administrative burden of navigating these systems, often losing money on the case even when reimbursement arrives.
