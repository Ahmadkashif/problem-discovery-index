# Cremation Services

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/funeral-homes|Funeral Homes]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $6.2B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~27%
**Digital Adoption:** Medium — Cremation-focused operators often have modern websites and online arrangement options, but internal workflow (chain of custody, cremation scheduling, remains tracking) is manual.
**Target Buyer:** Owner/operator of a cremation-focused funeral home or standalone crematory
**Automation Potential:** High — Chain of custody documentation, cremation scheduling, and remains tracking are rule-heavy and systematizable.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Cremation has surpassed burial as the dominant disposition method in the US (60%+ and rising), driven by lower cost, environmental preferences, and declining religious objection. Cremation-focused operators run a fundamentally different business than traditional funeral homes: lower price points ($1,500-4,000 vs. $7,000-12,000), higher volume, simpler facility requirements, and more price-sensitive consumers who comparison-shop online. The operational workflow centers on chain of custody (the body must be positively identified and tracked from receipt through cremation to remains return), cremation scheduling across multiple retorts, and regulatory compliance that varies by state.

## Current Tools & Gaps
CRAKN, Passare, and funeral home management systems support cremation workflow, but many cremation-specific operators find them overbuilt for their simpler needs. Chain of custody is tracked with paper tags and manual logs. Cremation scheduling across multiple retorts (ovens) is done on whiteboards. Families ordering direct cremation online often complete arrangements through generic web forms with no integration to the operator's workflow.
