# Auto Lending CUs

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/credit-unions|Credit Unions]]
**Category:** High Market Share

## Profile
**Market Size:** $500B+ in outstanding auto loans held by credit unions (32% of all US auto lending)
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~35% of CU loan portfolios
**Digital Adoption:** Medium — Indirect lending platforms (CU Direct, Open Lending) provide dealer-facing workflows, but used vehicle valuation, dealer relationship management, and member refinance targeting are manual or under-automated
**Target Buyer:** VP of Lending or Indirect Lending Manager at a CU with significant auto loan portfolio
**Automation Potential:** High — Auto lending involves standardized collateral valuation, rate setting, dealer negotiation, and portfolio monitoring processes with clear automation targets

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Credit unions are the single largest source of auto financing in the US, holding over $500B in auto loans. Auto lending CUs operate a distinct business model from general consumer lending — indirect lending through dealer networks means the CU never meets the borrower before funding the loan, eliminating the relationship advantage that defines CU lending in other categories. The competitive dynamics are different: CUs compete with captive finance (Toyota Financial, GM Financial) and banks on rate and approval speed at the point of sale. Turn time — how fast the CU can approve a dealer-submitted application — determines whether the dealer sends the next deal or routes it to a competitor.

## Current Tools & Gaps
CU Direct's CUDL platform dominates indirect auto lending with dealer-facing submission portals. Open Lending provides risk-based pricing and default insurance. MeridianLink handles LOS workflows. Valuation tools (Black Book, JD Power) provide wholesale values. The gap is decisioning intelligence: most CUs apply static rate sheets and credit policy matrices without real-time adjustment for market conditions, dealer performance, or portfolio concentration risk. Used vehicle valuation — critical for CUs that fund 60%+ used vehicles — relies on book values that don't reflect local market pricing.
