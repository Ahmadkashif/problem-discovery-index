# Underbanked Community CUs

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/credit-unions|Credit Unions]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $50-80B in combined assets across ~300 CUs with explicit underbanked-serving mission
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~6% of institutions, ~3% of assets
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Members may lack smartphones or broadband access; the CU itself often operates with minimal technology infrastructure; digital banking adoption among underbanked members is 20-40% vs. 80%+ at mainstream CUs
**Target Buyer:** CEO or Community Development Officer
**Automation Potential:** Medium — Alternative credit assessment, small-dollar lending, and financial education workflows are automatable but require non-standard data models

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Underbanked-serving CUs target populations that mainstream financial institutions have abandoned: unbanked and underbanked households (estimated 19% of US adults), thin-credit-file individuals, recent immigrants without established credit history, and low-income communities underserved by bank branches. These CUs must make lending decisions for people who don't have FICO scores, can't provide standard documentation, and may have been denied by every other institution. Their competitive set isn't other CUs or banks — it's payday lenders, check cashers, and buy-here-pay-here dealers that charge 300-600% APR. The mission is financial inclusion; the challenge is lending safely to people traditional risk models label as un-scoreable.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Standard CU core platforms require FICO scores and traditional documentation for lending workflows. No affordable CU lending tool supports alternative data underwriting (utility payments, rent history, phone bill consistency) as primary credit indicators. Small-dollar lending platforms (QCash, Oportun partnerships) exist but are limited in scope. Financial education delivery is ad hoc — workshops and pamphlets rather than integrated digital tools. The fundamental gap: CU technology assumes members have credit scores, bank histories, and standard documentation — the defining characteristic of underbanked members is that they don't.
