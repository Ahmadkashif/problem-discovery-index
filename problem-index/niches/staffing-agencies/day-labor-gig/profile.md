# Day Labor & Gig-Adjacent Temp Staffing

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/staffing-agencies|Staffing Agencies]]
**Category:** Low Digitized

## Profile
**Market Size:** $8-12B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~5%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — many day labor operations run on paper sign-in sheets, cash or check payments, and phone calls; digital tools are consumer-grade at best
**Target Buyer:** Owner-operator of a day labor dispatch office or temp staffing agency specializing in daily-pay, on-demand labor
**Automation Potential:** Medium — the dispatch workflow is simple but the worker population is transient and often lacks smartphones or bank accounts, limiting digital adoption

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Day labor staffing operates at the extreme end of temp work: workers show up at a dispatch hall or call in each morning, get assigned to a job site for the day, and are paid daily or weekly. The relationship is minimal — many workers are not in a traditional ATS, have no resume on file, and may work for the agency once and never return. The client relationship is equally transient: construction sites, event venues, and disaster cleanup operations need bodies today and may never call again. This operating model is completely different from multi-week temp placements and requires different systems for worker tracking, assignment, payroll, and compliance (particularly around wage-and-hour laws for daily-pay workers).

## Current Tools & Gaps
Many day labor operations use paper-based systems or basic spreadsheets. Some have adopted simple mobile apps for worker check-in. TempWorks and StaffSuite serve the low end of the market. The gaps are enormous: worker identity verification is often cursory, time tracking relies on paper tickets signed at the job site, daily pay calculation is done manually (especially when overtime splits across multiple clients in a single week), and there is no visibility into worker availability or reliability history because most workers are not tracked in a persistent database.
