# AI-Assisted Application Scoring with Fair Housing Compliance

**Niche:** [[niches/property-management/tenant-screening-leasing/profile|Tenant Screening & Leasing]]
**Industry:** [[industries/property-management|Property Management]]
**Type:** Build (Greenfield Opportunity)
**One-liner:** A tenant scoring system that applies documented, consistent criteria to every applicant — credit, income, rental history, eviction records — and monitors the outcomes for disparate impact across protected classes, ensuring that the PM's screening process is both effective (selecting tenants who will pay rent and care for the property) and legally defensible (applying the same standards to everyone).

## The Problem
Tenant screening data comes from 4-5 sources: credit report (FICO score, derogatory marks, collections), criminal background (convictions by type and date), eviction history (filings and judgments), income verification (pay stubs, tax returns, employment verification), and landlord references (payment history, lease violations, property condition). Synthesizing these into a decision requires weighing each factor, accounting for context (a medical collection is different from an eviction judgment), and applying consistent thresholds. Currently, this synthesis is the PM's judgment — which means it varies by PM, by day, and by unconscious biases. A PM who has been burned by a previous tenant with a 600 credit score may reject all future 600-score applicants, even though the specific circumstances differ. This inconsistency creates two problems: fair housing liability (HUD's disparate impact standard requires that screening criteria be applied uniformly and produce no disproportionate effect on protected classes) and suboptimal selection (PMs reject good tenants with imperfect histories or accept risky tenants who present well in person).

## Why Nobody Has Built This
RealPage's AI screening product faced an antitrust lawsuit (2022-2024) alleging that its algorithm functioned as a price-fixing tool by recommending similar screening outcomes across competing landlords using the same system. This legal exposure has deterred other companies from building AI-powered screening decisions. The fair housing compliance challenge is real: any automated scoring system must be auditable for disparate impact, and the model's criteria must be defensible under the Fair Housing Act's disparate impact standard (Inclusive Communities, 2015). Building a scoring model that is both predictive (accurate tenant quality prediction) and fair (no disparate impact on protected classes) requires careful feature selection, bias testing, and ongoing monitoring.

## What to Build
A screening decision support system (not a decision-making system) that: (1) applies the PM's documented screening criteria consistently — the PM sets their thresholds (minimum credit score, minimum income ratio, maximum age of criminal conviction, eviction policy) and the system applies them uniformly to every applicant; (2) produces a scored recommendation — "Meets all criteria: Approve" or "Below credit threshold (580 vs. 620 minimum), meets all other criteria: Conditional — consider with additional deposit" — with the specific criteria triggering each recommendation transparently documented; (3) monitors outcomes for disparate impact — tracking approval rates by race, national origin, familial status, and other protected classes, alerting the PM when approval rates diverge significantly from population baselines or from the rates for other protected groups; (4) generates compliant adverse action notices with specific, criteria-based denial reasons as required by FCRA.

## Target Customer
Property management companies managing 200+ units who want consistent, defensible screening decisions. Individual landlords who want to screen fairly without expertise in fair housing law. Fair housing advocacy organizations seeking tools that reduce discrimination in tenant selection.

## Impact If Built
Standardizes screening decisions, reducing outcome variability between PMs from 25-40% to under 5%. Provides fair housing compliance monitoring that catches potential disparate impact before it becomes a complaint. Reduces bad-tenant placements by 20-30% through consistent application of documented criteria versus inconsistent gut-feel decisions. Creates a documented screening record that is the PM's best defense against a fair housing complaint.
