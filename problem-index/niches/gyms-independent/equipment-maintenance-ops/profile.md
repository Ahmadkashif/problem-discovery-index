# Equipment Maintenance Operations

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/gyms-independent|Independent Gyms]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $1.2B
**Share of Parent Industry:** 3%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — Most independent gyms track equipment maintenance in notebooks, memory, or not at all. Preventive maintenance schedules are manufacturer-recommended but rarely followed. Repairs happen when equipment breaks.
**Target Buyer:** Facility Manager / Owner
**Automation Potential:** High — Maintenance scheduling, usage-based service intervals, parts ordering, and warranty tracking are highly rule-based and currently managed reactively rather than proactively.

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
A typical independent gym has $100K-500K in equipment — treadmills, ellipticals, cable machines, free weights, benches, bikes — that degrades through heavy use. Manufacturer maintenance schedules (belt replacement every 2,500 hours, lubrication every 3 months) assume average usage, but a treadmill at a busy gym might see 3x the manufacturer's assumed daily hours. Most independent gyms perform no preventive maintenance until equipment breaks, at which point they face $500-2,000 emergency repair bills, 1-3 weeks of downtime waiting for parts and service, and member dissatisfaction from out-of-order machines. The gym owner's approach to equipment maintenance is almost entirely reactive.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Large fitness chains use CMMS platforms (UpKeep, Fiix, Limble) for maintenance tracking. Equipment manufacturers (Life Fitness, Precor) provide maintenance schedules in owner manuals. But independent gyms don't use CMMS — the platforms are too complex and expensive for a single-location operator managing 30-80 pieces of equipment. The owner knows equipment needs service when it starts making noise or a member complains. No lightweight, gym-specific maintenance tool tracks equipment age, usage, service history, and warranty status in a format appropriate for a non-technical owner.
