# Finish Specification Tracking Through Production

**Niche:** [[niches/metal-fabrication/ornamental-and-architectural-metal/profile|Ornamental & Architectural Metal]]
**Industry:** [[industries/metal-fabrication|Metal Fabrication]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** The most expensive rework in ornamental metalwork happens when the wrong finish is applied — a $3,000 patina process on a piece that was supposed to be powder-coated, or a hand-rubbed bronze finish omitted from one panel in a 10-panel installation — because finish specifications live in the original design approval email, not on the shop floor.
**Tags:** #automation #worker-facing #compliance

## The Problem
Ornamental metal projects frequently involve multiple finish types within a single installation — hot-dipped galvanized structural elements, powder-coated railings in a specific RAL color, hand-applied patina on decorative panels, clear-coated stainless on accent pieces. The finish specification for each element is documented in the design approval (an email thread, a marked-up rendering, or a meeting note) but is not systematically translated to the production floor. When elements reach the finishing stage, the finisher refers to the job traveler — which may say "finish per design" without specifying which elements get which finish. The result: finish errors, which are among the most expensive fabrication mistakes because they require stripping and re-finishing (or re-fabrication if stripping damages the piece).

## Why It's Still Broken
Ornamental shops are small and communication is informal — the owner who approved the design with the client is often the same person supervising production, so finish specifications are communicated verbally. This works when the owner is present; it fails when the owner is at a different job site, when a finisher who wasn't present for the design discussion applies the finish, or when the project has a 3-month production timeline and the design discussion happened months ago. No tool tracks per-element finish specifications from design approval through production.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A simple finish specification tracker integrated with the job record: at design approval, the fabricator documents each element's finish specification (finish type, color code, preparation requirements, inspection criteria) with a photo reference. In production, each element's finish spec is visible on the digital traveler at the finishing station — the finisher scans the element's tag and sees the specific finish required with a photo of the approved reference sample. The quality inspector verifies finish compliance against the documented specification, not from memory.

## Who Feels the Pain
Finishers who are told "make it match the sample" without being shown the sample, and shop owners who absorb $1,000-$5,000 per finish error in rework cost and schedule delay.

## Impact If Fixed
Prevents finish specification errors that cost $1,000-$5,000 per incident in rework. For a shop producing 50 projects/year with a 5% finish error rate, eliminating these errors saves $25,000-$125,000 annually. More importantly, prevents delivery delays and client relationship damage that result from visible quality failures on decorative work.
