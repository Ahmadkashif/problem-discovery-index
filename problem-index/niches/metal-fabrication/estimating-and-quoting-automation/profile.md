# Estimating & Quoting Automation

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/metal-fabrication|Metal Fabrication]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $5-8B (embedded)
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~1-2% (embedded cost across all fab shops)
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — most estimators use spreadsheets with manually maintained rate tables; some use SteelCalc or ProNest for material takeoff but not integrated labor estimation
**Target Buyer:** Estimator or shop owner at fabrication shops quoting 20+ jobs per month
**Automation Potential:** High — material takeoff from drawings, labor estimation from geometric features, and quote generation follow patterns that experienced estimators apply consistently and that ML can learn from historical data

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Estimating is the revenue-determining function of every fabrication shop — the accuracy of the quote determines whether the job is won, and whether it is profitable if won. Fabrication estimating is uniquely difficult because every job is custom: the estimator must read an engineering drawing (or a napkin sketch), develop a production routing, estimate time per operation, and calculate material cost — a skill that takes years of shop-floor experience to calibrate. Estimating accuracy data (actual cost vs. quoted cost per job) exists in every shop's financial records but is almost never analyzed systematically. The estimating function is simultaneously the most valuable and least data-supported activity in metal fabrication.

## Current Tools & Gaps
SteelCalc and Tekla provide structural steel quantity takeoffs. ProNest and Lantek provide material cost estimation from nesting. Spreadsheets with shop-specific rate tables handle labor estimation. No tool learns from a shop's own historical estimating data to improve future quote accuracy — creating a feedback loop between the estimated and actual cost of each job to systematically identify and correct estimating biases.
