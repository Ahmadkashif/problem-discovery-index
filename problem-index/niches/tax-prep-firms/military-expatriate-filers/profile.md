# Military & Expatriate Filer Specialists

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/tax-prep-firms|Tax Prep Firms]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** ~$800M in preparation fees for military and expatriate individual returns
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~6%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — tax software handles the forms (2555, 1116, combat zone extensions) but the determination logic (bona fide residence test, physical presence test, treaty benefits, SOFA provisions) requires extensive manual research; client communication often crosses time zones and unreliable connectivity
**Target Buyer:** Preparer specializing in military or expat returns, often operating remotely themselves
**Automation Potential:** Medium — eligibility tests and treaty lookups follow deterministic rules, but the client circumstances (multiple countries, mid-year moves, combat zone deployments) create complex fact patterns

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Military and expatriate filers have tax situations that most preparers are not trained to handle: foreign earned income exclusion (Form 2555), foreign tax credits (Form 1116), combat zone tax benefits, military moving expense deductions (still available for military unlike civilians), Status of Forces Agreement (SOFA) provisions that affect state residency, and tax treaty benefits that vary by country. These clients are often in different time zones, have unreliable internet access (deployed military, developing-country expats), and submit documents in formats that include foreign-language employer statements and foreign currency amounts.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Tax software handles the relevant forms once data is entered, but provides no guidance on the complex determination logic: does the client meet the bona fide residence test or only the physical presence test? Is the housing exclusion or deduction more favorable? Which country's tax treaty applies when the client worked in two foreign countries? Can combat zone extension stacking extend the filing deadline by years? Preparers research these questions in tax manuals and IRS publications, spending 45-90 minutes per return on determination logic before data entry begins.
