# Multi-Jurisdiction Permitting Operations

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/food-trucks|Food Trucks]]
**Category:** Underserved Audience

## Profile
**Market Size:** $200-300M (embedded across food truck operations)
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~15% (operators who actively work across 3+ jurisdictions)
**Digital Adoption:** Low — permits are tracked on paper, in email inboxes, or in spreadsheets with manual renewal reminders
**Target Buyer:** Operator working across 3-8 cities or counties, each with different permit requirements, fees, and inspection cadences
**Automation Potential:** High — permit requirements are rule-based, deadlines are date-driven, and forms are structured data

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Most food trucks do not operate in a single city. A truck in the Dallas-Fort Worth metro might serve events in Dallas, Fort Worth, Arlington, Plano, and Frisco — each with separate health permits, business licenses, fire inspections, and commissary requirements. An operator in the DC metro crosses city, state, and county lines within a single route day. The permitting burden scales nonlinearly: 5 jurisdictions means 15-25 separate permits, each with different renewal dates, fee amounts, inspection schedules, and application processes. A single lapsed permit can result in a $500 fine or an on-site shutdown during peak service hours.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Paper files, email chains, and Google Calendar reminders. Some operators use spreadsheets to track renewal dates. No tool aggregates permit requirements by jurisdiction, auto-fills applications across multiple cities, tracks inspection schedules, or alerts operators when a renewal is approaching. The data itself — what each city requires — is buried in municipal websites, PDF handbooks, and phone calls to health departments.
