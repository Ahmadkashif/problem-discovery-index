# Billing & Collections Management

**Parent Industry:** [[industries/small-law-firms|Small Law Firms]]
**Category:** Highly Automatable

## Profile
**Market Size:** $2-3B (embedded in practice operations)
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~2-3%
**Digital Adoption:** Low-Medium — Clio and LawPay handle invoicing and payments, but the full billing pipeline from time capture through collection is fragmented
**Target Buyer:** Attorney (who is also the billing department), office manager, bookkeeper
**Automation Potential:** Very High — time capture, invoice generation, payment reminders, and trust reconciliation are all rule-based and repeatable

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Small firm attorneys consistently under-bill by 20-35% because they reconstruct time entries from memory at day's end. Studies show attorneys bill 1.5-2 hours per day less than they actually work — representing $50,000-$150,000 in annual revenue loss per attorney. Of the time that IS billed, 15-20% goes uncollected because invoices aren't followed up. Trust account (IOLTA) compliance adds another layer: commingling client funds with operating funds is the #1 bar discipline issue, and many small firms manage trust accounting in Excel. The entire billing pipeline — from capturing the billable event to depositing the payment — is fragmented across 3-4 tools with manual handoffs at each stage.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Clio handles time entry, invoicing, and basic trust accounting. LawPay processes credit card payments with bar-compliant trust/operating account separation. QuickBooks handles general accounting. But the pipeline from time capture → invoice → payment → collection → trust reconciliation requires manual intervention at every step. Clio's time tracking requires manual entry. LawPay doesn't trigger collection workflows for unpaid invoices. Trust reconciliation between Clio and the bank is manual.
