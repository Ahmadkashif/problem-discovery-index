## Profile
**Market Size:** $8-10B
**Share of Parent Industry:** ~9%
**Digital Adoption:** Low — most solo criminal defense attorneys use the most basic case management (if any), rely on paper files, and track court dates in a physical planner or Outlook calendar
**Target Buyer:** Solo criminal defense attorney or 2-attorney firm handling private criminal defense cases alongside court-appointed work
**Automation Potential:** Medium-High — discovery review (especially body camera footage), sentencing guideline research, and bail/bond coordination follow patterns that could be automated, but attorney resistance to technology and limited budgets constrain adoption

## What Makes This a Distinct Niche
Solo criminal defense attorneys operate under constraints unique in legal practice. Their clients are frequently in custody, making communication difficult — jail phone calls are recorded, in-person visits require scheduling through the facility, and clients may be transferred between facilities without notice. The billing model is split: retained clients pay upfront or in installments (often from family members), while court-appointed cases pay a fixed fee ($500-2,000 per case depending on jurisdiction) that rarely covers actual time invested. Prosecutors control the discovery flow and may produce hundreds of hours of body camera footage, thousands of pages of police reports, and forensic evidence on short timelines. The attorney must review all discovery material for Brady violations, witness inconsistencies, and procedural errors — work that large firms delegate to associates but solos must do themselves. Sentencing outcomes depend on judicial tendencies, local norms, and prosecutorial practices that vary dramatically by courthouse.

## Current Tools & Gaps
Criminal-defense-specific case management barely exists. Most solos use generic Clio or MyCase, or no software at all. JAVS (Justice AV Solutions) handles courtroom recording but not defense workflow. No mainstream tool handles criminal discovery management — organizing, reviewing, and annotating body camera footage, police reports, witness statements, and forensic evidence in a case-centric view. The critical gap is local outcome intelligence: understanding how a specific judge sentences for a specific charge in a specific jurisdiction, which prosecutors are open to plea negotiations, and what diversion programs are available. This knowledge currently lives entirely in the experienced defense attorney's memory.
