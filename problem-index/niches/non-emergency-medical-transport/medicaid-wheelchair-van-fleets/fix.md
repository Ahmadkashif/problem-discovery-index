# Securement Documentation for Medicaid Audits

**Niche:** [[niches/non-emergency-medical-transport/medicaid-wheelchair-van-fleets/profile|Medicaid Wheelchair Van Fleets]]
**Industry:** [[industries/non-emergency-medical-transport|Non-Emergency Medical Transport]]
**Type:** Fix (Pain Point)
**One-liner:** Drivers secure wheelchairs in vehicles daily but documentation of proper securement is paper-based, inconsistent, and frequently missing — creating audit liability and safety risk.
**Tags:** #compliance #automation #worker-facing #quick-win

## The Problem
WTORS (Wheelchair Tiedown and Occupant Restraint Systems) compliance requires that drivers verify and document four-point securement of every wheelchair and application of lap/shoulder belts to the patient on every trip. Currently, drivers sign a checklist on a paper trip sheet — a binary yes/no that provides no evidence of actual securement quality. During Medicaid audits, missing or incomplete securement documentation triggers claim recoupment on every trip without documentation, regardless of whether the securement was actually performed. A single audit can recoup $50K-$200K in wheelchair trip claims for documentation failures alone.

## Why It's Still Broken
Securement documentation falls into the gap between safety and billing. Drivers view it as paperwork — they secure the wheelchair (because they understand the safety need) but rush through the documentation because it slows their schedule by 2-3 minutes per trip across 8-12 trips per day. The paper checklist provides no verification mechanism — a driver can sign "secured" without actually performing all four tie-down points. Photo documentation would provide evidence but requires a smartphone workflow that does not exist in current dispatch apps. Driver turnover (60%+ annually) means constant retraining on documentation requirements.

## What a Fix Looks Like
A mobile app module integrated with the driver's trip management tablet that prompts photo capture of each completed securement (four-point tie-down visible, lap belt visible, shoulder belt visible) before allowing the driver to mark the pickup as complete. Photos are geotagged, timestamped, and stored linked to the specific trip record. During audits, the operator provides photographic evidence of securement for every wheelchair trip, eliminating the documentation gap that triggers recoupment. The 30-second photo step replaces the 60-second paper checklist, making compliance faster, not slower.

## Who Feels the Pain
NEMT operators who face $50K-$200K in audit recoupments for documentation gaps on wheelchair trips, billing staff who must defend claims with inadequate paper records, and drivers who bear personal liability for securement failures during accidents.

## Impact If Fixed
Eliminates wheelchair-trip documentation recoupment risk (estimated $50K-$200K per audit cycle), provides defensible evidence in accident investigations, and actually improves securement compliance by 15-20% through the accountability of photo verification — reducing injury risk for wheelchair patients.
