# AI-Powered Electrical Panel Inspection and Hazard Identification from Photos

**Industry:** [[electrical-contractors|Electrical Contractors]]
**Type:** High Impact
**One-liner:** An electrician or home inspector who photographs a residential electrical panel gets an instant AI analysis identifying manufacturer recalls (Zinsco, FPE Stab-Lok), double-tapped breakers, overloaded circuits, improper wire gauges, and heat damage signs — the same assessment a master electrician performs through years of accumulated visual pattern recognition, available to every inspection professional in seconds.

## The Problem
Electrical panel inspection is the highest-stakes visual assessment in residential and commercial electrical work. An experienced master electrician can look at a panel and within 30-60 seconds identify: the panel manufacturer (and whether it is a known fire risk — Federal Pacific Stab-Lok and Zinsco panels have documented failure modes that make them significant fire hazards); double-tapped breakers (two wires connected to a single breaker terminal, creating overload risk); circuit breakers that are undersized or oversized for their connected wire gauge; heat damage on terminals or bus bars indicating past overloading; improper conductor materials (aluminum wiring on 15/20A circuits, which requires specific receptacle types to be safe); and missing knockout seals that violate code. This assessment is performed routinely by master electricians, home inspectors, and insurance inspectors — but its quality is entirely dependent on the inspector's experience. A first-year electrician or a home inspector without electrical specialization may miss half of these findings. There is no current tool to systematically support this assessment.

## Why It's Unsolved
Panel inspection is a multi-label visual classification problem with several hard aspects: the panel interior is visually complex (dozens of breakers, multiple wire bundles, labels, bus bars), the relevant features are subtle (a slightly discolored terminal, a breaker tab that's been modified), the classification requires manufacturer-specific knowledge (knowing that this specific panel design has a known breaker failure mode), and the training data (labeled panel inspection images with expert findings) has not been assembled at the scale needed for a robust model. The commercial incentive to solve this hasn't been strong enough to drive the data collection investment — until now, when home inspection AI and insurance inspection AI are creating adjacent demand.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A mobile app where an electrician or home inspector takes 3-5 photos of the panel interior and the panel label. The model: (1) classifies the panel manufacturer from the label photo and flags known-hazardous models with the specific safety concern; (2) detects double-tapped breakers from the breaker photo; (3) detects visual signs of heat damage or arc damage on terminals and bus bars; (4) identifies visible wire gauge mismatches against breaker amperage ratings; (5) flags missing knockouts and other obvious code violations. The output is a structured inspection report with findings, severity (immediate concern / recommend electrician review / monitor), photo annotations, and the specific code or safety reference for each finding. The electrician reviews, adds findings from hands-on testing, and produces the final report. The AI handles the systematic visual scan; the electrician handles the hands-on diagnostics.

## Impact If Solved
In home inspection, hazardous panel conditions (Stab-Lok, double-taps, heat damage) are missed in an estimated 15-25% of inspections by non-specialist home inspectors — each missed finding represents a real fire or electrocution risk. For electrical contractors, this tool enables junior staff to perform initial panel assessments that previously required a master electrician — expanding service capacity. For insurance inspection, automated panel photo analysis could screen millions of homeowners insurance renewals for known high-risk panel types without dispatching a physical inspector, transforming underwriting accuracy.
