# Unpaid Detention and Lumper Time at Shippers and Receivers

**Industry:** [[owner-operator-trucking|Owner-Operator Trucking]]
**Type:** Worker Life Changing
**One-liner:** Owner-operators lose 5-10 hours per week sitting unpaid at loading docks waiting for shippers and receivers who treat driver time as free — and the lack of transparency about which facilities are chronic offenders makes the problem invisible.
**Tags:** #gradient-boosting #decision-trees #feature-engineering #evaluation-metrics #cross-validation #descriptive-statistics #expectation-variance-covariance #confidence-intervals #worker-facing

## The Problem
An owner-operator arrives at a shipper or receiver at the scheduled appointment time and is told to wait. Two hours later, they're still sitting in the parking lot. Under most broker contracts, detention pay doesn't kick in until 2 hours of free time have elapsed, and even then pays $25-$50/hour — roughly one-third of the operator's loaded mile revenue rate. Lumper fees (charges to unload freight, common at grocery distribution centers) add $200-$400 per stop that the operator must pay upfront and claim back from the broker, often waiting 30-60 days for reimbursement. An operator averaging 3-4 pickups/deliveries per week loses 5-10 unpaid hours and $200-$600 in float costs from lumper advances. This is effectively wage theft at scale, enabled by information asymmetry: operators don't know a facility's average dwell time before they accept the load.

## Why It Matters to the Worker
Every hour spent unpaid at a dock is an hour not driving, not earning, and not getting home. Detention time is the single most emotionally corrosive part of trucking — it communicates that the driver's time has zero value. Operators who complain to brokers get labeled as difficult and receive fewer load offers. The financial impact compounds: 500+ unpaid detention hours per year at an opportunity cost of $50/hour represents $25,000 in lost annual income.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A crowd-sourced and model-enhanced facility rating system that predicts dwell time at every shipper/receiver based on historical ELD data from operators who have visited that facility. Before accepting a load, the operator sees: "Average wait at this receiver: 3.2 hours. 80th percentile: 5.1 hours. Detention likelihood: 78%." This information transforms load selection — operators can factor realistic dwell time into their profitability calculation and either avoid chronic offenders or price the detention risk into their rate negotiation.

## Impact If Solved
If operators could avoid the worst 20% of detention facilities or negotiate rates that reflect true dwell time, the average operator would recover $8,000-$15,000/year in lost productivity and reduce the emotional toll of feeling powerless at the dock.
