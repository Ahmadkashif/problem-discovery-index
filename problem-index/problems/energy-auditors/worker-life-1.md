# Field Data Collection Tedium

**Industry:** [[energy-auditors|Energy Auditors]]
**Type:** Worker Life Changing
**One-liner:** Auditors get their eyes back on the building instead of buried in a tablet entering 200+ data points per home.
**Tags:** #cnns #transfer-learning #loss-functions #backpropagation #evaluation-metrics #gradient-descent #matrix-algebra #worker-facing #automation

## The Problem
A standard residential energy audit requires recording 200+ discrete data points: nameplate data from furnaces, water heaters, and air conditioners (model numbers, AFUE/EF/SEER ratings, serial numbers for age determination), insulation types and depths in attics/walls/basements, window types and dimensions across every elevation, duct locations and insulation levels, lighting counts by fixture type, appliance inventory, and blower door / duct blaster test readings. Auditors currently photograph nameplates, then manually transcribe them into audit software — often hours later back at the office. Transcription errors are common (misread model numbers, transposed digits on efficiency ratings), and the re-entry work means auditors spend as much time on data entry as on the actual diagnostic assessment.

## Why It Matters to the Worker
Field auditors chose this career to solve building science problems, not to be data entry clerks. The transcription grind causes fatigue-driven errors that trigger QA rejections from utility programs — meaning the auditor has to revisit the home. In summer attics (130+ degrees) and winter crawlspaces, every extra minute spent squinting at nameplates and typing into a tablet is a minute of physical misery. The cognitive load of tracking which data points have been captured versus missed creates persistent low-grade stress throughout the visit.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A mobile capture system where the auditor photographs equipment nameplates, insulation, windows, and test instrument displays, and the system extracts structured data automatically — model numbers, efficiency ratings, dimensions, test readings — populating the audit software fields in real time. The auditor reviews and confirms extracted values rather than typing them. Voice annotation handles non-visual observations (draft locations, odors, noise).

## Impact If Solved
Field time per audit drops 30-45 minutes (from ~3 hours to ~2-2.5 hours for residential), transcription errors drop by 80%+, and QA rejection rates decrease proportionally. Auditors can complete 1-2 additional audits per week without extending hours.
