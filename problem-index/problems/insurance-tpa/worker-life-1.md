# Claims Examiner Decision Fatigue

**Industry:** [[insurance-tpa|Insurance Third-Party Administrators (TPAs)]]
**Type:** Worker Life Changing
**One-liner:** Claims examiners stop making their worst decisions at 3 PM because AI-assisted document extraction and pre-populated decision templates reduce each claim from 60 minutes of cognitive labor to 15 minutes of review — cutting the daily decision count from 8-10 from-scratch analyses to 15-20 confirmations.
**Tags:** #bert #transfer-learning #word-embeddings #attention-mechanisms #transformers #loss-functions #evaluation-metrics #entropy-cross-entropy-kl-divergence #worker-facing

## The Problem
A claims examiner makes 8-10 substantive coverage decisions per day, each requiring focused attention on unstructured medical records, policy language, and regulatory requirements. By mid-afternoon, the examiner has processed 200+ pages of medical documentation, compared dozens of diagnosis-treatment combinations against policy terms, and written 5-6 decision rationales. Decision fatigue is real and measurable — error rates on claims adjudicated after 2 PM are 15-25% higher than morning decisions. Examiners report feeling "mentally drained" by early afternoon but cannot slow down because SLA deadlines create constant time pressure. The cognitive load is compounded by interruptions — phone calls from claimants, emails from attorneys, and supervisor reviews break concentration, forcing the examiner to re-read documents they've already processed.

The consequences of decision fatigue are serious: an incorrect denial triggers an appeal that costs 3-5x more to process than the original claim. An incorrect approval overpays the carrier's money. Both create compliance risk. Examiners who consistently make errors face disciplinary action, creating a punitive environment that adds stress without addressing the root cause — too many high-stakes decisions per day with inadequate cognitive support.

## What a Solution Looks Like
AI-assisted claims processing that handles the extractive and preparatory work — pulling relevant facts from medical records, mapping them to policy terms, pre-populating the coverage determination with a recommended decision and supporting rationale — so the examiner's role shifts from generating decisions to reviewing and approving them. Each claim arrives on the examiner's desk as a structured summary with: extracted facts, applicable policy provisions highlighted, recommended decision with confidence score, and a draft rationale. The examiner reviews, modifies if needed, and approves in 10-15 minutes instead of building from scratch in 45-90 minutes. This reduces the cognitive intensity per claim, enabling sustainable throughput without afternoon error spikes.

## Impact If Solved
Reduces examiner cognitive load by 60-70% per claim, shifting from generation to review. Eliminates the afternoon decision fatigue effect — error rates become consistent throughout the day. Reduces examiner burnout and turnover (currently 25-30% annually in claims roles). Increases examiner job satisfaction by removing the tedious document extraction work and focusing their expertise on judgment calls. Enables senior examiners to review more claims at a supervisory level rather than processing them directly.
