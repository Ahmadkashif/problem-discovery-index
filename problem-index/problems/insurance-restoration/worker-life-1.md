# Technician Hazardous Exposure Risk

**Industry:** [[insurance-restoration|Insurance Restoration]]
**Type:** Worker Life Changing
**One-liner:** Restoration technicians stop walking into Category 3 water losses, mold-contaminated spaces, and asbestos-containing materials without real-time hazard assessment — because a system tells them what PPE they need before they enter, not after they've been exposed.
**Tags:** #cnn #binary-classification #computer-vision #worker-facing

## The Problem
Restoration technicians routinely enter environments with invisible hazards. A sewage backup (Category 3) requires full-face respirators, Tyvek suits, and decontamination protocols — but the water source isn't always obvious on arrival, especially when clean water and sewage have mixed. Mold behind walls releases spores when drywall is cut, and technicians may begin demo with dust masks before discovering extensive mold contamination requiring HEPA respirators. Pre-1980 buildings may contain asbestos in floor tiles, pipe insulation, popcorn ceilings, or joint compound — disturbed during demo without prior testing. Lead paint in pre-1978 homes creates dust during demolition. Technicians average 3-5 different job environments per day, each with different hazard profiles, and PPE decisions are made quickly on-site with limited information.

## Why It Matters to the Worker
Long-term health consequences of restoration work are severe and under-documented. Mold exposure causes chronic respiratory issues. Sewage exposure risks hepatitis, E. coli, and other infections. Asbestos exposure has a 20-40 year latency before mesothelioma symptoms appear. The industry's workforce skews young (25-40) and male, with a culture that minimizes health concerns. Workers' compensation claims for chronic exposure are difficult to prove without exposure documentation. The average restoration technician has no systematic record of which hazards they've been exposed to across their career.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A mobile pre-entry assessment tool that combines property data (build year, construction type, prior claims history), job type (water category, fire/smoke, mold), and visual indicators from arrival photos to generate a hazard probability assessment and PPE recommendation before the technician enters the structure. The system maintains a per-technician exposure log that documents every job entry with hazard classification, PPE worn, duration of exposure, and any test results (air quality, mold spore counts, asbestos sampling). Over time, this creates a career exposure profile that supports health monitoring and workers' compensation documentation.

## Impact If Solved
Prevents an estimated 15-25% of under-protected entries where technicians encounter hazards without appropriate PPE. Creates the first systematic exposure tracking system for restoration workers, supporting early detection of chronic health issues. Reduces employer liability for inadequate PPE provision by documenting hazard assessments and PPE recommendations at every job entry.
