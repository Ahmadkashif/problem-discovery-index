# Front Desk Multi-System Juggling

**Industry:** [[hotels-boutique|Boutique Hotels]]
**Type:** Worker Life Changing
**One-liner:** Front desk agents stop alt-tabbing between 4-6 disconnected systems and start working from a single pane that pulls reservations, payments, guest messages, and channel updates into one view.
**Tags:** #llm #automation #nlp #worker-facing #data-integration

## The Problem
A boutique hotel front desk agent's typical check-in involves the PMS (Cloudbeds or Mews) for the reservation, a separate payment terminal for authorization, the channel manager (SiteMinder) to confirm OTA details, a guest messaging platform (or raw OTA messaging portals) for any pre-arrival requests, and sometimes a paper or spreadsheet-based log for housekeeping status and maintenance notes. During a busy afternoon with 15 arrivals in a 3-hour window, the agent is context-switching across these systems dozens of times per hour — re-entering guest names, cross-referencing confirmation numbers, and manually reconciling discrepancies between what the OTA says and what the PMS shows. Errors compound: a room marked clean in the PMS but still being serviced, a rate override that didn't sync to the channel manager, a guest preference noted in the messaging app but invisible at the desk.

## Why It Matters to the Worker
Front desk agents at boutique hotels are often the entire guest-facing operation — no concierge desk, no bell staff, no dedicated reservations team. The cognitive load of managing multiple disconnected systems while maintaining the warm, personal interaction that boutique guests expect creates chronic stress. Agents describe the feeling of "performing hospitality" while mentally tracking which screen has the information they need. New hires take 3-4 weeks just to learn the system choreography, and turnover at the front desk averages 6-9 months partly because the tools make an already demanding job unnecessarily exhausting.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A unified front desk interface — or an LLM-powered overlay that sits on top of existing systems — that presents a single guest card at check-in: reservation details (pulled from PMS), payment status (from the processor), pre-arrival messages and requests (from all messaging channels), room readiness (from housekeeping), and any notes from previous stays. The agent speaks or types naturally ("pull up the Johnson reservation arriving today") and the system retrieves and cross-references across all connected platforms. Rate discrepancies, missing authorizations, or housekeeping delays surface as alerts rather than requiring the agent to discover them mid-interaction.

## Impact If Solved
Front desk agents reclaim 30-45 minutes per shift currently spent on system navigation and manual cross-referencing, reduce check-in errors by 60-70%, and can actually focus on the guest standing in front of them — which is why most of them took the job in the first place.
