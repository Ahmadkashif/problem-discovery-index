# Backlist Metadata & Discoverability Optimization

**Industry:** [[independent-publishers|Independent Publishers]]
**Type:** Low Impact (Customisation Opportunity)
**One-liner:** Generic SEO and metadata tools exist but fail to account for publishing-specific taxonomy (BISAC codes, Thema subjects, ONIX 3.0 fields) and the discovery mechanics of Amazon, Bookshop.org, and library catalog systems.
**Tags:** #bert #transfer-learning #attention-mechanisms #word-embeddings #evaluation-metrics #entropy-cross-entropy-kl-divergence #loss-functions #gradient-descent #data-integration

## The Problem
Backlist titles generate 50-70% of an independent publisher's annual revenue, yet most backlist metadata was written at publication and never updated. BISAC codes may be outdated or too broad, Amazon keywords are absent or generic, and book descriptions were crafted for trade catalogs rather than online search. Every year a publisher's backlist loses discoverability as newer titles with better-optimized metadata push older titles down search rankings and recommendation algorithms.

## What Already Exists
General-purpose SEO tools (SEMrush, Ahrefs) can suggest keywords, and Amazon's own keyword tool provides search volume hints. Firebrand and Consonance allow bulk metadata editing. But none of these tools understand the intersection of BISAC/Thema classification hierarchies, ONIX 3.0 field requirements, retailer-specific keyword strategies, and the actual content of the book's text and reviews.

## The Customisation Gap
The system needs to ingest a book's full text (or a substantial excerpt), existing metadata, reader reviews, and comparable title performance data, then recommend updated BISAC codes, Thema subjects, Amazon keywords, and rewritten descriptions optimized for each retail channel's specific search algorithm. It must output ONIX 3.0-compliant metadata that can be pushed directly to distribution partners like Ingram or Baker & Taylor without manual reformatting.

## Impact If Solved
A 10-15% improvement in backlist discoverability translates directly to revenue uplift on titles with zero additional production cost — pure margin. For a publisher with 200 backlist titles averaging $5K/year each, even a modest lift represents $100K-$150K in recovered annual revenue.
