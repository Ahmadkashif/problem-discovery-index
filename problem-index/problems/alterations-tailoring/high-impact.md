# Expert Fit Assessment Automation

**Industry:** [[alterations-tailoring|Alterations & Tailoring]]
**Type:** High Impact
**One-liner:** The master tailor's 30-second visual scan of a garment on a body — reading fabric drape, seam stress, ease distribution, body asymmetry, and hemline fall to diagnose every fit issue and prescribe the correct alteration sequence — becomes a computer vision system that preserves this vanishing expertise and enables consistent fit assessment across all staff.
**Tags:** #cnn #multiclass-classification #computer-vision #tacit-knowledge-ml #revenue-impact

## The Problem
When a customer puts on a garment and stands before a master tailor, the tailor performs a visual assessment that takes 15-30 seconds but encodes decades of pattern recognition. They see that the left shoulder seam sits 1cm forward (indicating the client's left shoulder is more rounded), that the jacket back has horizontal stress lines below the collar (the client's upper back is broader than the pattern assumes), that the trouser front shows diagonal pull lines from hip to knee (the client's hips are rotated slightly), and that the hemline dips 2cm longer on the right side (the client's right leg is slightly shorter). From these visual signals — which the client cannot see and the tailor processes subconsciously — the tailor prescribes a specific sequence of alterations: take in the left shoulder seam, let out the back across the shoulder blades, rotate the trouser front pattern, and level the hem to the shorter leg.

This assessment is the foundation of the entire alteration. If the diagnosis is wrong, every downstream alteration is wrong. A tailor who misses the body asymmetry and simply hems to an even length will produce a garment that looks crooked when worn. A tailor who doesn't read the stress lines correctly will take in fabric in the wrong location, creating new distortions. The assessment requires understanding garment construction (where seams are, which seams can be altered, how much seam allowance exists), fabric behavior (a wool crepe drapes differently than a polyester blend and responds differently to pressing), and body mechanics (how the client's posture, stance width, and arm carriage affect the garment at rest and in motion).

The crisis is workforce succession: the average master tailor in the US is 55-65 years old. When they retire, their fit assessment expertise — built from 30,000-50,000 garment evaluations — leaves with them. Younger staff and intake workers cannot perform this assessment, leading to incorrect alteration prescriptions, customer dissatisfaction, costly rework (10-15% of orders), and an inability to handle complex garments (suits, wedding dresses, formal wear) that represent the highest-margin work.

## Why It's Hard

**Data collection challenge:** Capturing expert fit assessment requires photographing or filming the garment on the client from multiple angles while the master tailor simultaneously verbalizes their assessment — what they see, what it means, and what alterations it indicates. This narrated assessment protocol is unnatural for tailors who have never articulated their visual reasoning. The photography must be standardized (consistent lighting, background, pose angles) across diverse body types and garment categories, and the client must consent to body-centric photography, which is a sensitive ask in a fitting room context.

**Labeling challenge:** The "correct" assessment is subjective — two master tailors looking at the same garment on the same body may prescribe different alteration approaches that both produce excellent results. One might adjust the shoulder to fix a collar roll issue; another might adjust the collar directly. The label space is not a simple classification but a structured prescription (list of alterations with locations, directions, and magnitudes) where multiple valid prescriptions exist. Inter-rater reliability between expert tailors is estimated at 0.6-0.7 for specific alteration prescriptions, though higher (0.85+) for identifying that a fit issue exists.

**Deployment challenge:** The system must be faster and more trustworthy than the expert it replaces. A master tailor completes assessment in 15-30 seconds — if the AI takes 2 minutes and requires the staff person to upload photos from specific angles, the workflow is slower and adoption fails. The system must work with a smartphone camera in variable fitting room lighting, not a controlled studio setup. And it must earn trust: a master tailor will reject the system the first time it misses a body asymmetry they would have caught.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A smartphone-based system that guides the intake staff through a standardized photo capture protocol (front, back, left side, right side, plus any areas the client flags). The computer vision model analyzes the garment-on-body images to: (1) detect fit issues by category (shoulder alignment, back width, chest ease, waist suppression, hip balance, sleeve length, trouser break, hemline level), (2) assess severity of each issue (minor adjustment vs. structural alteration), (3) identify fabric type and construction features that affect alteration approach, (4) generate a structured alteration prescription with recommended sequence, and (5) flag issues that require a master tailor's judgment versus those that a less experienced seamstress can handle. The system learns from each completed alteration — comparing the prescribed alterations, the actually performed alterations, and the client satisfaction outcome.

## Implementation Path
Initial training requires 5,000-10,000 annotated garment-on-body photo sets across the major garment categories (trousers, jackets/blazers, dresses, skirts, shirts) with expert tailor assessments as labels. Data collection through partnership with 20-30 high-volume alteration shops, each contributing 10-20 annotated cases per week over 6-12 months. Transfer learning from existing pose estimation and garment segmentation models accelerates development. First deployment targets the most common and standardized alterations (trouser hemming, waist adjustment, sleeve shortening) where the assessment is more consistent across experts, expanding to complex garments (suits, wedding dresses) as the model matures. 3-5 person team (CV engineers, garment construction domain expert, data annotator team), 12-18 months to first usable product. Requires ongoing expert feedback loop for continuous improvement.
