# Last-Minute Event Change Management for Coordinators

**Industry:** [[catering-companies|Catering Companies]]
**Type:** Worker Life Changing
**One-liner:** Event coordinators stop being human shock absorbers for client changes that cascade through procurement, prep, staffing, and delivery with no system to trace the blast radius.
**Tags:** #llm #text-generation #nlp #worker-facing

## The Problem
Event coordinators at catering companies live in a state of perpetual change management. A client texts at 4 PM on Thursday that the Friday rehearsal dinner guest count jumped from 65 to 90. Another emails at 9 AM that three tables need to be vegan instead of the standard menu. A venue calls to say the loading dock is unavailable and setup must use the front entrance, adding 30 minutes to load-in. Each change triggers a cascade: guest count changes affect food quantities, staffing levels, rental orders, and delivery vehicle capacity. Menu swaps affect procurement (is the ingredient available from the supplier by tomorrow?), prep schedules, and allergen matrices. Venue changes affect setup timelines, equipment selections, and crew call times. The coordinator must manually trace each change through every affected system — calling the kitchen, texting the crew lead, emailing the rental company, updating the BEO, revising the invoice — while fielding the next change from a different event. During peak season, a coordinator may be managing 8-12 active events simultaneously, each with its own change stream.

## Why It Matters to the Worker
Coordinators describe the role during event season as "being on call 18 hours a day for problems that aren't emergencies but feel like emergencies." The cognitive load of tracking which changes have been communicated to which stakeholders — did I tell the kitchen about the vegan swap? did I update the rental order? did I adjust the crew count? — produces chronic anxiety and frequent errors of omission. The highest-performing coordinators burn out within 2-3 years. The errors they make under load (forgetting to update a staffing plan, missing a dietary change) create event-day crises that the coordinator then absorbs emotionally as personal failures.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A change management interface where the coordinator logs a client change (or it is extracted from an incoming text/email) and the system automatically maps the downstream impacts: food quantity adjustments, staffing implications, rental order changes, delivery logistics, BEO revisions, and invoice updates. The coordinator reviews and approves each downstream action rather than manually generating them. Notifications to affected parties (kitchen, crew lead, rental vendor) are drafted and sent upon approval. The system maintains a change log per event so nothing falls through the cracks and the coordinator can see at a glance which changes have been fully propagated and which still have pending downstream actions.

## Impact If Solved
Reduces coordinator change-processing time from 15-25 minutes per change (across all downstream communications) to 3-5 minutes of review-and-approve. Eliminates the "did I forget to tell someone?" anxiety loop that is the primary source of coordinator stress and burnout. Extends average coordinator tenure in the role from 2-3 years to a sustainable career track.
