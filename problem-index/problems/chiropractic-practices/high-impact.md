# Postural Compensation Pattern Recognition

**Industry:** [[chiropractic-practices|Chiropractic Practices]]
**Type:** High Impact
**One-liner:** Chiropractors stop relying solely on 20 years of clinical intuition to see the connected compensatory patterns across a patient's entire kinetic chain — because a vision system identifies shoulder elevation, head forward translation, pelvic tilt, foot pronation, and their biomechanical relationships from standardized photos and movement screens, making expert-level postural assessment available to every DC on day one.

## The Problem
Chiropractic clinical skill is fundamentally about pattern recognition across the kinetic chain. An experienced DC looks at a patient standing in their underwear and sees a story: the right foot pronates slightly, which externally rotates the tibia, which creates a functional leg-length discrepancy, which tilts the pelvis, which the lumbar spine compensates for with a lateral curve, which the thoracic spine counter-curves to maintain eye-level, which drives one shoulder high, which creates cervical tension on the opposite side. The patient presents with neck pain, but the experienced chiropractor knows the neck is the symptom — the foot is the cause.

This whole-body compensatory pattern recognition is the single most valuable clinical skill in chiropractic care. It determines treatment accuracy (adjusting the neck when the problem is the foot provides temporary relief but not resolution), treatment efficiency (addressing root causes requires fewer visits), and patient outcomes (patients whose compensatory patterns are correctly identified recover faster and have lower recurrence rates). New graduates see individual joint dysfunction in isolation. It takes 10-15 years of clinical experience to consistently read compensatory chains across the entire body.

The problem is compounded by technique diversity. Diversified, Gonstead, Activator, Thompson, and CBP (Chiropractic BioPhysics) each assess posture through different frameworks. A Gonstead practitioner focuses on the "Gonstead level" (pelvic unleveling and its spinal consequences). A CBP practitioner measures sagittal balance and lateral translation. An Activator practitioner uses leg-length inequality as the primary indicator. These systems overlap but don't agree on assessment methodology, creating inconsistency even among experienced practitioners.

## Why It's Hard

**Data collection challenge:** Capturing this expertise requires standardized postural photographs (anterior, posterior, lateral views) paired with the experienced chiropractor's clinical assessment of compensatory patterns, treatment decisions, and patient outcomes over a full treatment course. The photographs must be taken consistently (same distance, same lighting, same patient positioning) to enable computer vision analysis. Palpation findings (joint mobility, muscle tone, tenderness) are critical clinical inputs that cannot be captured by photographs alone — they must be recorded as structured data annotations. This means building a data collection protocol that adds 5-10 minutes of structured documentation to every new patient exam, in a profession already drowning in documentation burden.

**Labeling challenge:** Different technique systems produce different assessments of the same patient. A Gonstead practitioner and a CBP practitioner examining the same patient may identify different primary subluxation levels and different compensatory patterns. Both may achieve good outcomes through different treatment approaches. The labeling question becomes: whose assessment is "correct"? The answer must be outcome-based — the model should learn which postural assessment patterns predict the best patient outcomes, regardless of which technique philosophy they align with. This requires outcome tracking over weeks to months per patient.

**Deployment challenge:** The model must augment, not replace, clinical judgment. Chiropractors will reject a system that tells them what to adjust — but they will adopt a system that shows them compensatory patterns they might have missed, especially patterns outside their technique specialty. The output must be a visual overlay highlighting biomechanical relationships, not a diagnosis. It must present findings as "consider evaluating" rather than "adjust here." And it must work within the existing exam workflow — processing standardized photos in under 60 seconds while the patient is still in the room.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A camera-based assessment system integrated into the exam room. The patient stands on a marked floor position, and a calibrated camera (or smartphone on a standardized mount) captures anterior, posterior, and bilateral lateral photos. The model identifies anatomical landmarks (acromion, ASIS, PSIS, malleoli, tragus, C7 spinous process), measures postural deviations, and — critically — identifies compensatory relationships between regions. Output: a visual body map showing primary deviations and predicted compensatory chains, comparison to prior visits showing change over time, and correlation with outcome data from similar presentation patterns. The system generates objective postural findings formatted for insurance documentation, converting subjective visual assessment into measurable data points that support medical necessity.

## Implementation Path
Phase 1: Pose estimation model (building on MediaPipe/OpenPose) trained on chiropractic-specific anatomical landmarks with standardized photo protocol. 500-1,000 patients with expert-annotated postural assessments for initial training. Phase 2: Compensatory pattern classification — training on expert assessments paired with treatment approaches and outcomes. Requires 2,000-5,000 patient treatment courses with pre/post photos and outcome measures. Phase 3: Outcome prediction — which compensatory patterns respond best to which treatment approaches. Requires longitudinal outcome data (PROMIS, ODI, NDI scores) across 5,000+ completed treatment courses. Timeline: 12-18 months for Phase 1 deployment, 24-36 months for full compensatory pattern recognition. Team: 3-4 ML engineers, 1 biomechanics specialist, clinical advisory panel of DCs from multiple technique backgrounds.
