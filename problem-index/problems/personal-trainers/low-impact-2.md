# Scope-Aware Nutrition Guidance

**Industry:** [[personal-trainers|Personal Trainers]]
**Type:** Low Impact (Customisation Opportunity)
**One-liner:** Trainers want to help clients with nutrition but risk scope-of-practice violations; existing nutrition apps know nothing about training context or legal boundaries.
**Tags:** #llm #text-generation #nlp #compliance

## The Problem
Clients constantly ask their trainer for nutrition advice — meal plans, supplement recommendations, calorie targets, macro splits. Trainers without a registered dietitian (RD) credential cannot legally prescribe meal plans or provide medical nutrition therapy in most US states, but the line between "general nutrition education" (permitted) and "individualized dietary counseling" (restricted) is blurry. Most trainers either avoid the topic entirely (losing a major value-add) or wing it with generic advice that may cross legal boundaries, especially when working with clients who have diabetes, eating disorders, or food allergies.

## What Already Exists
MyFitnessPal, Cronometer, and MacroFactor handle calorie and macro tracking. Nutrition coaching platforms like Healthie and Practice Better serve RDs. These tools either provide generic tracking with no training context (they don't know the client just started a hypertrophy block and needs a caloric surplus) or are built for licensed dietitians and assume clinical scope.

## The Customisation Gap
A trainer-specific nutrition tool must understand scope-of-practice rules by state, clearly delineate between permitted general guidance (e.g., "aim for 1g protein per pound of bodyweight") and restricted clinical advice (e.g., "follow this 1,800-calorie renal diet"), and integrate with the client's current training phase to provide contextually appropriate suggestions. It should flag when a client's question requires referral to an RD and generate the referral language. Output must be framed as education, not prescription, with audit-trail documentation.

## Impact If Solved
Trainers add a high-value nutrition education component to their services — worth $50-100/month in additional revenue per client — without legal risk. Clients get integrated guidance instead of managing separate apps for training and nutrition.
