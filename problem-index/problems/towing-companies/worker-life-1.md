# Tow Operator Roadside Safety

**Industry:** [[towing-companies|Towing Companies]]
**Type:** Worker Life Changing
**One-liner:** Tow operators work inches from highway traffic at night, in rain, on blind curves — a rear-facing awareness system gives them eyes in the back of their head when their hands are on the winch.
**Tags:** #cnn #binary-classification #computer-vision #worker-facing

## The Problem
Tow truck operators are struck and killed at a rate that makes towing one of the most dangerous occupations in the United States — the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration reports an average of 60+ tow operator fatalities per year, with the majority involving being struck by passing vehicles while working roadside. The operator's attention is necessarily focused on the disabled vehicle: assessing damage, positioning the truck, operating the winch or wheel-lift, and securing the load. During this 10–30 minute window, they are exposed to passing traffic with only cones, strobes, and a reflective vest as protection. Night calls (which represent 35–40% of volume) and highway shoulder work are the highest-risk scenarios. Operators develop a hypervigilance — constantly glancing over their shoulder — that adds cognitive load and still cannot protect against a vehicle approaching from a blind spot at 65 mph.

## Why It Matters to the Worker
Every operator knows someone who has been hit. The industry calls them "struck-by" incidents, and near-misses are a weekly occurrence for highway-rotation operators. This constant threat creates chronic stress that compounds the physical demands of the job — heavy lifting, weather exposure, unpredictable hours. Operators with families describe the anxiety of every late-night highway call. Many experienced operators leave the industry specifically because the risk is no longer worth the pay, creating a workforce retention crisis that hits small companies hardest.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A rear-facing camera system mounted on the tow truck's light bar that uses computer vision to detect approaching vehicles, calculate closing speed and trajectory, and trigger a graduated alert — ambient light change for distant vehicles, audible tone for approaching vehicles, and urgent alarm with directional indicator for vehicles that are not moving over. The system must function in low light, rain, and glare conditions and deliver alerts through a wearable (wristband vibration or bone-conduction earpiece) so the operator receives warnings even when facing away from the camera and operating loud equipment.

## Impact If Solved
Even a 2-second earlier warning changes outcomes — an operator who hears an alert has time to move behind the truck bed or the disabled vehicle. Reducing struck-by incidents by 30–50% would save 20–30 lives per year industry-wide and dramatically improve operator willingness to take highway rotation shifts.
