# AI-Assisted Sewer Camera Inspection and Pipe Condition Assessment

## The Problem
Sewer camera inspection is one of the highest-value diagnostics in residential and commercial plumbing. An experienced drain technician watches the camera footage in real time, identifies defects as they appear, estimates their severity, and mentally assembles a condition report that determines the recommended repair (spot repair, pipe lining, full replacement) and its urgency. This assessment capability takes years to develop — junior technicians frequently miss subtle indicators that an expert would catch: a slight pipe deflection that signals root intrusion beginning, early-stage cracking that hasn't yet caused a leak, grease accumulation at a specific joint that will cause a backup within 6 months. The consequence of a missed finding is either a repeat service call (and a dissatisfied customer) or a pipe failure that becomes an emergency. Currently, this tacit assessment capability means that sewer camera inspections can only be fully interpreted by experienced technicians — junior staff can run the camera but need an expert to review the footage.

## Why It's Unsolved
Sewer pipe video analysis is a technically demanding computer vision problem: the camera moves through the pipe, lighting conditions are variable, the pipe interior is dirty and visually complex, and defect types overlap (root intrusion can look like debris accumulation; cracking can be obscured by biofilm). Municipal sewer infrastructure inspection has developed PACP (Pipeline Assessment Certification Program) coding standards and some automated detection tools (Envirosight ROVVER X AI) — but these are designed for large-diameter municipal sewers, are priced for infrastructure budgets ($50K+), and don't apply to residential/commercial plumbing's smaller diameter pipes and different defect distributions. The residential plumbing market has received no equivalent investment.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A mobile-connected AI layer for standard residential sewer inspection cameras (RIDGID SeeSnake, Spartan Tool) that processes the camera video stream in real time or from post-run upload. The model detects and classifies defects frame-by-frame: root intrusion (severity: light / moderate / heavy), pipe offset (degrees of deflection), cracking (longitudinal vs. circumferential, depth), bellied sections, grease/debris accumulation, and pipe material identification. It produces a structured condition report with: footage timestamp for each finding, defect category and severity, recommended action (monitor / schedule repair / urgent repair), and a customer-facing summary with photos of key findings. The technician reviews the report, adds contextual notes, and delivers a professional assessment to the homeowner in the driveway — not after spending 30 minutes reviewing footage back at the office.

## Impact If Solved
Enables junior technicians to produce master-quality sewer inspection reports, expanding the firm's diagnostic capacity without requiring senior tech supervision on every camera run. Increases upsell rate on legitimate repair recommendations — a customer shown annotated footage with specific defect locations and severity is far more likely to approve a repair than one told verbally "you have root intrusion." Industry data suggests documented inspection findings improve repair authorization rates by 30-50%.
