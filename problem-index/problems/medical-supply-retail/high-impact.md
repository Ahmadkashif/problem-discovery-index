# Insurance Eligibility Verification and Prior Authorization for DME

**Industry:** [[medical-supply-retail|Medical Supply Retail]]
**Type:** High Impact
**One-liner:** The day after this is solved, a DME supplier submits a wheelchair order and gets a prior authorization decision in minutes instead of days, eliminating the 45–90 minute manual phone-fax-portal cycle that currently consumes 40% of revenue cycle staff time and delays patient equipment delivery by weeks.
**Tags:** #llm #bert #text-classification #nlp #compliance #revenue-impact

## The Problem
Every DME order above a cost threshold — wheelchairs, hospital beds, CPAP machines, wound VAC systems — requires prior authorization from the patient's insurance payer before the supplier can deliver and bill. This process involves verifying the patient's eligibility and benefits, collecting clinical documentation from the referring physician (Certificate of Medical Necessity, detailed written orders, progress notes), assembling the submission package with correct HCPCS codes and modifiers, submitting via payer-specific channels (fax, web portal, phone), and then following up through multiple rounds of "additional information requested" cycles. A single prior auth can take 45–90 minutes of active staff time spread across 3–15 business days of calendar time. First-pass denial rates run 20–35% — often for documentation technicalities like a missing physician signature date, an incomplete CMN field, or a diagnosis code that doesn't match the payer's LCD (Local Coverage Determination) criteria. Each denial triggers a resubmission cycle that doubles the labor cost.

Staff who handle prior auth must simultaneously understand clinical terminology (to interpret physician notes), insurance policy language (to match coverage criteria), HCPCS coding rules (to select the correct billing codes), and payer-specific submission quirks (Humana wants the CMN faxed to one number, Aetna requires portal submission, Medicare Advantage plans each have different LCD interpretations). This knowledge is deeply institutional and takes 6–12 months to develop in a new hire. Turnover in these roles is high because the work is repetitive, adversarial (payers are incentivized to deny), and emotionally draining (patients waiting for critical equipment call daily asking why it hasn't arrived).

## Why It's Unsolved
The core difficulty is that prior authorization is not a single standardized process — it is a matrix of payer-specific rules, state-level coverage policies, product-specific documentation requirements, and physician-specific documentation habits that interact in combinatorial ways. Medicare has LCDs for each DME category; Medicaid rules vary by state; commercial payers each publish their own medical policies that may or may not align with Medicare criteria. An LLM-based system must parse and reason over this entire policy landscape in real time, cross-referencing the patient's clinical documentation against the applicable coverage criteria to predict whether a submission will be approved, denied, or pended for additional information. The second barrier is data access: clinical documentation arrives in inconsistent formats (handwritten CMNs, scanned PDFs of physician notes, structured EHR exports), requiring robust OCR and document understanding before any classification can begin. The third barrier is regulatory: any system that automates coverage determinations must be auditable and explainable, because denials can be appealed and the basis for the original submission must be defensible.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A system that ingests a new DME order (patient demographics, diagnosis codes, prescribed equipment, referring physician), automatically pulls the patient's insurance eligibility and benefits via EDI 270/271 transactions, retrieves the applicable payer coverage policy (LCD, medical policy document), parses the clinical documentation package (CMN, physician notes, face-to-face encounter records) using OCR and NLP, and then performs a pre-submission audit: does this documentation package meet the coverage criteria for this specific payer, this specific product, and this specific patient? The system flags gaps ("CMN Section B is missing the physician signature date," "progress notes do not document a face-to-face encounter within the required 6-month window," "diagnosis code L89.312 maps to a wound care supply set that requires wound measurements not present in the notes") and predicts the probability of first-pass approval. For high-confidence submissions, it auto-generates the submission package and routes it to the correct payer channel. For flagged submissions, it generates a specific checklist of what the referring physician needs to provide before submission.

## Impact If Solved
A 60–70% reduction in prior auth labor hours per order would save a mid-size DME supplier ($5–15M revenue) $150K–$400K annually in billing staff costs alone. First-pass approval rates would increase from 65–80% to 90%+, reducing the average order-to-delivery time from 2–3 weeks to 3–5 days and dramatically improving patient satisfaction and equipment utilization rates. At industry scale, this represents $3–5B in recoverable administrative waste.
