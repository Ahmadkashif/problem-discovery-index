## 1. DME Fit Prediction from Patient Measurements and Condition
#cnn #gradient-boosting #multiclass-classification #computer-vision #tabular-ml #tacit-knowledge-ml

**Problem statement:** Capture and replicate the tacit expertise of experienced DME fitters who look at a patient — their body habitus, posture, gait pattern, skin condition, and functional movement — and intuitively know which wheelchair seat width, cushion type, back support configuration, or orthotic design will work before taking a single measurement. A veteran fitter with 15 years of experience can watch a patient transfer from bed to chair and immediately narrow the wheelchair selection from 200 options to 3, predict which cushion will prevent pressure injuries for that specific body type, and anticipate fitting adjustments that a new fitter would only discover after the patient returns with complaints. This tacit pattern recognition — built from thousands of fittings — is the single most valuable and most fragile asset in a DME operation.

**ML task:** Multiclass classification (equipment configuration recommendation) + regression (predicted patient satisfaction and clinical outcome scores per configuration)
**Input data:** Structured patient data (height, weight, BMI, diagnosis codes, functional mobility assessment scores such as the Functional Independence Measure), photographs or short video clips of the patient in their home or clinic environment (seated posture, transfer technique, gait if ambulatory) captured via smartphone, historical fitting records (equipment selected, configuration parameters, modifications made post-delivery, returns and refitting events) from Brightree or equivalent DME software, and — critically — expert fitter annotations on why they selected a particular configuration ("patient has significant pelvic obliquity, needs a contoured cushion with left build-up" or "patient's trunk control is poor, needs a recline back with lateral supports").
**Target:** A ranked list of 3–5 recommended equipment configurations per patient, each specifying: product model, key dimensional parameters (seat width, depth, back height), accessory selections (cushion, headrest, armrests, footrests), and HCPCS codes. Secondary target: predicted probability of equipment return or modification request within 90 days per configuration.
**Evaluation metric:** Top-3 accuracy (does the expert fitter's actual selection appear in the model's top 3 recommendations?) targeting > 80%. For the return-risk prediction, AUC-ROC with asymmetric cost — a false negative (predicting low return risk for a configuration that gets returned) costs $500–$2,000 in logistics and refitting, while a false positive (flagging a good configuration) costs only a few minutes of fitter review. Secondary metric: reduction in 90-day equipment return rate compared to baseline.
**Scope:** This is a 12–18 month build for a first product category (manual wheelchairs), with significant data collection challenges. The core difficulty is that the expert fitter's decision process is multi-modal — they integrate visual assessment, physical examination, patient-reported preferences, and clinical documentation — and much of their reasoning is pre-verbal ("I just know this patient needs a Jay J3 cushion, not the J2"). Data collection requires instrumenting the fitting workflow: smartphone photos/video at intake, structured capture of the fitter's equipment selection and rationale, and 90-day follow-up on outcomes (returns, modifications, patient satisfaction). Labeling is challenging because expert fitters may disagree — one prefers Jay cushions, another prefers ROHO, and both achieve good outcomes through different paths. The model must learn a distribution of valid configurations, not a single right answer. The vision component (CNN or ViT for body habitus and posture assessment) must be combined with tabular features (measurements, diagnosis, functional scores) through an ensemble architecture. Team: 2–3 ML engineers, 1 computer vision specialist, 1 clinical advisor (experienced ATP — Assistive Technology Professional). Deployment must be a real-time mobile tool that returns recommendations within 10 seconds during the intake assessment — slower than that and fitters will skip it. Privacy is paramount: patient images must be processed on-device or in a HIPAA-compliant pipeline with explicit consent.
**Data availability:** No public datasets exist for DME fitting decisions. Brightree and competitor systems contain historical order data (equipment selected per patient) but typically lack the visual and contextual information that drove the selection. Building the training dataset requires a 6–12 month instrumented data collection phase across 10–20 DME suppliers with experienced fitters willing to annotate their reasoning. The ATP (Assistive Technology Professional) certification community may serve as a source of expert annotators. Equipment manufacturers (Permobil, Sunrise Medical, Invacare) may partner on data collection since better fitting reduces their warranty and return costs.

## 2. Prior Authorization Approval Prediction
#gradient-boosting #bert #binary-classification #nlp #tabular-ml #compliance #revenue-impact

**Problem statement:** Predict whether a DME prior authorization submission will be approved, denied, or pended on first pass, given the patient's clinical documentation, the requested equipment, and the specific payer's coverage criteria — enabling staff to fix documentation gaps before submission rather than after denial.

**ML task:** Multi-class classification (approved / denied-documentation / denied-medical-necessity / denied-coverage / pended-additional-info)
**Input data:** Structured order data (HCPCS codes, modifiers, diagnosis codes, patient demographics), clinical documentation features extracted via NLP from CMN forms, physician notes, and face-to-face encounter records (presence/absence of key clinical elements like functional mobility scores, weight, diagnosis-specific criteria), payer identifier and plan type, historical submission outcomes for the same payer-product combination, and extracted coverage policy features (key criteria from the applicable LCD or payer medical policy).
**Target:** Submission outcome class with associated probability, plus — for predicted denials — the specific documentation gap or coverage criterion that is likely to trigger the denial (e.g., "CMN Section B question 4 is blank," "physician notes do not document a qualifying mobility examination").
**Evaluation metric:** Recall on the "denied" classes is paramount — missing a denial prediction means the submission goes out with a preventable gap. Target recall > 90% for denial predictions with precision > 70% to avoid excessive false alarms that slow down the submission workflow. F1-weighted across all classes as the balancing metric.
**Scope:** A gradient-boosted model on structured features (codes, payer, demographics) can achieve a useful baseline in 3–4 months with a team of 2 ML engineers and 1 domain expert (experienced billing specialist). Adding NLP-based features from clinical documentation (BERT fine-tuned on medical/DME text to extract key clinical elements) extends the timeline to 6–8 months but significantly improves performance on documentation-gap denials. The biggest engineering challenge is building the document ingestion pipeline — CMN forms arrive as scanned PDFs, faxes, and EHR exports in dozens of formats, and OCR accuracy on handwritten CMN fields is inconsistent.
**Data availability:** DME suppliers have rich historical data on submission outcomes (approved/denied/pended) tied to the order details and payer. Brightree's database contains this for thousands of suppliers. The gap is in linking outcomes to the specific clinical documentation that accompanied each submission — most systems store the outcome but not a machine-readable version of the supporting documents. A data partnership with 5–10 DME suppliers willing to share anonymized submission-outcome data, paired with a document digitization effort, would yield a training set of 50K–200K labeled submissions within 6 months.

## 3. Reorder Timing Prediction for Recurring DME Supplies
#gradient-boosting #time-series-forecasting #tabular-ml #revenue-impact

**Problem statement:** Predict when a patient will need to reorder recurring medical supplies (CPAP masks and filters, wound care dressings, ostomy supplies, diabetic testing supplies) based on their usage pattern, insurance coverage cycle, and clinical condition changes — enabling proactive outreach before the patient runs out and before the insurance-eligible reorder date.

**ML task:** Time-series regression (predicted next reorder date per patient per supply category) + binary classification (will the patient reorder at all, or have they churned?)
**Input data:** Historical order dates and quantities per patient per supply SKU, insurance eligibility windows (e.g., Medicare allows CPAP mask replacement every 3 months), patient clinical data (diagnosis, compliance data from CPAP devices via ResMed AirView or Philips Care Orchestrator APIs), seasonal patterns (allergy season increases CPAP supply usage), and patient communication history (did they respond to the last outreach call?).
**Target:** Predicted reorder date (within a 7-day window) and churn probability (will this patient reorder from this supplier or switch to an online competitor?).
**Evaluation metric:** Mean absolute error on reorder date prediction (target < 5 days). For churn prediction, AUC-ROC with emphasis on recall — identifying patients at risk of churning is more valuable than precision, since the cost of a proactive outreach call to a non-churning patient is minimal.
**Scope:** Straightforward tabular ML problem buildable in 2–3 months by 1–2 ML engineers. The cold-start problem is minimal because most patients have 2+ years of reorder history. LightGBM on structured features (days since last order, average reorder interval, insurance window, compliance metrics) will outperform more complex approaches for this data volume. Deployment is a daily batch job that generates a prioritized outreach list for customer service reps.
**Data availability:** DME suppliers have complete order history in their billing systems. CPAP compliance data is available via manufacturer APIs (ResMed, Philips). Insurance eligibility windows are codified in CMS guidelines and payer contracts. The main gap is capturing the reason for non-reorder (patient switched suppliers vs. patient's condition resolved vs. patient deceased) — this requires linking to patient status data that may not be in the supplier's system.

## 4. Claim Denial Root Cause Classification
#bert #multiclass-classification #nlp #compliance #revenue-impact

**Problem statement:** Automatically classify insurance claim denials into actionable root cause categories — distinguishing between documentation errors (fixable by the supplier), medical necessity disputes (require physician involvement), coverage exclusions (require patient notification), and billing/coding errors (fixable by the billing team) — from the unstructured denial notifications that arrive as EDI 835 remittance advice, payer portal messages, and faxed letters.

**ML task:** Multiclass classification (8–12 denial root cause categories) with entity extraction (specific missing document, incorrect code, or policy reference)
**Input data:** Denial notification text (from EDI 835 CARC/RARC codes, payer portal denial letters, and faxed denial notices processed via OCR), original claim details (HCPCS codes, modifiers, diagnosis codes, billed amount), payer identifier and plan type, and historical denial-resolution pairs (what action resolved similar denials in the past).
**Target:** Root cause category (e.g., "missing-CMN-signature," "expired-face-to-face-encounter," "non-covered-diagnosis," "incorrect-modifier," "exceeded-frequency-limit"), responsible party (supplier billing team, physician's office, patient), and recommended resolution action with estimated success probability.
**Evaluation metric:** Macro F1-score across root cause categories, targeting > 0.85. Weighted by denial dollar amount — correctly classifying a $5,000 power wheelchair denial matters more than a $50 supply reorder denial. Secondary metric: time-to-resolution reduction compared to manual triage.
**Scope:** A BERT-based classifier fine-tuned on denial notification text can reach useful accuracy in 3–4 months with 10K–20K labeled denial examples. The entity extraction component (identifying the specific missing document or incorrect code) adds 2–3 months. Team: 1–2 ML engineers, 1 billing domain expert for labeling guidance. The hardest part is handling the long tail of rare denial reason codes — some codes appear in < 0.1% of denials but represent high-dollar claims.
**Data availability:** DME suppliers accumulate thousands of denial notifications annually, and most are stored digitally (EDI 835 files are structured; portal messages are semi-structured; faxed letters require OCR). A partnership with 3–5 mid-to-large DME suppliers would yield 50K–100K labeled denials within the first year. The labeling effort is moderate — experienced billing staff can categorize denials quickly because they do this work manually every day.

## 5. Inventory Demand Forecasting by Product Category and Payer Mix
#gradient-boosting #time-series-forecasting #tabular-ml #data-integration

**Problem statement:** Predict weekly demand per product category at an individual DME supplier location, accounting for seasonal patterns (respiratory equipment demand spikes in winter), payer mix shifts (a large local employer switching insurance carriers changes the coverage landscape overnight), and referral source activity (a new orthopedic surgeon opening a practice nearby will increase mobility equipment referrals within 3–6 months).

**ML task:** Time-series regression (weekly unit demand per product category per location)
**Input data:** Historical order volumes by product category, HCPCS code, and payer (2+ years), referral source data (which physicians refer to this supplier and their referral volume trends), local demographic and health data (CMS chronic condition prevalence data by county, Census Bureau population and age demographics), seasonal and weather features, payer contract changes (new contracts, lost contracts, reimbursement rate changes), and competitor intelligence (new DME supplier openings in the market area, online DME retailer penetration).
**Target:** Weekly unit demand forecast per product category (wheelchairs, respiratory, wound care, orthotics, etc.) at each supplier location for a 4–12 week horizon, translated into purchasing and stocking recommendations.
**Evaluation metric:** Weighted MAPE (mean absolute percentage error) where weights reflect product margin and carrying cost — overstocking a $200 wheelchair cushion that expires in 12 months is less costly than overstocking a $50 wound care kit that expires in 90 days. Target WMAPE < 15% at the weekly-category level.
**Scope:** A standard demand forecasting problem with DME-specific feature engineering (payer mix, referral patterns, CMS data). LightGBM with calendar, demographic, and payer features can be built in 3–4 months by 1–2 ML engineers. The cold-start challenge is real for smaller suppliers with limited history — hierarchical models that pool across similar suppliers (same metro area, same size, same product mix) address this. Deployment is a weekly batch job feeding into the purchasing workflow.
**Data availability:** Order history is available from Brightree and similar DME software. CMS provides public datasets on chronic condition prevalence, Medicare utilization, and provider referral patterns. Payer mix data is internal to each supplier. The main gap is competitive intelligence — there is no reliable data source on competitor DME supplier activity in a local market, though state licensure databases provide some signal.
