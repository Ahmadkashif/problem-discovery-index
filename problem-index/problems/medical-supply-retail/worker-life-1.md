# DME Fitter Documentation Burden

**Industry:** [[medical-supply-retail|Medical Supply Retail]]
**Type:** Worker Life Changing
**One-liner:** Experienced DME fitters spend 30–45 minutes per patient on paperwork — Certificates of Medical Necessity, detailed written orders, delivery tickets, patient instruction forms — time stolen from the hands-on fitting work they were trained for and that directly affects whether a patient's wheelchair, orthotic, or CPAP actually works correctly.

## The Problem
Every DME fitting generates a documentation trail that must satisfy both clinical and billing requirements. A wheelchair fitting requires a detailed written order specifying seat width, depth, back height, armrest type, footrest configuration, cushion type, and any custom modifications — all tied to specific HCPCS codes and justified by the patient's functional limitations. A CPAP setup requires documenting mask type, pressure settings, humidifier configuration, patient education on cleaning and compliance, and a signed acknowledgment of receipt. A custom orthotic fitting requires measurements, casting notes, alignment specifications, and clinical justification. Fitters complete these forms by hand or type them into Brightree's order entry screens, often after the patient has left, reconstructing details from memory and abbreviated notes. The documentation must be precise enough to withstand a Medicare audit — a missing measurement or an inconsistent code can trigger a post-payment audit that demands the supplier refund thousands of dollars. Fitters report spending 40–50% of their workday on documentation rather than patient-facing fitting.

## Why It Matters to the Worker
DME fitters chose this career because they want to help patients regain mobility, breathe better, or heal faster — not because they enjoy filling out CMN forms. The documentation burden is the single largest source of job dissatisfaction in the role. Fitters who fall behind on paperwork face audit risk for their employer and personal stress from an ever-growing backlog. The cognitive load of remembering precise measurements and configuration details hours after a fitting introduces errors that can result in equipment returns, patient discomfort, and refitting appointments that double the workload. Many experienced fitters leave the field for less documentation-heavy roles, taking decades of fitting expertise with them.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A mobile-first documentation assistant that rides alongside the fitting workflow. During the fitting, the fitter speaks naturally ("patient is 5-10, 220 pounds, uses a standard transfer, needs a 20-inch seat width with a Jay J2 cushion for pressure relief, left leg is 2 inches shorter so we're adding a leg rest extension on the left side") and the system generates the structured documentation: populated CMN fields, detailed written order with correct HCPCS codes and modifiers, delivery ticket, and patient instruction sheet. The system cross-references the spoken configuration against the payer's coverage criteria and flags inconsistencies in real time ("you specified a Group 2 power wheelchair but the physician's order only documents a Group 1 need — do you want to flag this for the physician?"). Post-fitting, the fitter reviews and signs a pre-filled document package instead of building it from scratch.

## Impact If Solved
Fitters reclaim 15–25 minutes per patient encounter — equivalent to 1.5–2.5 additional fittings per day for a full-time fitter. Documentation error rates drop, reducing audit exposure and equipment return rates. Fitter job satisfaction and retention improve measurably, protecting the institutional knowledge that makes complex fittings successful.
