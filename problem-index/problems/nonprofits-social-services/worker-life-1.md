# Case Manager Documentation Burden

**Industry:** [[nonprofits-social-services|Social Services Nonprofits]]
**Type:** Worker Life Changing
**One-liner:** Case managers get 15-20 hours per week back for actual client work instead of typing progress notes, intake assessments, and referral forms that say the same things in slightly different ways.
**Tags:** #large-language-models #transfer-learning #attention-mechanisms #transformers #word-embeddings #entropy-cross-entropy-kl-divergence #worker-facing

## The Problem
A typical case manager in a social services nonprofit carries 30-60 active clients and is required to document every interaction: intake assessments (45-90 minutes of structured questions), progress notes after each meeting or phone call, referral forms when connecting clients to partner agencies, safety plans for at-risk clients, and service plans that must be reviewed and updated every 30-90 days depending on the funder. Each documentation type has its own format — the county contract wants SOAP-style notes, the federal grant wants standardized assessment tools (VI-SPDAT, PHQ-9, AUDIT-C), and the organization's internal system wants a narrative summary. Case managers routinely stay 1-2 hours past their shift completing documentation, and many report that paperwork is the primary reason they leave the field within 2-3 years.

## Why It Matters to the Worker
Case managers enter social services because they want to help people — and then discover that the job is 50-60% data entry. The emotional toll compounds: they know that every hour spent typing a progress note is an hour they could have spent helping a client navigate the housing waitlist or accompanying them to a benefits appointment. Burnout and turnover in social services case management runs 30-40% annually, and exit interviews consistently cite documentation burden as the top or second-highest reason for leaving. The workers who stay develop shortcuts — copy-pasting from previous notes, using vague language to finish faster — which degrades the data quality that funders and supervisors depend on.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A mobile-first tool that lets case managers record a brief voice memo after each client interaction (2-3 minutes of natural speech), then automatically generates a structured progress note in the correct format for each applicable funder, pre-populates referral forms with client data already in the system, flags when standardized assessments are due, and drafts service plan updates based on accumulated notes. The case manager reviews and approves each generated document — the tool augments rather than replaces professional judgment. It runs on the case manager's phone because half their work happens in the field (home visits, shelter walk-throughs, court accompaniments) where laptops are impractical.

## Impact If Solved
Returns 15-20 hours per week to direct client service per case manager. Reduces annual turnover from 35% to an estimated 20% by removing the single largest source of professional dissatisfaction. Improves documentation quality because structured generation from voice memos captures details that exhausted workers skip when typing notes at 7 PM.
