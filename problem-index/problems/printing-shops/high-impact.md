# Print Job Estimating Accuracy and Speed

**Industry:** [[printing-shops|Printing Shops]]
**Type:** High Impact
**One-liner:** Print estimators who quote jobs by mentally simulating the entire production process — calculating paper waste from sheet size and imposition, estimating ink coverage and press time from the artwork, predicting finishing complexity from the binding specification — take 30-90 minutes per quote using knowledge that took a decade to develop, and their departure leaves the shop unable to price work accurately.
**Tags:** #gradient-boosting #regression #tabular-ml #revenue-impact

## The Problem
Print job estimating requires integrating dozens of variables: substrate type and weight, sheet size versus finished size (determining imposition and paper waste), ink coverage (affecting ink cost and drying time), number of press passes (CMYK plus spot colors), press speed for the substrate/ink combination, makeready time (which varies by press, substrate, and color complexity), finishing operations (folding, cutting, stitching, perfect binding, lamination, die-cutting — each with its own setup time and running speed), and packaging/shipping requirements. An experienced estimator holds mental models of how these variables interact for their specific equipment — they know that a heavy cover stock on their Heidelberg Speedmaster runs at 8,000 impressions per hour instead of the rated 12,000, that their folder adds 15 minutes of setup for a gate fold versus a letter fold, and that a 6-color job with tight registration requires an extra 20 minutes of makeready. This shop-specific knowledge is the estimator's value, and it does not transfer to a new hire through any training process — it takes 3-5 years to develop adequate estimating judgment and 10+ years for expert-level accuracy.

## Why It's Unsolved
The variables are continuous and interaction-heavy — paper waste depends on imposition, which depends on sheet size, which is constrained by press size and grain direction, which affects fold quality. Generic cost estimation software uses published production rates that don't match any specific shop's equipment and crew capabilities. EFI Pace and Avanti include estimating modules, but they require manual input of production standards that most small shops have never measured and don't update as equipment ages. The real estimating knowledge is the gap between the published rate and the actual rate for a specific shop on a specific job type — and this gap varies by job complexity in ways that only experience can predict.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A regression model trained on a shop's historical job data — job specifications (substrate, quantity, size, colors, finishing) as inputs, actual production time and material consumption as outputs — that predicts actual cost for a new job specification based on how similar jobs actually performed in that specific shop. The estimator enters the job specification; the model returns a predicted cost with confidence interval and the 3-5 most similar historical jobs for reference. The estimator reviews, adjusts for any unusual features the model hasn't seen, and finalizes the quote. The model continuously improves as new jobs are completed and their actual costs are recorded.

## Impact If Solved
Reduces quote generation time from 30-90 minutes to 10-20 minutes of estimator review. Surfaces systematic pricing errors — which job types the shop consistently under- or over-estimates — enabling margin recovery. For a shop quoting 200 jobs per month with a 25% win rate, improving average quote accuracy by 2% of margin on won jobs recovers $40,000-$100,000 annually. Makes the shop's estimating capability persistent — it survives the estimator's retirement.
