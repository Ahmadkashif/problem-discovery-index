# Pressman Color Matching During Makeready

**Industry:** [[printing-shops|Printing Shops]]
**Type:** Worker Life Changing
**One-liner:** Pressmen who spend 20-45 minutes per job adjusting ink density, water balance, and registration by eye to match a PMS swatch or customer proof — the highest-skill task on the press floor and the one most dependent on years of visual calibration that the pressman cannot articulate — could cut makeready color time in half with a model that predicts ink key settings from spectrophotometer scan bar readings.

## The Problem
Color matching on an offset press is a multi-variable adjustment task. The pressman adjusts ink keys (controlling ink film thickness across the width of the press), water balance (the ratio of fountain solution to ink that affects ink transfer and dot gain), and impression pressure to achieve color that matches the target — a PMS swatch, a customer-supplied proof, or a previous production run. This adjustment is performed by pulling proof sheets during makeready, comparing them visually and with a spectrophotometer to the target, and adjusting parameters iteratively until the match is within tolerance. An experienced pressman with a well-calibrated eye achieves an acceptable match in 10-20 proof sheets (20-30 minutes); a less experienced operator may need 30-50 sheets (45+ minutes). The visual assessment involves judging color under specific lighting conditions (D50 viewing booth), accounting for the substrate's influence on perceived color (uncoated stock shifts color differently than coated), and knowing which ink key adjustments will move the color in the desired direction — a psychophysical skill that develops over 5-10 years of press operation.

## Why It Matters to the Worker
Makeready is the pressman's showcase — it's where they demonstrate their craft. But it's also the most stressful part of the job: every minute of makeready is non-productive time that reduces press utilization, and production managers track makeready times as a performance metric. The pressman is under pressure to achieve color match quickly while maintaining quality. When jobs require tight color matching (brand colors, skin tones, product photography), the pressure intensifies — an out-of-tolerance print run means pulling the job and re-running, which is both a waste and a visible failure. Experienced pressmen describe the cognitive load of color matching as the most fatiguing aspect of their work — more demanding than the physical operation of the press.

## What a Solution Looks Like
A closed-loop color control system that reads the scan bar (a strip of color patches printed in the trim area of every sheet) with an inline or near-line spectrophotometer, compares the measured color values (L*a*b*) to the target values, and recommends specific ink key adjustments to the pressman. The recommendation accounts for ink/water balance, substrate characteristics, and the current press condition (which the model learns from the pressman's historical adjustment patterns on this press). The pressman reviews the recommendation, applies it, and pulls the next proof — reducing the iterative cycle from 10-20 rounds to 3-5 rounds. The system learns from each job, improving its predictions as it accumulates data on this press, this ink set, and this substrate family.

## Impact If Solved
Reduces color makeready time from 20-45 minutes to 10-15 minutes per job. Reduces proof sheet waste by 50-70% (from 10-20 sheets to 3-5 sheets per makeready). Makes acceptable color matching achievable by less experienced pressmen — a junior operator with the system's guidance achieves results that previously required 10+ years of visual calibration. For a shop running 8 jobs per day on an offset press, recovering 20 minutes of makeready per job saves 2.7 hours of production time daily.
