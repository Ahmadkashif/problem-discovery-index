# Solar Installers

**Category:** Trades & Home Services

## Industry Overview

The US residential and commercial solar installation industry generates approximately $25 billion annually. The market has grown at 15-25% annually over the past decade, driven by declining panel costs, state and federal incentives (federal ITC, net metering policies), and rising utility rates. The market includes both large national installers (Sunrun, SunPower, Tesla Energy) and thousands of regional and local solar contractors. Regional firms typically differentiate on local expertise (utility relationships, permit approval timelines, local incentive knowledge) and installation quality.

The industry is capital-intensive for customers — a residential system is $15,000-$30,000 — and the sales cycle is long (2-8 weeks from lead to signed contract). Installation timelines are often delayed by interconnection queue wait times with utilities and permit processing timelines with municipalities.

## Dominant Platforms

- **Aurora Solar** — the leading solar design and proposal generation platform; simulates energy production from roof layout and shade analysis
- **Solargraf / EagleSoft** — solar design and proposal platforms with competitive feature sets
- **JobNimbus / HubSpot** — CRM and pipeline management platforms used for the long solar sales cycle
- **Energy Toolbase** — commercial solar financial modeling and storage optimization platform
- **SolarEdge / Enphase** — inverter monitoring platforms with system performance data

## Workforce and Knowledge Structure

Solar installation involves both technical design knowledge and field installation craft. Design engineers use simulation software (Aurora Solar) to optimize system layout for roof geometry, shade conditions, and load matching. Field crews develop installation technique knowledge for racking system installation, weatherproofing roof penetrations, and conduit routing. The critical tacit knowledge that differentiates experienced solar installers is in weatherproofing — ensuring that roof penetrations for conduit and mounting hardware don't create leak paths is a skill developed through experience with failure modes.

Sales expertise is a distinct knowledge domain: reading a customer's utility bill, understanding the local net metering policy and interconnection timeline, and modeling the economics of solar versus battery storage requires market-specific expertise that takes 2-3 years to develop in a specific utility territory.

## Key Economic Pressures

- Customer acquisition cost is very high ($300-$1,200 per installed watt is a common benchmark); efficient lead-to-close conversion is critical
- Interconnection queue wait times are increasing in many utility territories; customers who cancel during the wait period represent sunk sales cost
- Material cost volatility (panel prices, racking, battery storage components) affects fixed-price contract margins on long-timeline projects
- Installation labor efficiency determines project profitability; projects that encounter unexpected roof conditions (structural reinforcement needed, difficult conduit routing) erode margin
