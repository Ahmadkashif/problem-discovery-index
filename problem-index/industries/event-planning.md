# Event Planning

## Profile
**Category:** Specialty Services
**Market Size:** ~$5B US event planning services (B2B and B2C, excluding venues and vendors)
**Tech Maturity:** Low — HoneyBook, Aisle Planner, AllSeated, and Social Tables handle basic planning workflows, but budgeting, vendor coordination, and day-of logistics remain heavily manual and experience-dependent
**Workforce:** Event planners (lead and assistant), event coordinators, day-of coordinators, venue liaisons, vendor managers, and business owners

## Key Pain Themes
Budget estimation is where expertise separates successful planners from struggling ones — an experienced planner hears a client describe their "elegant garden wedding for 150" and immediately knows the real budget is $85K-$110K, while the client says "$50K." This gap between vision and budget causes the majority of client conflicts. Vendor coordination across 10-30 vendors per event (caterer, florist, photographer, DJ, rental company, bakery, lighting, transportation) requires relentless follow-up and timeline management with no central system of record. Day-of execution demands contingency management (weather, vendor no-shows, timeline slippage) that only comes from experience. Client emotional management consumes enormous energy — weddings especially involve family dynamics, stress, and decisions with perceived permanent consequences.

## Current Tech Landscape
HoneyBook handles client management, contracts, and payments for independent planners. Aisle Planner provides collaborative planning tools and vendor management. AllSeated and Social Tables focus on floor plan design and seating arrangements. Planning Pod offers comprehensive event management for corporate events. None address the core expertise challenges: budget estimation from concept, vendor negotiation optimization, or day-of contingency management. Most planners use a combination of spreadsheets, email, and group texts.
