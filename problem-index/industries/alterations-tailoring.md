# Alterations & Tailoring

## Profile
**Category:** Specialty Services
**Market Size:** ~$2B US alterations and tailoring market
**Tech Maturity:** Very Low — the industry operates almost entirely on paper tickets, handwritten measurements, and cash registers. No dominant industry-specific software exists. Some shops use generic POS systems (Square, Clover) for payments, but order tracking, pricing, and workflow management are manual
**Workforce:** Master tailors, seamstresses, fitters, pressers, front desk/intake staff, and apprentices — many shops are 1-3 person operations run by a single master tailor with decades of experience

## Key Pain Themes
The core challenge is that every garment is unique — a "hem pants" ticket can take 10 minutes (simple woven trouser, clean-cut hem) or 90 minutes (lined wool trouser with pick-stitched hem, cuff, and tapered leg) — and accurate assessment of difficulty, time, and price depends entirely on the tailor's expert eye. Master tailors assess fit problems in seconds by reading fabric drape, body asymmetry, ease distribution, and seam stress, but this knowledge is entirely tacit and retires when they do. The workforce is aging rapidly — the average master tailor in the US is 55-65, and few young workers enter the trade because formal apprenticeship paths have collapsed. Pricing is inconsistent and opaque — the same alteration can cost $15 or $75 across shops, and even within a shop, pricing varies by who does the intake. Customer expectation management is a constant friction point: clients bring in a $30 fast-fashion dress and expect couture-level alterations, or request structurally impossible changes without understanding garment construction.

## Current Tech Landscape
There is no dominant software platform for alterations shops. Some use iRoar or Stitch-It for basic ticket management, but adoption is minimal. Most shops use paper tickets pinned to garments, a wall calendar for due dates, and a cash register or Square terminal for payments. Measurement data lives in the tailor's memory or on scraps of paper. No digital system captures the tacit knowledge of fit assessment, and no pricing database exists that accounts for garment construction, fabric type, and alteration complexity.
