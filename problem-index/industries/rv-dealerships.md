# RV Dealerships

## Profile
**Category:** Automotive
**Market Size:** ~$25B US RV sales and service market
**Tech Maturity:** Low — IDS (Integrated Dealer Systems), Lightspeed, and DealerCenter provide basic DMS functionality, but RV-specific complexities (multi-system vehicles, trade-in appraisal, seasonal service demand) are poorly served by tools designed for auto dealerships
**Workforce:** Sales consultants, service technicians (automotive + residential systems), parts managers, trade-in appraisers, finance managers, service advisors, and detailers

## Key Pain Themes
Trade-in valuation is the industry's unique headache — an RV is a vehicle, a house, and an appliance suite combined, making appraisal fundamentally more complex than a car. Water damage is the hidden killer: a roof leak that's been silently destroying the subfloor for two years isn't visible from a walkaround, but it turns a $30,000 trade into a $5,000 wholesale unit. Service departments face extreme seasonality (spring de-winterization and fall winterization create 4-week backlogs) and multi-system complexity where a single vehicle requires automotive, plumbing, electrical, LP gas, and appliance expertise. Sales consultants must know 50-200 unique units on the lot, each with different floor plans, features, and condition issues. Parts sourcing for RV-specific components (awnings, slide-out mechanisms, water heaters) involves specialty suppliers with long lead times.

## Current Tech Landscape
IDS and Lightspeed provide dealer management (sales, F&I, service, parts) but lack RV-specific intelligence. NADA guides provide base values but can't account for RV condition variables (water damage, appliance age, interior wear, modification history). RVTrader and RVUSA handle online listings. Service scheduling uses generic shop management tools not designed for multi-day, multi-system RV repairs. No platform addresses the trade-in appraisal complexity that defines the RV business.
