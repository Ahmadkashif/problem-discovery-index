# Environmental Consultants

## Profile
**Category:** Professional Services
**Market Size:** ~$45B in US environmental consulting; ~15,000 firms ranging from 2-person shops to global engineering conglomerates
**Tech Maturity:** Low — field data collection is paper/tablet-based, lab data integration is manual, regulatory tracking is spreadsheet-based
**Workforce:** Environmental scientists, geologists, hydrogeologists, field technicians, regulatory compliance specialists, project managers

## Key Pain Themes
Phase I and Phase II Environmental Site Assessments are the primary revenue driver, yet each report requires manually searching 15+ federal and state regulatory databases, synthesizing historical records, and adapting 60-70% boilerplate language to site-specific conditions. Field technicians spend nearly as much time documenting sample collection (chain-of-custody forms, field notes, GPS coordinates, site conditions) as they do collecting samples. Lab analytical results arrive in inconsistent formats — PDF, CSV, proprietary LIMS exports — and must be manually compared against dozens of regulatory screening levels that vary by state, media type, and land use. Permit compliance tracking across a portfolio of 50-200 client sites relies on spreadsheets that break when agency deadlines shift or new monitoring requirements are added mid-cycle.

## Current Tech Landscape
EQuIS and Geotracker dominate environmental data management but are expensive, complex to configure, and designed for large remediation programs rather than routine consulting workflows. Field collection apps like Fulcrum and ArcGIS Field Maps handle generic geospatial data but lack environmental-specific features like chain-of-custody tracking, regulatory limit lookups, and lab data import. Most firms under 50 people rely on Word templates, Excel spreadsheets, and manual database searches — the technology gap between what exists and what small-to-mid environmental firms actually use is enormous.
