# Collections Agencies

## Profile
**Category:** Financial Services
**Market Size:** ~$20B US debt collection industry; ~7,000 agencies ranging from 5-person shops to publicly traded firms with thousands of seats
**Tech Maturity:** Medium — FICO Debt Manager, Latitude by Genesys, and Artiva (Ontario Systems) handle account management; predictive dialers (Five9, Genesys, LiveVox) are standard; but analytics, compliance automation, and contact optimization remain largely manual or rule-based
**Workforce:** Collection agents/specialists, skip tracers, compliance officers, account managers, quality assurance analysts, and IT staff managing dialer integrations

## Key Pain Themes
Collection agencies operate on razor-thin margins where the difference between profitability and loss is the ability to reach the right debtor at the right time with the right approach. Agents spend up to 70% of their shift on non-productive contacts — wrong numbers, voicemails, disconnected lines — burning through dialer lists without meaningful conversations. Regulatory pressure from the CFPB's Regulation F, state-level FDCPA variations, and TCPA restrictions creates a compliance minefield where a single scripting error can trigger lawsuits and consent decrees. Skip tracing remains fragmented across LexisNexis, TLO, and credit bureau data, with no unified view of debtor contactability, forcing agents to manually cross-reference multiple systems before each call block.

## Current Tech Landscape
Legacy collection platforms (FICO, Latitude, Artiva) manage accounts and payment plans but offer minimal predictive intelligence — most "scoring" is static segmentation by balance size and days-past-due. Predictive dialers optimize call pacing but not call targeting; they maximize agent talk time without distinguishing between high-probability and low-probability contacts. Compliance monitoring is largely post-hoc through QA call reviews sampling 2-5% of calls, leaving 95%+ of interactions unaudited for FDCPA/Reg F violations.
