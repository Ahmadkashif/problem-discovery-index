# Catering Companies

## Profile
**Category:** Hospitality & Food Service
**Market Size:** ~$65B US catering market
**Tech Maturity:** Low — CaterTrax, Total Party Planner, and generic CRMs exist but most operators run on spreadsheets, group texts, and printed BEOs taped to kitchen walls
**Workforce:** Executive chefs, sous chefs, event coordinators, catering sales managers, kitchen prep staff, service captains, bartenders, delivery/setup crews, dishwashers

## Key Pain Themes
Catering is a high-variance business where every event is a custom project with unique venue constraints, dietary requirements, service styles, and timing. Profitability hinges on accurate pre-event estimation — food quantities, labor hours, rental costs, travel time — and experienced operators carry these estimates in their heads while newer operators systematically over-prep or under-quote. Multi-event days compound the problem: a single kitchen may be producing for a 300-person corporate lunch, a 50-person rehearsal dinner, and a 120-person cocktail reception simultaneously, each with different service timing, plating styles, and dietary matrices. Last-minute client changes (guest count swings, menu substitutions, venue shifts) cascade through procurement, prep schedules, staffing plans, and delivery logistics with no centralized system absorbing the shock.

## Current Tech Landscape
CaterTrax and Better Cater handle basic proposal generation and event management but lack production planning intelligence. Total Party Planner offers BEO templates but no integration with procurement or staffing. Most operators use a patchwork of QuickBooks for invoicing, Google Sheets for event timelines, WhatsApp groups for crew communication, and handwritten prep lists. No widely adopted tool connects the proposal-to-production-to-post-event-P&L pipeline, leaving profitability analysis as a quarterly exercise rather than a per-event practice.
