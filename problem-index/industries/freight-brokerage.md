# Freight Brokerage

**Category:** Logistics & Transportation
**Tags:** #tabular-ml #time-series-forecasting #nlp #revenue-impact #automation

## Industry Overview

The US freight brokerage industry generates approximately $80 billion annually. Freight brokers act as intermediaries between shippers (manufacturers, retailers, distributors) and carriers (trucking companies), matching freight loads to available truck capacity in exchange for a margin on the transaction (typically 15-25% of the all-in rate). The market is served by large national brokers (C.H. Robinson, Echo Global, Coyote) and thousands of smaller regional and independent brokerages.

The economics of freight brokerage are driven by spot rate volatility: brokers profit from their ability to secure capacity at carrier rates below what they charge shippers. In tight capacity markets, brokers who have deep carrier relationships secure capacity when others can't; in loose markets, finding the cheapest carrier rate drives margin.

## Dominant Platforms

- **DAT / Truckstop / ITS Dispatch** — load boards for posting loads and finding carriers; spot rate data
- **McLeod Software / TMW Suite / Mercury Gate** — transportation management systems (TMS) for mid-to-large brokerages
- **Turvo / Flexport** — modern digital freight platform and TMS
- **Parade / Newtrul** — carrier capacity procurement and automation tools

## Workforce and Knowledge Structure

Experienced freight brokers develop carrier relationship depth — knowing which carriers run specific lanes reliably, what their capacity looks like on given days of week, and which carriers are negotiable vs. firm on rate. This carrier network knowledge, built over years of transactions, is the primary competitive advantage of a senior broker over a junior one. Senior brokers can cover a load in 30 minutes by calling 3 carriers they know; junior brokers post to load boards and wait.

Pricing judgment is the second tacit skill — understanding when a market is tightening before it appears in spot rate data, and adjusting bid prices accordingly to protect margin.

## Key Economic Pressures

- Margin compression — as the market has digitized, shippers have more visibility into market rates; fat margins on opaque pricing are harder to sustain
- Carrier capacity management — double-brokering fraud and carrier reliability issues create costly service failures
- Load coverage speed — shippers expect load coverage within hours; slow coverage loses customer relationships
- Market rate volatility — rates move 20-40% within a quarter during volatile periods; brokers locked into shipper contracts at fixed rates get squeezed when carrier costs spike
