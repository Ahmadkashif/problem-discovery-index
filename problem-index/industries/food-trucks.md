# Food Trucks

## Profile
**Category:** Hospitality & Food Service
**Market Size:** ~$1.5B US food truck market; ~35,000 active trucks nationwide
**Tech Maturity:** Low — Square/Clover POS and social media are standard; inventory, location planning, permitting, prep forecasting, and bookkeeping are almost entirely manual or spreadsheet-based
**Workforce:** Owner-operators who simultaneously serve as chef, driver, cashier, and marketer, typically with 1-3 part-time staff

## Key Pain Themes
Revenue is dominated by location selection — the same truck selling the same menu can gross $200 on a bad corner or $2,000 at the right event, and operators rely on years of gut feel to make that daily bet. Permitting is a jurisdictional nightmare: each city, county, and health district has different application forms, fee schedules, inspection cadences, and commissary requirements, and a single lapse can shut an operator down mid-shift. Prep waste is a constant drain because food trucks cannot store surplus the way a brick-and-mortar restaurant can; over-prep means thrown-out product, under-prep means turning away customers at 1 PM. The owner-operator model compresses 14-hour days into a single person doing purchasing, prep, driving, serving, cleaning, social media, and bookkeeping with no administrative support.

## Current Tech Landscape
Square and Clover dominate POS, providing basic sales tracking and card processing but minimal forecasting or inventory integration. Social media (Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok) is the primary marketing channel, managed manually between service windows. There is no widely adopted food-truck-specific ERP, route planner, or prep optimization tool — most operators use Google Sheets, Waze, and paper checklists.
