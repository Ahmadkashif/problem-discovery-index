# Municipal Services

## Profile
**Category:** Nonprofit & Government
**Market Size:** ~$200B US municipal operations (non-education, non-public safety); ~19,500 municipalities
**Tech Maturity:** Low-Medium — Tyler Technologies, CityWorks, and Accela handle permitting and asset management; 311 systems from SeeClickFix and PublicStuff exist but adoption is uneven; most infrastructure knowledge lives in the heads of senior public works staff, not in digital systems.
**Workforce:** Public works crews, code enforcement officers, city clerks, urban planners, utility operators, parks maintenance workers, street supervisors, fleet mechanics

## Key Pain Themes
Municipal operations run on institutional memory more than any private-sector equivalent — a retiring streets superintendent takes 30 years of pavement failure pattern recognition out the door. Capital improvement planning relies on windshield surveys and subjective scoring, producing maintenance backlogs that grow faster than budgets. Citizen-facing processes (311 requests, FOIA, permitting) are high-volume, low-automation workflows where a single clerk may handle 200+ requests per week across incompatible systems. Code enforcement is reactive rather than predictive, meaning violations accumulate until a complaint triggers inspection, by which point remediation costs have multiplied.

## Current Tech Landscape
Tyler Technologies (Munis, EnerGov) and CityWorks dominate asset management and permitting but are configured for compliance tracking, not predictive operations. 311 platforms like SeeClickFix provide intake but lack intelligent routing or priority scoring. GIS layers exist in most municipalities but are rarely connected to maintenance scheduling or failure prediction models — the data is there, the analysis is not.
